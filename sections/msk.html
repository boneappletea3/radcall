📋 Reporting Checklist — Acetabular Fractures
  • Elementary fracture type: anterior wall / anterior column / posterior wall / posterior column / transverse
  • Associated fracture pattern: T-type / anterior column + posterior hemitransverse / both column / transverse + posterior wall
  • Femoral head: congruent / subluxed / dislocated (direction)
  • Articular step-off: measure in mm (surgical threshold typically >2–3mm)
  • Intraarticular fragments: present / absent; size and location
  • Posterior wall deficiency: estimate % involvement (surgical if >40%)
  • Marginal impaction of articular surface: present / absent
  • Ipsilateral femoral neck fracture: present / absent

Tibial Plafond (Pilon) Fractures — Ruedi-Allgower Classification

📋 Reporting Checklist — Pilon
  • Number and location of major fragments at the articular surface
  • Degree/location of articular depression (>2-3mm significant)
  • Associated tendon injury or entrapment
  • Syndesmotic disruption, bony fragments in syndesmosis
  • Retinaculum injury
Pilon fracture: High-energy axial-load fracture of the distal tibial articular surface (plafond). Caused by axial compression (fall from height, MVA). Classified by Rüedi-Allgöwer (I: undisplaced; II: displaced without comminution; III: comminuted/impacted) or AO/OTA system. Staged treatment: initial spanning external fixator → delayed ORIF.
Shoulder

Relevant Anatomy

Glenohumeral joint: Ball-and-socket; glenoid covers only 25–30% of humeral head → stability depends on labrum, capsule, rotator cuff, and ligaments.
Rotator cuff (SITS): Supraspinatus (abduction, initiation) · Infraspinatus (ER) · Teres minor (ER) · Subscapularis (IR, anterior stabilizer).
Glenoid labrum: Fibrocartilaginous ring deepening glenoid; anterior-inferior = most common Bankart site. Normal superior labrum variants (Buford complex, sublabral foramen).
Key ligaments: IGHL (inferior glenohumeral ligament = primary anterior stabilizer with arm abducted/ER) · MGHL · SGHL · CC ligaments (trapezoid + conoid = AC joint stability) · CA arch.

📋 Reporting Checklist — Glenohumeral Dislocation
  • Direction: anterior / posterior / inferior (luxatio erecta)
  • Posterior: "light bulb" sign / vacant glenoid / trough line
  • Humeral head: Hill-Sachs defect (location, size); reverse Hill-Sachs "trough" (>25% involvement?)
  • Glenoid rim: Bankart fracture (osseous fragment size — % of glenoid width); reverse Bankart
  • Greater tuberosity fracture: present / displaced (>5 mm in young, >3 mm in athletes)
  • Lesser tuberosity: fracture (posterior dislocation marker)
  • Surgical neck: associated fracture (→ changes management)
  • Post-reduction: concentricity of joint; residual fragment interposition

Compartment Syndrome
Do not delay fasciotomy for imaging. Compartment syndrome is a clinical diagnosis (pain out of proportion, pain with passive stretch, paresthesias, paralysis, pallor, pulselessness — the "6 P's"). Compartment pressure >30 mmHg or within 30 mmHg of diastolic BP = fasciotomy. Promptly report any diffuse muscle swelling within a compartment and urge urgent clinical correlation. Irreversible muscle necrosis begins within 4–6 hours of ischemia.
Imaging role: Imaging is limited in acute compartment syndrome — it is a clinical diagnosis. MRI: diffuse T2/STIR hyperintensity of all muscles within involved compartment (edema/ischemia); loss of normal muscle architecture; absent enhancement in advanced ischemia/necrosis. Useful for: subacute/chronic exertional compartment syndrome (exercise-induced), post-fasciotomy assessment, evaluating extent of muscle necrosis. US: muscle swelling, loss of normal echogenicity (not routinely used for diagnosis).

Distal Humerus & Proximal Forearm

Distal Humerus Fractures — AO-ASIF Classification

Two-column concept: medial column → medial epicondyle; lateral column → lateral epicondyle/capitellum. Column disruption compromises structural stability. CT recommended after XR identification due to high rate of surgical injuries. Report: column involvement, epicondylar displacement direction/degree, articular involvement, comminution.
📋 Reporting Checklist — Distal Humerus
  • Column involvement — medial, lateral, or both
  • Direction and displacement of epicondylar avulsion / single column fractures
  • Articular involvement and intraarticular extension
  • Comminution or two-column injury
AO-ASIF TypeDescriptionManagement
A — ExtraarticularA1: epicondylar avulsion · A2: simple metaphyseal · A3: metaphyseal with comminutionMinimally displaced A1: conservative. Displaced avulsion, A2, A3: surgery
B — Partial articular (single column)B1: lateral condylar (sagittal) · B2: medial condylar (sagittal) · B3: frontal articular (coronal shear — capitellum/trochlea)Most require surgery. B1/B2 without comminution may initially trial conservative but usually need fixation
C — Complete articular (both columns)C1: simple articular + simple metaphyseal · C2: simple articular + metaphyseal comminution · C3: articular + metaphyseal comminutionSurgery in medically fit patients

Coronoid Process Fractures — O'Driscoll Classification

Coronoid provides anterior margin of ulnohumeral joint — resists varus stress and prevents posterior subluxation. Isolated coronoid fractures are uncommon; most occur with elbow dislocation or radial head fractures. Anteromedial facet involvement is key — it confers valgus stability and is the MCL attachment site. CT with 3D reformats recommended for all coronoid fractures.
TypeLocationSignificance
I — TipCoronoid tip (~1/3 of process)Conservative if stable; instability risk increases with larger fragments. Even tiny tip fractures herald additional occult injuries (LUCL, lateral capsule) in the terrible triad setting
II — Anteromedial facetAnteromedial facet ± sublime tubercle (MCL insertion)Nearly always requires surgery — varus and posteromedial rotatory instability. Subtypes by degree of medial involvement and sublime tubercle involvement
III — BaseBase of coronoid (>50% of process)Always surgical — major instability. Often seen with complex dislocations

Olecranon Fractures — Mayo Classification

Olecranon = posteroinferior margin of ulnohumeral joint, buttress against anterior dislocation, triceps insertion. Mechanism: axial load at 90° flexion or forceful hyperextension. Report: displacement (gap >2 mm = surgical), comminution (= plate fixation vs tension band), coronoid process involvement (= medial plate fixation for instability).
📋 Reporting Checklist — Olecranon
  • Degree of displacement (>2 mm or increases with flexion/extension = surgery)
  • Comminution (= plate fixation rather than tension band wiring)
  • Coronoid process involvement (= medial plate fixation to prevent instability)
  • Fracture pattern: transverse (tension band), oblique (screw), comminuted (plate)
Type 1 non-displaced (<2 mm gap, stable during flexion) · Type 2 displaced >2 mm, stable elbow · Type 3 displaced + elbow instability
Elbow
Primary stabilizers: Ulnohumeral articulation · anterior bundle of MCL (valgus stability) · LUCL (prevents PLRI).
Secondary stabilizers: Radiocapitellar articulation · common flexor-pronator tendon · common extensor tendon · joint capsule.
Key concept: Injury to primary stabilizers → elbow instability. Any fracture involving a primary stabilizer attachment site should prompt a search for associated ligament and soft-tissue injuries.

Radial Head Fractures — Mason-Johnston Classification

Most common elbow fracture in adults (33–50% of all elbow fractures). FOOSH mechanism with forearm pronation and elbow extension/flexion 0–80°. Subtle fractures indicated by fat pad elevation (anterior and posterior).
📋 Reporting Checklist — Radial Head
  • Degree of displacement (mm)
  • Articular surface involvement (%)
  • Comminution
  • Associated dislocation (Mason-Johnston type IV)
  • Search for associated injuries: coronoid fracture, MCL/LUCL tear, Essex-Lopresti (check DRUJ on wrist XR)
TypeDescriptionManagement
INo or minimal (<2 mm) displacementConservative (sling, early ROM)
IIDisplaced ≥2 mm, <30% articular surfaceConservative if preserved ROM; surgery if poor ROM or >30% articular surface
IIIComminuted, no dislocationSurgery (ORIF or radial head replacement)
IVAny radial head fracture + elbow dislocationSurgery
Essex-Lopresti: Comminuted radial head fracture + DRUJ dislocation + interosseous membrane disruption → "floating radius." Nearly always requires surgery. Suspect when wrist pain accompanies a radial head fracture; check DRUJ on wrist XR (radioulnar distance >5 mm vs. contralateral = diagnostic).

Elbow Dislocation

Second most common joint dislocation in adults (after shoulder). Most commonly posterior. Classified as simple (no fracture) or complex (associated fracture). FOOSH mechanism with axial compression, supination, and valgus stress.
StageInjury PatternInstability
ILUCL + RCL damagePosterolateral rotatory instability (PLRI)
IIStage I + capsule + lateral soft tissuesTrochlea perched on coronoid (near-complete dislocation)
IIIStage II + anterior MCL bundleFrank posterior dislocation with varus + valgus instability
Drop sign: Ulnohumeral distance ≥4 mm on postreduction lateral XR — may predict PLRI development. Associated injuries to search for: radial head fracture (axial compression on capitellum), coronoid fracture (shearing), medial epicondyle fracture, flexor-pronator and brachialis muscle injuries, MCL/LUCL tears.
Terrible Triad: Posterior elbow dislocation + radial head fracture + coronoid fracture → extensive ligament damage → chronic instability if inadequately treated. Early surgical repair critical. Get CT if 2 of 3 identified — even a tiny coronoid tip fracture is significant in this setting. Evaluate anteromedial facet on CT with 3D reformats.

Monteggia Fracture-Dislocation — Bado Classification

Ulnar fracture + radiocapitellar dislocation. All require early surgical repair — delayed recognition → chronic instability. Suspect when ulnar fracture is identified; radiocapitellar dislocation can be missed if spontaneously reduced before XR. CT recommended to detect additional fractures.

Distal phalanx tuft fractures: Most common hand fracture. Usually stable (fibrous septa prevent displacement). Report if displaced — implies nailbed injury (open fracture requiring nail plate removal + nailbed repair). Base fractures are unstable (flexor/extensor tendon insertions) and require surgery. Fracture line through sclerotic margin. Other causes: aneurysmal bone cyst, giant cell tumor, epidermoid inclusion cyst. Chondrosarcoma rare but suspect if extensive endosteal scalloping, cortical breakthrough, or soft-tissue component.

📋 Reporting Checklist — Pelvic Fractures
  • Classification: AO/OTA Type A (stable) / B (partially unstable) / C (unstable); Young-Burgess: APC / LC / VS / CM — specify grade
  • Pubic symphysis: alignment and widening — normal <6 mm men, <10 mm women
  • Pubic rami: superior and inferior rami; obturator ring — step defect; ipsilateral / contralateral / bilateral
  • Iliac bone: fracture lines with or without extension into SI joint (crescent fracture)
  • SI joint: symmetry and widening — normal 2–4 mm, wider posteriorly than anteriorly; dislocation
  • Sacrum: fracture identification — Denis zone I (lateral) / II (foraminal) / III (central); transverse component; bilateral (H-type / U-type); comminution
  • Avulsion fractures: L5 transverse process · inferolateral sacrum · ischial spine · ischial tuberosity · rectus abdominis at pubic crest
  • Vasculature: active arterial extravasation (blush) → recommend IR; hematoma — pelvic wall / presacral / rectus sheath
  • Pelvic organs: bladder and/or urethral injury; Foley catheter position
  • Acetabulum: articular involvement — step-off, intraarticular fragments

📋 Reporting Checklist — Hip Dislocation

  • Posterior: femoral head displaced superolaterally; lesser trochanter NOT visible on AP

  • Contralateral hip imaging if stress fracture suspected

Describes angle of fracture line relative to horizontal. Higher angle = greater shear force = increased instability and AVN risk. More predictive in younger adults.

Subtrochanteric Fractures

Isolated lesser trochanter avulsion in adults → malignancy until proven otherwise.
Subtrochanteric zone = lesser trochanter to 5 cm below. High-stress region: lateral cortex under tension, medial cortex under compression → requires cephalomedullary nail (not sliding hip screw).

📋 Reporting Checklist — Proximal Humerus
  • Displacement: measure in mm or cm — threshold ≥1 cm per Neer
  • Angulation: measure in degrees — threshold ≥45° (note: some surgeons use ≥30° for varus/valgus)
  • Articular split: present? Simple vs comminuted; fragments detached from both tuberosities and/or dislocated into joint?
  • Anatomic neck fracture OR fractures of both GT and LT — high AVN risk; if anatomic neck fractured: measure medial metaphyseal segment (length <8 mm and displacement >2 mm = higher AVN risk)
  • Segmental surgical neck fracture: shaft segment isolated between two fracture lines
  • Greater tuberosity: displacement direction (superior = supraspinatus; posterior = infraspinatus/teres minor); >5 mm → surgical in young/active
  • Lesser tuberosity: displacement → subscapularis avulsion; associated posterior dislocation?
  • Glenohumeral dislocation: direction (anterior/posterior); location of articular fragments; Hill-Sachs / reverse Hill-Sachs; glenoid rim fracture
  • Neer classification: 1-part / 2-part (specify segment) / 3-part / 4-part / 4-part valgus-impacted / head-splitting
  • Acromion, coracoid, AC joint

Periprosthetic Femoral Fractures
Orthopedic management depends on three radiographic criteria — report all three explicitly:
(a) Fracture location — trochanteric, along femoral stem, or well distal to stem
(b) Prosthesis stability — stable vs. loose (present in ~70% of periprosthetic fractures)
(c) Bone stock quality — good vs. poor (determines revision hardware options)
Medial buttress: The medial cortex along the proximal femoral stem is a key landmark. Fractures that disrupt the medial buttress may compromise femoral stem stability regardless of where the fracture line originates — note any medial cortex involvement explicitly.

Normal findings — don't overcall loosening:

📋 Reporting Checklist — Periprosthetic Femoral Fracture
  • General fracture description: displacement, comminution, angulation, alignment
  • Fracture location: greater trochanter / lesser trochanter / along or just distal to femoral stem / well distal to stem (≥4–5 cm from tip)
  • Implant stability — signs of loosening:
    • Fracture extension to or along fixation surface (textured ingrowth or cement mantle)
    • Periprosthetic osteolysis / lucency (>2 mm; progressive; divergent)
    • Subsidence, tilting, or rotation vs. prior imaging
    • Prosthesis or cement fracture
  • Bone stock: presence and severity of bone loss (cortical thinning, medullary widening, cancellous loss, osteopenia, comminution)
  • Bone loss location: trochanteric / proximal stem / entire stem / extensive with distortion
  • Imaging recommendation: CT if radiographs equivocal and patient cannot bear weight, or for surgical planning (note: metallic streak artifact may limit CT)

Necrotizing Fasciitis
SURGICAL EMERGENCY: Gas tracking along fascial planes is pathognomonic on CT. Do not delay surgical debridement for additional imaging if clinical suspicion is high. CT findings: gas in fascia (linear/bubbly hypodensities along fascial planes), asymmetric fascial thickening, fluid tracking along fascial planes, absent or diminished fascial enhancement, skin thickening. Report any gas in soft tissue planes with urgency. Mortality 20–40% — increases significantly with operative delay beyond 12–24 hours.
Imaging limitations: CT sensitivity 88–90%, specificity ~93%. Negative CT does NOT exclude necrotizing fasciitis. If clinical suspicion is high (pain out of proportion, rapid progression, skin changes, toxicity, crepitus), operate regardless of CT findings. LRINEC score (Laboratory Risk Indicator for Necrotizing Fasciitis) can aid but does not replace clinical judgment.

Osteomyelitis & Septic Arthritis
MRI — gold standard: T1: marrow replacement (low signal replacing normal bright fatty marrow — most sensitive finding); STIR/T2: hyperintense edema in marrow and surrounding soft tissues; post-contrast: abnormal marrow enhancement, periosteal enhancement, soft tissue/joint enhancement. Periosteal reaction on radiograph delayed 7–10 days after onset — normal XR does not exclude osteomyelitis. Three-phase bone scan useful when MRI contraindicated.
Septic arthritis — urgent: Joint space widening + periarticular edema + joint effusion on MRI. Cartilage destruction rapid (within 24–48h of untreated infection). Requires urgent joint aspiration (synovial fluid WBC >50,000, predominant PMNs) and surgical washout. Do not delay aspiration for MRI if clinical suspicion high. Common organisms: S. aureus (all ages), N. gonorrhoeae (sexually active adults), Group B Strep (neonates).
