Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH)
Graf alpha angle (ultrasound): >60° = normal · 50–60° = mildly dysplastic · <50° = dysplastic. Assess at 6 weeks (before ossification of femoral head). X-ray (after 4–6 months): Hilgenreiner's/Perkin's lines; Shenton's line continuity.
Hypertrophic Pyloric Stenosis (HPS)
Clinical context: Presents 2–8 weeks of age (peak 3–5 weeks); firstborn males more common; projectile non-bilious vomiting; palpable "olive" in epigastrium ~70%. US is diagnostic study of choice (sensitivity ~97–99%, specificity ~100%).
US CriterionNormalHPS (Positive)
Pyloric muscle thickness (single wall)<3mm≥4mm
Pyloric channel length<14mm≥17mm
Pyloric transverse diameter<13mm≥13mm
Technique and pitfalls: Measure on a RELAXED pylorus — do not measure during active contraction (falsely elevated). Real-time: absent gastric emptying through pylorus despite active antral peristalsis. Electrolyte consequence: hypochloremic, hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis — correct BEFORE surgical pyloromyotomy (Ramstedt procedure).

Intussusception
Most common bowel obstruction in children age 3 months–5 years. Ileocolic type ~95%. Classic triad: colicky pain + currant jelly stool + palpable mass — only ~50% present with all three. Treat with pneumatic or hydrostatic enema reduction under fluoroscopy or US guidance.
US findings: "Target sign" (donut sign) — concentric rings of bowel-within-bowel on transverse view; "pseudokidney sign" — elongated kidney-shaped mass on longitudinal view. US is primary diagnostic modality (sensitivity ~98%, specificity ~88%).
FeatureDescription
Target/donut sign (transverse US)Hypoechoic outer rim (intussuscipiens) surrounding echogenic center (intussusceptum with mesenteric fat); multiple concentric rings
Pseudokidney sign (longitudinal US)Elongated ovoid mass resembling a kidney; hyperechoic mesenteric fat surrounded by hypoechoic bowel wall layers; most common in RUQ/epigastrium
Lead pointIdentified in ~5% of cases; Meckel diverticulum, polyp, duplication cyst, lymphoma; suspect if age >5 years, recurrent, or identifiable mass
Free fluidSimple anechoic fluid does NOT preclude reduction; complex/echogenic free fluid → concern for perforation/ischemia
Trapped lymph nodesMesenteric nodes within intussusceptum; reactive post-viral lymphadenopathy is most common identifiable "lead point"
Contraindications to enema reduction: Pneumoperitoneum · Peritonitis · Hemodynamic instability. Success rate: ~80–90% pneumatic reduction. Recurrence: ~10%. Surgical consult for failed reduction, suspected lead point, or perforation.

Malrotation
Upper GI signs of malrotation: DJJ (duodenojejunal junction) NOT to the left of midline and NOT at level of duodenal bulb. Corkscrew duodenum = volvulus. Reverse SMA/SMV orientation on US (SMV left of SMA).

Non-Accidental Trauma (NAT)
High specificity injuries for NAT: Classic metaphyseal lesions (CML/bucket-handle fractures) · Posterior rib fractures · Subdural hematomas (especially interhemispheric, mixed-density) · Retinal hemorrhages · Bruising in non-mobile infant

📋 Reporting Checklist — Non-Accidental Trauma
  • All fractures: list each by location, type, and estimated age (acute / healing / chronic)
  • High-specificity fractures: posterior rib fractures / classic metaphyseal lesions (CMLs) / interhemispheric subdural / scapular body
  • Moderate-high specificity: spinous process / sternal fractures
  • Dating discrepancy: multiple fractures of differing ages — yes / no
  • Intracranial findings: subdural (bilateral / posterior interhemispheric) / SAH / cortical contusions / DAI
  • Retinal hemorrhage: ophthalmology exam recommended — not assessable on CT
  • Visceral injuries without adequate trauma mechanism
  • Clinical history adequacy: mechanism adequate / inadequate / inconsistent with findings
  • Mandatory reporting obligation: note per institutional protocol
