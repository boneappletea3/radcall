Chest X-Ray (PA) XR
CXR — Chest X-Ray (PA)
  • 1
    History & Indication
    • Clinical context: acute vs. routine; symptoms (SOB, cough, chest pain, fever)
    • Prior CXRs or CT for comparison
  • 2
    Scout / Image Quality
    • Rotation: clavicles equidistant from spinous process
    • Inspiration: ≥6 anterior ribs above diaphragm
    • Exposure: vertebrae visible through cardiac shadow; vascular markings in lungs
  • 3
    Airways
    • Tracheal position and caliber
    • Carina angle (normal <70°)
    • Visible bronchi for central mass or mucous plug
  • 4
    Cardiac Silhouette
    • Cardiothoracic ratio ≤0.5 on PA
    • Cardiac borders: right (SVC, RA), left (aortic knuckle, PA, LAA, LV)
    • Mediastinal width — superior (aortic knuckle, trachea deviation)
  • 5
    Hila
    • Hilar size, density, and position (left normally slightly higher)
    • Hilar fullness: adenopathy vs. vascular prominence
  • 6
    Lung Zones
    • Upper zones: vascular caliber, apical disease
    • Mid zones: perihilar masses, consolidation
    • Lower zones: consolidation, effusion, atelectasis
    • Interstitial pattern: Kerley B lines, reticular, nodular
    • Ground-glass opacity or consolidation distribution
  • 7
    Pleural Spaces
    • Costophrenic angles: blunting (>200–300 mL)
    • Fissures: major (oblique) and minor (horizontal) — for lobar collapse localization
    • Pneumothorax: visceral pleural line, absent lung markings
  • 8
    Diaphragm & Below
    • Right hemidiaphragm slightly higher (1.5–2.5 cm)
    • Subphrenic free air
    • Gastric bubble, bowel loops
  • 9
    Bones & Soft Tissues
    • Ribs: fractures (acute vs. healing), lytic/sclerotic lesions
    • Clavicles, scapulae, humeral heads
    • Soft tissues: subcutaneous emphysema, breast shadows, calcifications

Chest X-Ray (Lateral) XR
CXR Lateral — Systematic Review
  • 1
    History & Indication
    • Correlate with PA: localize abnormalities to anterior vs. posterior, upper vs. lower
    • Prior lateral CXR for comparison
  • 2
    Image Quality
    • True lateral: posterior ribs overlap
    • Rotation check: rib posterior arcs should align
  • 3
    Retrosternal Space
    • Clear space anterior to heart (Raider triangle)
    • Anterior mediastinal mass (4 Ts): thyroid, thymoma, teratoma, terrible lymphoma
  • 4
    Cardiac Borders
    • Anterior border: RV
    • Posterior border: LA (upper), LV (lower)
    • IVC where it enters RA
  • 5
    Retrocardiac Space
    • Posterior to heart — should be clear
    • Consolidation/mass in lower lobes
  • 6
    Trachea & Posterior Tracheal Band
    • Trachea: anterior wall, posterior tracheal stripe
    • Hilar overlay sign
  • 7
    Fissures
    • Major (oblique) fissure: runs from T4–5 posterior to anterior diaphragm
    • Minor fissure: horizontal, anterior
  • 8
    Vertebrae
    • Progressive lucency from upper to lower thoracic spine (normally gets darker)
    • Loss of disc space height, vertebral body compression
    • Dense vertebral body = mass or fracture
  • 9
    Diaphragm & Hemidiaphragms
    • Right: anterior (liver) to posterior; left: anterior (stomach bubble) — lower
    • Posterior costophrenic sulcus: effusion blunting
  • 10
    Retrotracheal Triangle & Posterior Mediastinum
    • Posterior mediastinal masses: neurogenic, paraspinal abscess
  • 11
    Bones & Soft Tissues
    • Sternum: fracture (look for angulation)
    • Thoracic spine: kyphosis, fractures
    • Scapulae, shoulder joints
Portable / ICU CXR XR
Technique limitations: AP projection (heart appears larger), supine (effusions layer posteriorly, PTX may be anterior), portable equipment (lower kVp, more scatter). Always note technique.
ICU CXR — Systematic Review
  • 1
    History & Indication
    • Current clinical status: post-procedure (line/tube placement) vs. interval change (new respiratory failure, desaturation)
    • Compare with most recent prior portable CXR
  • 2
    Image Quality
    • AP vs. PA (note magnification)
    • Rotation, inspiration, exposure
    • Supine vs. upright position noted
  • 3
    Lines & Tubes — Check Every One
    • ETT: tip 3–7 cm above carina, at level of aortic knuckle (~T4–6); not in right mainstem
    • NGT/OGT: tip below left hemidiaphragm; side port below GEJ
    • CVL (IJ/subclavian): tip in SVC/cavoatrial junction; no pneumothorax
    • PICC: tip in SVC or cavoatrial junction
    • Chest tube: tip position, lung re-expansion
    • PA catheter: tip in right or left PA, no more than 5 cm from midline
    • IABP: tip at level of aortic knuckle (T4–6), 1–2 cm below left subclavian
    • Pacemaker leads: RA appendage, RV apex
  • 4
    Interval Pneumothorax
    • Supine PTX: deep sulcus sign, hyperlucent hemithorax (anteromedial or subpulmonic PTX)
    • Post-procedure (line placement, thoracentesis, barotrauma)
  • 5
    Airspace Disease
    • New consolidation vs. prior
    • Atelectasis vs. true pneumonia (typically more rapid change)
    • Pulmonary edema: vascular redistribution, Kerley B lines, bilateral confluent opacities
  • 6
    Pleural Effusion (Supine)
    • Homogeneous opacity over entire hemithorax or layering along dependent pleura
    • Apical capping
    • Obscuration of hemidiaphragm
  • 7
    Cardiac Silhouette
    • Interval widening: pericardial effusion, mediastinal hematoma
    • Pneumomediastinum: lucency outlining mediastinal structures
  • 8
    Miscellaneous
    • Subcutaneous emphysema
    • Rib fractures (trauma patients)
    • Osseous changes
⚠ Check every line and tube position before evaluating for new pathology.
CT Chest CT
Use lung windows (W:1500/L:-600) for parenchyma, mediastinal windows (W:400/L:40) for soft tissues, bone windows for ribs/spine.
CT Chest — Systematic Review
  • 1
    Triage / Gestalt
    • Scroll through entire study at low magnification first
    • Identify obvious dominant abnormality (consolidation, mass, effusion, pneumothorax)
    • Note contrast phase: non-contrast, arterial, venous, or delayed
  • 2
    Scout Image
    • Cardiac size, mediastinum, bony thorax
    • Vertebral alignment on scout lateral if available
  • 3
    History & Clinical Context
    • Indication: staging, infection, ILD, trauma, post-op?
    • Prior CT or CXR for comparison
  • 4
    Adequacy
    • Coverage: lung apices through adrenals/upper abdomen
    • Contrast timing if contrast-enhanced
    • Motion/artifact
  • 5
    Support Devices
    • Lines and tubes (if ICU patient)
    • Pacemaker/defibrillator leads position
  • 6
    Airways
    • Trachea: caliber, deviation, intraluminal lesion
    • Main, lobar, segmental bronchi: obstruction, mucous plugging
    • Bronchiectasis (traction or cylindrical)
  • 7
    Mediastinum (Mediastinal Windows)
    • Superior: vascular structures, trachea, lymph nodes
    • Middle: heart chambers, pericardium, aortic root, PA
    • Posterior: esophagus, descending aorta, paraspinal
  • 8
    Hila
    • Hilar lymph nodes: size >1 cm short axis
    • Hilar masses: primary lung vs. nodal
  • 9
    Pleural Spaces
    • Effusion: free vs. loculated, unilateral vs. bilateral
    • Pleural thickening, calcification, masses
    • Pneumothorax: size estimation
  • 10
    Lung Parenchyma (Lung Windows)
    • Nodules: size, density (solid/subsolid/GGO), morphology, distribution
    • Consolidation: distribution, air bronchograms, density
    • Ground-glass opacity: extent, distribution
    • Interstitial pattern: septal thickening, reticulation, honeycombing, distribution (UIP vs. NSIP)
    • Emphysema: centrilobular/panlobular/paraseptal
    • Mosaic attenuation: air trapping vs. perfusion vs. infiltrative
    • Lymphadenopathy: mediastinal and hilar nodes
  • 11
    Extrathoracic Structures
    • Chest wall and ribs: fractures, lytic/blastic lesions
    • Thoracic spine: vertebral body height, alignment, marrow density
    • Thyroid (if visualized): nodules, goiter extension
    • Upper abdomen: liver dome, adrenals, spleen, stomach
    • Axillae: lymphadenopathy
    • Breast tissue (incidental)
  • 12
    Last Checks
    • Bone windows for rib and vertebral fractures (don't miss)
    • Review coronal/sagittal reconstructions
    • Compare with prior imaging
    • Measure any significant findings (nodule diameter, lymph node SAX)
CT Pulmonary Angiography (CT-PA) CT
Adequate opacification: PA attenuation ≥200 HU. Check for filling defects centrally before peripherally (saddle → main → lobar → segmental → subsegmental).
CT-PA — Systematic Review
  • 1
    History & Clinical Context
    • Pre-test probability (Wells score, PERC)
    • Prior CT-PA or V/Q for comparison
    • Anticoagulation status
  • 2
    Adequacy / Technical Quality
    • PA opacification ≥200 HU (check pulmonary trunk)
    • Respiratory motion — streak artifact
    • Coverage from above aortic arch through bilateral costophrenic angles
    • Bolus timing: arterial phase adequate
  • 3
    Pulmonary Arteries — Central
    • Saddle embolus: filling defect straddling bifurcation
    • Main PA: bilateral filling defects
    • Right PA and left PA trunk
  • 4
    Pulmonary Arteries — Lobar
    • RUL, RML, RLL lobar arteries
    • LUL, lingula, LLL lobar arteries
  • 5
    Pulmonary Arteries — Segmental & Subsegmental
    • All segmental branches bilaterally
    • Subsegmental emboli: report presence; note clinical significance in isolation
  • 6
    Signs of Right Heart Strain
    • RV:LV diameter ratio >0.9 (or >1.0) on axial
    • Interventricular septal flattening or bowing toward LV (D-sign)
    • Contrast reflux into IVC/hepatic veins
  • 7
    Lung Parenchyma
    • Hampton's hump: pleural-based wedge consolidation (infarction)
    • Westermark sign: oligemia distal to embolus
    • Atelectasis, ground-glass opacity adjacent to infarct
    • Concurrent pneumonia, consolidation
  • 8
    Pleural Spaces
    • Effusion (exudative if infarction)
    • Hemothorax (trauma setting)
  • 9
    Mediastinum & Heart
    • Mediastinal widening, aortic caliber
    • Pericardial effusion
    • Cardiac chambers
  • 10
    Pulmonary Hypertension Signs
    • PA diameter >29 mm
    • PA:aorta diameter ratio >1
    • RV enlargement, tricuspid regurgitation signs
  • 11
    Extrathoracic Incidentals
    • Axillae, chest wall, bones
    • Upper abdomen (liver, adrenals, kidneys)
    • Thyroid
  • 12
    Last Checks
    • Coronal and sagittal reconstructions
    • Deep vein thrombosis if legs included
    • Chronic vs. acute PE features: eccentric/calcified/web-like vs. central/acute filling defect
⚠ Isolated subsegmental PE: correlation with clinical probability required before treatment decision.
MRI Chest MRI
Sequences vary: cardiac MRI uses ECG-gated cine GRE/SSFP; mediastinal/pleural MRI uses T1, T2, STIR, T1+C. Note indication-specific protocol.
MRI Chest — Sequence-Based Review
  • 1
    History & Indication
    • Cardiac MRI (function, ischemia, cardiomyopathy, valvular disease) vs. mediastinal/pleural mass vs. chest wall
    • Prior echo, CT, or cardiac MRI for comparison
    • Gadolinium given? Note timing and dose
  • 2
    Adequacy
    • Cardiac gating quality (for cardiac MRI)
    • Sequences present: T1, T2, STIR, SSFP/cine, phase-contrast, LGE, T1+C
    • Respiratory motion, artifact level
    • Coverage: cardiac MRI (heart + great vessels) vs. mediastinal (lung apices to diaphragm)
  • 3
    T2 / STIR Sequences
    • Cardiac: myocardial edema (T2-bright = myocarditis, acute ischemia)
    • Mediastinal masses: T2 characterization — cystic (very bright), solid, mixed
    • Pericardium: T2-bright pericardial effusion vs. thickening
    • Pleural disease: T2-bright effusion vs. solid tumor (mesothelioma)
    • Chest wall: muscle edema, rib marrow signal
  • 4
    Cine / SSFP Sequences (Cardiac MRI)
    • Biventricular function: LV EF, RV EF (normal LV EF ≥55%)
    • Wall motion: regional RWMA (segment, territory)
    • LV wall thickness: hypertrophy distribution (concentric vs. asymmetric)
    • Valves: regurgitant/stenotic jets, morphology
    • Pericardial motion: constriction signs (septal bounce, respirophasic variation)
  • 5
    T1 Pre-Contrast
    • Fat content: T1-bright = fat (lipoma, dermoid, thymolipoma), hemorrhage, proteinaceous fluid
    • Mediastinal mass characterization: anterior (4Ts), middle (vascular), posterior (neurogenic)
    • Cardiac masses: T1 vs. T2 signal to differentiate thrombus vs. tumor
  • 6
    T1 Post-Contrast / Late Gadolinium Enhancement (LGE)
    • LGE pattern for cardiomyopathy: subendocardial (ischemic) vs. mid-wall/epicardial (non-ischemic)
    • Pericardial enhancement: pericarditis, constrictive pattern
    • Mediastinal/pleural mass enhancement: solid enhancement = malignancy or thymoma
    • Perineural spread if neck/mediastinal mass
    • Myocarditis: focal mid-wall LGE typical in lateral wall
  • 7
    Phase-Contrast / Flow Sequences (if performed)
    • Quantify valvular regurgitant fractions
    • Aortic/pulmonary flow assessment
    • Congenital shunt quantification: Qp:Qs
  • 8
    Vascular Structures
    • Aorta: caliber, MRA if included (aneurysm, dissection flap)
    • Pulmonary arteries: thrombus, compression by mass
    • SVC/IVC: patency, thrombus, extrinsic compression
  • 9
    Extrathoracic Structures
    • Liver dome, spleen, adrenals (upper abdomen)
    • Chest wall and ribs: bone marrow signal, cortical destruction
    • Axillary soft tissues and lymph nodes
    • Thyroid and lower neck
  • 10
    Last Checks
    • Coronal and sagittal reformats
    • Compare with prior echo or CT
    • Report EF, volumes, mass dimensions if applicable
Abdominal X-Ray (AXR) XR
AXR — Abdominal X-Ray
  • 1
    History & Indication
    • Obstruction? Perforation? Constipation? Post-procedure?
    • Prior AXR or CT for comparison
  • 2
    Image Quality
    • Coverage: from diaphragm to pubic symphysis
    • Exposure and positioning
  • 3
    Bowel Gas Pattern
    • Small bowel: central position, valvulae conniventes (stack of coins), normal <3 cm
    • Large bowel: peripheral/haustra, normal <6 cm (cecum <9 cm)
    • Obstruction: dilated loops proximal to transition; step-ladder pattern (SBO)
    • Ileus: diffuse non-obstructive dilation
    • Pneumatosis intestinalis: intramural air
  • 4
    Free Air
    • Rigler sign: both sides of bowel wall visible
    • Football sign: free air outlining falciform ligament
    • Subdiaphragmatic air on upright/decubitus (if available)
  • 5
    Solid Organs
    • Liver size (right flank stripe)
    • Spleen size (left flank stripe)
    • Kidneys: renal outlines; psoas shadow obliteration
  • 6
    Calcifications
    • Renal calculi: opaque, overlying collecting system
    • Gallstones: 10–15% opaque, RUQ
    • Pancreatic: chronic pancreatitis
    • Phleboliths vs. ureteral stones (phleboliths have lucent center)
    • Vascular: aortic/iliac calcification, AAA
  • 7
    Masses / Soft Tissue
    • Pelvic mass displacing bowel loops
    • Retroperitoneal mass: lateral displacement of ascending/descending colon
  • 8
    Bones
    • Lumbar spine: alignment, disc spaces, pedicle integrity
    • Pelvic bones: iliac wings, SI joints, pubic symphysis
    • Hip joints: femoral heads
  • 9
    Extraabdominal
    • Lung bases: pleural effusion, consolidation
    • Lower rib fractures
    • Subcutaneous tissues
Fluoroscopic Esophagram Fluoro
Single-contrast (water-soluble if perforation suspected) vs. double-contrast (barium + effervescent). Obtain multiple projections. Lateral neck for pharynx/Zenker. RAO for esophagus.
Esophagram — Fluoroscopic Review
  • 1
    History & Indication
    • Dysphagia (oropharyngeal vs. esophageal), odynophagia, GERD, suspected perforation, post-op assessment
    • Prior endoscopy or barium results
  • 2
    Swallow Mechanics & Pharynx
    • Oral phase: bolus formation, premature spillage
    • Pharyngeal phase: velopharyngeal closure, laryngeal elevation, epiglottic retroversion, cricopharyngeal relaxation
    • Aspiration: barium in larynx/trachea; penetration vs. aspiration
    • Pharyngeal residue: valleculae, pyriform sinuses
    • Pharyngeal masses or mucosal irregularity
  • 3
    Cervical Esophagus
    • Cricopharyngeal bar/impression
    • Zenker diverticulum: posterior midline outpouching above cricopharyngeus
    • Webs: thin anterior defects
  • 4
    Thoracic Esophagus
    • Caliber: narrowing (stricture, extrinsic compression, ring)
    • Mucosal pattern: smooth vs. irregular (carcinoma), fold thickening
    • Peristalsis: primary, secondary, tertiary contractions
    • Extrinsic impressions: aorta, left atrium, aberrant subclavian
  • 5
    Esophageal Motility
    • Primary peristalsis: complete stripping wave
    • Achalasia: dilated esophagus, bird-beak tapering at GEJ, aperistalsis
    • Diffuse esophageal spasm: corkscrew, tertiary contractions
    • Scleroderma: aperistalsis lower 2/3, dilated, patulous GEJ
  • 6
    Gastroesophageal Junction
    • Schatzki ring: mucosal ring at B-ring (squamocolumnar junction), symptomatic if <13 mm
    • Hiatal hernia: gastric mucosa above GEJ above diaphragm
    • Reflux: barium reflux above GEJ (note: seen in normals; clinical correlation needed)
  • 7
    Filling Defects & Strictures
    • Carcinoma: irregular mucosal destruction, shouldering, apple-core lesion
    • Benign stricture: smooth, tapered
    • Leiomyoma: smooth filling defect, intact overlying mucosa
    • Varices: tortuous longitudinal defects; worm-like
  • 8
    Last Checks
    • Full column and mucosal relief views
    • Lateral fluoroscopic views of pharynx for posterior lesions
    • Delayed images if achalasia suspected
Fluoroscopic Upper GI Series Fluoro
Standard double-contrast (barium + gas). Include esophagus (single-contrast), stomach, duodenum. Prone and supine positions. Dedicated esophagram may be separate.
Upper GI — Fluoroscopic Review
  • 1
    History & Indication
    • Epigastric pain, nausea/vomiting, suspected leak (post-op — use water-soluble), PUD, Crohn's, malignancy workup
    • Prior endoscopy or upper GI results
  • 2
    Esophagus
    • Quick assessment: caliber, peristalsis, GEJ, hiatal hernia
    • If dedicated esophagram needed, order separately
  • 3
    Stomach — Fundus & Cardia
    • Position: fundus under left hemidiaphragm
    • Fundal masses, linitis plastica pattern
  • 4
    Stomach — Body & Antrum
    • Rugal folds: thickened (Ménétrier, Zollinger-Ellison), effaced (carcinoma, linitis plastica)
    • Gastric ulcer: lesser curvature (benign — Hampton's line, ulcer collar) vs. greater curvature (malignant features: Carman meniscus sign)
    • Gastric outlet obstruction: antral narrowing, delayed emptying
  • 5
    Duodenum — Bulb
    • Bulb: triangular shape; ulcer niche — base, apex, posterior wall
    • Deformity: chronic scarring from PUD
    • Filling defects: polyp, Brunner gland hyperplasia
  • 6
    Duodenum — Second through Fourth Portions
    • C-loop: widening (pancreatic mass, annular pancreas)
    • Mucosal pattern: thickened folds (Zollinger-Ellison, Crohn's, celiac, lymphoma)
    • Duodenal masses, web, malrotation
  • 7
    Small Bowel (if included)
    • Caliber: normal jejunum <3 cm, ileum <2.5 cm
    • Fold pattern: jejunum (plicae circulares close together) vs. ileum (fewer folds)
    • String sign of Crohn's
    • Entero-enteric fistula, obstruction
CT Chest / Abdomen / Pelvis (CAP) CT
Most often venous phase (70–80 s). Lung windows for chest; soft tissue and bone windows throughout. Oral contrast may be given. Check all three body cavities systematically.
CT CAP — Systematic Review
  • 1
    History & Indication
    • Oncologic staging/surveillance vs. infection/inflammation vs. trauma
    • Prior CT or PET-CT for comparison
    • Contrast phase: arterial, venous, delayed, or portal
  • 2
    Image Quality
    • Coverage: lung apices to pubic symphysis
    • Contrast timing adequate
    • Motion artifact
  • 3
    Chest (Lung Windows)
    • Nodules/masses: size, density, location
    • Pleural effusions, pneumothorax
    • Lymphadenopathy: mediastinal, hilar
    • Airways: obstruction
    • Interstitial changes
  • 4
    Chest (Mediastinal Windows)
    • Heart and pericardium
    • Great vessels: aorta caliber, PE if arterial phase
    • Esophagus: wall thickening
    • Chest wall, ribs
  • 5
    Liver
    • Lesion detection: hypodense (venous), hypervascular (arterial)
    • Morphology: cirrhosis (nodular surface, caudate hypertrophy, lobar atrophy)
    • Biliary: bile duct dilation, stones, air
  • 6
    Gallbladder & Bile Ducts
    • Gallbladder: wall thickening, stones, pericholecystic fluid
    • Common bile duct: diameter >6 mm (>10 mm post-cholecystectomy)
  • 7
    Pancreas
    • Enhancement pattern: hypo- vs. hypervascular lesion
    • Pancreatic duct dilation (>3 mm)
    • Peripancreatic fat stranding
    • Cystic lesions: IPMN, serous/mucinous cystadenoma
  • 8
    Spleen
    • Size (>13 cm = splenomegaly)
    • Focal lesions: hemangioma, metastasis, lymphoma
    • Splenic vein patency
  • 9
    Adrenal Glands
    • Size: >10 mm limb width = adenoma vs. metastasis
    • Attenuation: <10 HU = adenoma (lipid-rich)
    • Bilateral enlargement: metastases, lymphoma, CAH
  • 10
    Kidneys & Ureters
    • Renal mass: Bosniak classification for cysts, solid enhancement
    • Hydronephrosis: level of obstruction
    • Stones: hyperdense in collecting system
  • 11
    Bowel
    • Small bowel: wall thickness, mesenteric fat stranding, obstruction
    • Colon: wall thickening, diverticulitis, mass
    • Appendix: diameter >6 mm, periappendiceal fat stranding
    • Free fluid / free air
  • 12
    Pelvis
    • Bladder: wall thickening, mass, stones
    • Uterus/ovaries or prostate/seminal vesicles
    • Pelvic lymph nodes (iliac, obturator)
    • Pelvic free fluid
  • 13
    Retroperitoneum & Vasculature
    • Aorta: diameter, aneurysm, dissection
    • Iliac arteries, portal vein, IVC patency
    • Para-aortic and pelvic lymphadenopathy (>1 cm SAX)
  • 14
    Bones
    • Vertebral bodies: compression fractures, lytic/blastic lesions
    • Pelvic bones, sacrum, femoral heads
    • Ribs: fractures, lytic lesions
CT Abdomen / Pelvis (AP) CT
CT Abdomen/Pelvis — Systematic Review
  • 1
    History & Indication
    • Acute abdomen vs. staging vs. follow-up
    • Contrast phase: non-contrast, arterial, venous, portal, excretory
  • 2
    Image Quality
    • Coverage: diaphragm to pubic symphysis
    • Contrast timing, motion artifact
  • 3
    Free Air / Free Fluid
    • Pneumoperitoneum: free air under diaphragm, outlining liver (Rigler sign on scout)
    • Ascites: dependent free fluid; hemoperitoneum (hyperdense >30 HU)
  • 4
    Liver
    • Lobar anatomy (Couinaud segments for surgical planning)
    • Lesions: hypodense (metastasis, abscess), hypervascular (HCC, hemangioma, FNH)
    • Cirrhosis morphology, portal hypertension (varices, splenomegaly, ascites)
    • Bile duct dilation: intrahepatic vs. extrahepatic
  • 5
    Gallbladder & Bile Ducts
    • Cholecystitis: wall >3 mm, pericholecystic fluid, Murphy sign on CT
    • Choledocholithiasis: hyperdense stone in CBD
  • 6
    Pancreas
    • Pancreatitis: stranding, necrosis (non-enhancing), pseudocyst, abscess
    • Mass: pancreatic adenocarcinoma (hypo), IPMN (duct or cystic), PNET (hypervascular)
  • 7
    Spleen
    • Size, focal lesions, trauma (laceration, subcapsular hematoma), infarct
  • 8
    Adrenals
    • Bilateral (mets, pheochromocytoma, adrenal hyperplasia) vs. unilateral (adenoma, mets)
    • Attenuation <10 HU or washout >60% = adenoma
  • 9
    Kidneys
    • Mass characterization: Bosniak cyst, RCC enhancement
    • Hydronephrosis and collecting system
    • Renal infarct (wedge-shaped perfusion defect)
    • Pyelonephritis: striated nephrogram, perinephric stranding
  • 10
    Bowel
    • Small bowel obstruction: transition point, closed loop, ischemia signs
    • Colitis: wall thickening, distribution (infectious vs. ischemic vs. IBD)
    • Appendicitis: >6 mm, periappendiceal fat, appendicolith
    • Diverticulitis: sigmoid fat stranding, abscess, fistula
    • Bowel ischemia: pneumatosis, portal venous gas, mesenteric vascular thrombosis
  • 11
    Mesentery & Retroperitoneum
    • Mesenteric lymph nodes, fat stranding
    • Retroperitoneal fibrosis, masses, hematoma
  • 12

MRI Abdomen — Sequence-Based Review
  • 1
    History & Indication
    • Liver lesion characterization (HCC, metastasis, hemangioma, FNH, adenoma)
    • Biliary pathology (MRCP)
    • Pancreatic mass characterization

  • 2
    Adequacy
    • Sequences present: T2 axial + coronal, DWI, T1 in-phase/out-of-phase, T1 fat-sat multiphasic dynamic, T1+C delayed
    • MRCP present if biliary indication
    • Breath-holding quality, motion artifact, coverage (diaphragm to iliac crests)
  • 3
    T2-Weighted Images
    • Liver signal: T2-bright lesions (hemangioma very bright, HCC intermediate, metastasis variable)
    • Bile ducts: intrahepatic and extrahepatic duct caliber
    • Gallbladder: wall signal, stones (T2 dark)
    • Spleen: homogeneous T2 bright; focal lesions
    • Pancreas: T2 signal, duct visibility
    • Kidneys: corticomedullary differentiation, cysts (very T2 bright), solid masses
    • Ascites: T2-bright free fluid
    • Bowel: wall thickness, luminal dilation
  • 4
    MRCP (if performed)
    • Biliary tree: CBD caliber, intrahepatic ducts, tapering vs. abrupt cutoff
    • Pancreatic duct: caliber, side branches (IPMN — main duct vs. side branch)
    • Choledocholithiasis: T2-dark stone in duct
    • Strictures: primary sclerosing cholangitis (beading), cholangiocarcinoma (dominant stricture)
    • Post-surgical anatomy: bilioenteric anastomosis
  • 5
    DWI / ADC Map
    • Diffusion-restricting lesions in liver: HCC, metastasis, abscess, cholangiocarcinoma
    • Pancreatic adenocarcinoma: hypointense on ADC
    • Lymph nodes: size + diffusion restriction = malignancy
    • Bowel wall diffusion restriction: ischemia, colitis
  • 6
    T1 In-Phase / Out-of-Phase
    • Signal drop on opposed-phase = intracellular fat (adrenal adenoma, HCC with fatty change, NAFLD/steatosis)
    • Diffuse hepatic steatosis: liver-to-spleen signal ratio
    • Focal fat deposition: geographic, perivascular
    • Adrenal lesions: chemical shift analysis
    • T1-bright lesions on in-phase: fat (angiomyolipoma), blood products
  • 7
    T1 Fat-Sat Dynamic Post-Contrast
    • Arterial phase (25–30 s): hypervascular lesions — HCC (arterial enhancement), FNH (spoke-wheel), hemangioma (peripheral nodular), PNET, hypervascular mets
    • Portal venous phase (70 s): hypovascular metastases (most conspicuous), liver parenchymal phase
    • Delayed phase (3–5 min): hemangioma fill-in, cholangiocarcinoma progressive enhancement, capsular retraction
    • Hepatobiliary phase (Eovist, 20 min): hypointense HCC vs. FNH (isointense or bright), adenoma dropout
  • 8
    Extrahepatic Organs
    • Spleen: focal lesions, size
    • Adrenal glands: unilateral vs. bilateral, enhancement pattern
    • Kidneys: enhancement pattern, collecting system
    • Retroperitoneum: lymph nodes, aorta
    • Bowel: wall enhancement, mesentery
  • 9
    Last Checks
    • Coronal and sagittal reformats
    • LI-RADS scoring if HCC risk: size, enhancement, washout, capsule, threshold growth
    • Measure all significant lesions (size, location, segment)
    • Compare with prior to determine stability/growth

MRI Prostate (mpMRI) — Sequence-Based / PI-RADS Review
  • 1
    History & Indication
    • PSA level and PSA density (PSA/gland volume)
    • Prior prostate biopsy (Gleason score, laterality)
    • Prior treatment: radiation, HIFU, focal ablation (post-treatment changes)
    • Prior mpMRI for comparison
  • 2
    Adequacy
    • Sequences: T2 axial + coronal + sagittal, DWI (multiple b-values including ≥1400), ADC map, DCE (T1 dynamic)
    • Endorectal coil (higher SNR) vs. surface coil only
    • Field strength: 3T preferred
    • Motion artifact, rectal distention (gas artifacts)
    • Note if prior biopsy hemorrhage (T1-bright on pre-contrast)
  • 3
    T1 Pre-Contrast — Hemorrhage Mapping
    • T1-bright signal in prostate = post-biopsy hemorrhage (can mimic or obscure lesions)
    • If hemorrhage present: ADC and DCE reliability reduced in affected areas
    • Seminal vesicles: T1-bright = hemorrhage
    • Biopsy-naïve prostate: T1 is baseline for hemorrhage exclusion
  • 4
    T2 Multiplanar — Anatomy & Zonal Assessment
    • Peripheral zone (PZ): normally homogeneously T2 bright; focal hypointensity = concern
    • Transition zone (TZ): heterogeneous (BPH nodules); TZ primary score based on T2
    • Central zone (CZ): T2 dark, around ejaculatory ducts — can mimic tumor
    • Seminal vesicles: T2 bright lumen; loss of signal = invasion (SVI)
    • Neurovascular bundles: posterolateral 5 and 7 o'clock — ECE assessment
    • Capsule: intact (smooth) vs. ECE (bulging, retraction, obliteration of rectoprostatic angle)
    • Bladder neck: invasion = T3b
    • Urethra and verumontanum: orientation landmarks
  • 5
    DWI (High b-Value ≥1400) / ADC Map — PRIMARY for PZ
    • High b-value DWI: focal hypointense lesion in PZ = PI-RADS 4–5 candidate
    • ADC map: focal markedly hypointense = clinically significant (Gleason ≥3+4)
    • ADC quantification: values <900–1000 μm²/s suggest significance
    • DWI primary score for PZ: 1–5 scale
    • TZ: DWI secondary to T2 for scoring
  • 6
    DCE T1 Dynamic Post-Contrast — SECONDARY for PZ
    • Positive DCE: focal early enhancement (earlier than or simultaneous with surrounding tissue)
    • Positive DCE upgrades PI-RADS 3 PZ lesion → PI-RADS 4
    • Negative DCE: no early focal enhancement
    • DCE not used for TZ scoring
  • 7
    Locoregional Extent
    • Extracapsular extension (ECE): capsular bulge, retraction, irregular margin, neurovascular bundle involvement (T2 + DCE)
    • Seminal vesicle invasion (SVI): T2 dark mass in SV lumen, loss of T2-bright lumen (T3b)
    • Bladder neck invasion (T3b)
    • Lymph nodes: obturator, external iliac, hypogastric; size ≥8–10 mm SAX or DWI restriction
    • Rectum: rectal wall invasion
  • 8
    Bones & Pelvic Structures
    • Sacrum, iliac wings, pubis, femoral heads: T1 low signal (replace normal fat) = metastasis
    • Pelvic floor and levator ani
  • 9
    Last Checks / PI-RADS Scoring
    • Assign PI-RADS score to all lesions ≥PI-RADS 3
    • Note lesion size, location (clock face, zone, apex/mid/base, distance from capsule)
    • Record total prostate volume for PSA density
    • Compare lesion locations with prior biopsy mapping
⚠ PI-RADS 4–5 warrants biopsy. PI-RADS 3 is equivocal — correlate with PSA density, prior biopsy, DCE.

    • Patient fasting (6 h for gallbladder)

    • Body and tail visibility (often limited by gas)

  • 8
    Kidneys
    • Size (normal 9–12 cm)

    • Hydronephrosis: grade 1–4

  • 9
    Aorta & IVC
    • Aortic diameter: normal <3 cm; AAA >3 cm

  • 10
    Free Fluid

  • 11
    Doppler Assessment
    • Portal vein: flow direction, velocity (normal hepatopetal)
    • Hepatic veins: triphasic waveform (loss = elevated right heart pressure, Budd-Chiari)
    • Renal arteries if indicated: RI, PSV
    • Hepatic artery: post-transplant assessment

Mammography — Systematic Review
  • 1
    History & Indication
    • Screening vs. diagnostic (targeted to area of clinical concern)
    • Age, risk factors (BRCA, dense breasts, prior biopsy), implants
    • Prior mammograms — essential for comparison
  • 2
    Image Quality
    • Positioning: MLO — pectoral muscle visible to nipple level, PNL, IMF open; CC — medial tissue, nipple in profile
    • Compression: adequate (motion blur)
    • Exposure: technically adequate on all four views
  • 3
    Breast Composition (ACR Density)
    • A: Almost entirely fatty (lowest masking)
    • B: Scattered fibroglandular (mild masking)
    • C: Heterogeneously dense (moderated masking — ultrasound may be recommended)
    • D: Extremely dense (significant masking — consider supplemental screening)
  • 4
    Masses
    • Shape: round, oval, irregular
    • Margins: circumscribed (likely benign), obscured, microlobulated, indistinct, spiculated (high suspicion)
    • Density: high, equal, low, fat-containing (lipoma, hamartoma, oil cyst, galactocele)
    • Location: quadrant, clock face, depth (anterior/middle/posterior third)
  • 5
    Calcifications
    • Typically benign: round/punctate, milk of calcium, vascular (tram-track), coarse/popcorn, skin, eggshell
    • Suspicious: amorphous, coarse heterogeneous, fine pleomorphic, fine linear/branching (casting)
    • Distribution: diffuse, regional, grouped/clustered, linear, segmental (most suspicious)
  • 6
    Asymmetries
    • Global: large area without mass — usually normal
    • Focal: seen on two projections — needs further assessment
    • Developing: new or growing on prior — high suspicion
    • One-view only: summation vs. real finding
  • 7
    Architectural Distortion
    • Radiating lines without central mass
    • Scar (prior surgery — correlate with history)
    • Radial scar/complex sclerosing lesion
    • Malignancy — high-suspicion finding; needs diagnostic workup
  • 8
    Skin & Nipple
    • Skin thickening: >2 mm diffuse (heart failure, lymphedema, inflammatory breast Ca), focal
    • Nipple retraction: acquired vs. congenital
    • Areolar thickening: Paget disease
  • 9
    Lymph Nodes & Axilla
    • Intramammary and axillary lymph nodes
    • Abnormal: round, dense, loss of fatty hilum
    • Axillary nodes: ≥3 cm or round = metastatic until proven otherwise
  • 10
    BI-RADS Assignment & Comparison
    • Compare with prior mammograms: new finding vs. stable
    • BI-RADS 0: needs additional imaging
    • BI-RADS 1–2: routine; BI-RADS 3: 6-month follow-up (≤2% malignancy)
    • BI-RADS 4: biopsy (4A 2–10%, 4B 10–50%, 4C 50–95%)
    • BI-RADS 5: highly suspicious (≥95%), biopsy

CT Head — Systematic Review
  • 1
    History & Indication
    • Acute stroke vs. headache vs. trauma vs. altered mental status vs. seizure vs. post-op
    • Time of symptom onset (stroke — thrombolytics window)
    • Prior CT head or MRI for comparison
  • 2
    Adequacy
    • Coverage: skull vertex to foramen magnum
    • Non-contrast vs. contrast-enhanced vs. CT perfusion
  • 3
    Blood / Hyperdense Lesions (First Pass)
    • Identify any hyperdense blood immediately
    • Epidural: biconvex lens-shaped, lenticular — lens does not cross sutures (temporal/temporal-parietal, MMA injury)
    • Subdural: crescent shape, crosses sutures, follows brain contour; acute (hyperdense >55 HU), subacute (isodense), chronic (hypodense)
    • Subarachnoid: cerebral sulci (interpeduncular, suprasellar, perimesencephalic cisterns), sylvian fissures — hyperdense
    • "SAH until proven otherwise": CT + LP if CT negative within 6 h of headache
    • Intraparenchymal hemorrhage: location (lobar — amyloid, hypertensive nuclei: putamen, thalamus, pons, cerebellum)
    • Intraventricular hemorrhage: ventricle density, hydrocephalus
  • 4
    Ischemia / Hypodense Lesions
    • Early infarct signs: loss of gray-white differentiation, insular ribbon sign, effacement of sulci
    • Hyperdense MCA sign: clot in MCA
    • Established infarct: hypodense, cortical ribbon, volume loss
    • ASPECTS score for MCA territory: 10 = normal, <7 = poor thrombolysis outcome
  • 5
    Ventricles & Cisterns
    • Ventricular size: hydrocephalus (communicating vs. non-communicating)
    • Third ventricle: dilated = obstruction at cerebral aqueduct or downstream
    • Fourth ventricle: compressed (posterior fossa mass) or dilated
    • Basal cisterns: effacement = raised ICP (herniation risk)
    • Transfalcine herniation: shift >5 mm = significant (mass effect)
  • 6
    Brain Parenchyma
    • Cerebral edema: diffuse sulcal effacement, loss of gray-white differentiation
    • Mass lesions: ring-enhancing (abscess, high-grade glioma, met), solid (low-grade glioma, lymphoma), calcified
    • Calcifications: physiologic (pineal, choroid) vs. pathologic (toxoplasmosis, NCC)
    • White matter changes: periventricular, subcortical (leukoaraiosis)
  • 7
    Midline Structures
    • Falx and tentorium: acute blood layering
    • Pineal gland: midline position (deviation = mass effect)
    • Midline shift measurement at level of foramen of Monro
  • 8
    Posterior Fossa
    • Cerebellum: hemorrhage, infarct (PICA, SCA territory), mass
    • Brainstem: hemorrhage (pons = hypertensive), mass
    • Fourth ventricle position
  • 9
    Skull & Calvarium
    • Fractures: linear vs. depressed; temporal bone fractures (longitudinal vs. transverse on CT temporal windows)
    • Pneumocephalus: air in intracranial compartment (trauma, post-op)
    • Calvarial lesions: lytic (mets, myeloma) vs. sclerotic
    • Sinuses: opacification (hemorrhage in trauma, sinusitis)
  • 10
    Extracranial Structures
    • Scalp: soft tissue hematoma (contrecoup)
    • Orbits: trauma, foreign body
    • Mastoid air cells
    • Cervical spine on scout
  • 11
    Herniation Patterns
    • Subfalcine: cingulate gyrus under falx
    • Uncal: medial temporal lobe through tentorial notch → CN III compression, posterior communicating artery, PCA
    • Central: downward displacement of diencephalon
    • Tonsillar: cerebellar tonsils through foramen magnum → cardiorespiratory arrest
  • 12
    CT Angiography (if CTA performed)
    • Vessel occlusion (LVO): ICA terminus, MCA M1/M2, basilar artery
    • Aneurysm: location (anterior communicating most common), size, morphology
    • Carotid/vertebral dissection: intimal flap, wall hematoma (crescent sign)
  • 13
    Last Checks
    • Subdural windows (W:200/L:80) for isodense subdurals
    • Bone windows for all skull fractures
    • Measure any shift, hemorrhage volume (ABC/2 method)
    • Soft copy review with adjust window/level
⚠ SAH: if clinical suspicion high and CT negative, LP at ≥6 h of onset (xanthochromia) or CT angiography.

    • Bone windows through entire cervical spine

Standard sequences: T1, T2, FLAIR, DWI/ADC, SWI or GRE, T1+C. Review in this sequence order. Additional: MRA, MRV, MRS, perfusion (ASL/DSC), functional MRI.
MRI Brain — Sequence-Based Review
  • 1
    History & Indication
    • Stroke (DWI protocol), seizure (epilepsy protocol), headache, dementia, mass lesion, MS workup, infection, post-op
    • Prior MRI brain: date, field strength, sequences — load and compare directly
    • Gadolinium given: dose, timing, adverse reaction history
  • 2
    Adequacy
    • Sequences present: T1, T2, FLAIR, DWI/ADC, SWI/GRE, T1+C
    • 3T vs. 1.5T (note limitation)
    • Motion artifact, ghosting
    • Coverage: vertex to foramen magnum
  • 3
    T2 / FLAIR — Parenchymal Survey (Start Here)
    • White matter: periventricular lesions (MS — ovoid, juxtacortical, perpendicular to ventricles; Dawson fingers on sagittal FLAIR), subcortical, deep WM
    • Cortical signal: FLAIR cortical/gyral T2 brightness — meningitis, leptomeningeal carcinomatosis, seizure (post-ictal)
    • Leukoaraiosis: periventricular/subcortical confluent T2 bright — microvascular disease, Fazekas scale
    • Cerebral edema: sulcal effacement, loss of gray-white differentiation
    • Basal ganglia: T2 signal (hepatic encephalopathy — T1 bright; Wilson disease — T2 bright)
    • Brainstem: T2 bright pontine lesion (MS, glioma, osmotic demyelination)
    • Cerebellum: atrophy, T2 bright lesions (stroke territory, demyelination)
    • Ventricles: hydrocephalus (communicating — trans-ependymal T2 shine-through; non-communicating — mass at aqueduct or 4th)
    • Hippocampi: T2 signal, volume (hippocampal sclerosis)
    • Sulci: cortical atrophy pattern
  • 4
    DWI / ADC — Restricted Diffusion
    • Acute infarct: DWI bright + ADC dark = cytotoxic edema (onset <6 h — DWI positive before T2); vascular territory distribution
    • Subacute infarct: T2 shine-through on DWI with ADC pseudo-normalization (>1–2 weeks)
    • Abscess: rim-enhancing + DWI central restriction (high viscosity pus)
    • Epidermoid: DWI bright (vs. arachnoid cyst — DWI dark)
    • Dense cellularity: lymphoma, PNET/medulloblastoma, GBM central necrosis (variable)
    • Hypoxic-ischemic injury: bilateral cortical ribboning, basal ganglia restriction
    • Prion disease (CJD): cortical ribboning + basal ganglia/thalamus (pulvinar sign)
  • 5
    SWI / GRE — Blood Products & Susceptibility
    • Microhemorrhages: small punctate hypointense foci
    • Distribution: lobar (CAA — β-amyloid angiopathy), deep nuclei/infratentorial (hypertensive)
    • Cavernoma: popcorn morphology, complete hemosiderin ring
    • Calcifications: SWI — bilateral basal ganglia calcifications (Fahr, hypoparathyroidism)
    • Large blood products: subdural, epidural on SWI for staging (acute bright → subacute T1 bright → chronic dark on SWI)
    • Intraventricular blood: subtle blood products
  • 6
    T1 Pre-Contrast
    • T1-bright lesions: fat (lipoma, dermoid), blood products (met-hemoglobin 3–14 days), melanoma mets, proteinaceous cyst, calcification
    • Cortical laminar necrosis: T1-bright gyral pattern (subacute infarct)
    • Posterior pituitary bright spot: absent = central diabetes insipidus
    • Basal ganglia T1 brightness: hepatic failure, manganese, Pantothenate kinase, hypoxic-ischemic
    • White matter T1 dark: active demyelination, edema
  • 7
    T1 Post-Contrast — Enhancement Patterns
    • Blood-brain barrier breakdown: any enhancement = significant
    • Ring-enhancing: abscess (thick smooth ring, DWI central restriction) vs. GBM (thick irregular) vs. met (thin regular ring) — MAGIC DR mnemonic
    • Leptomeningeal enhancement: meningitis (bacterial — intense), leptomeningeal carcinomatosis, neurosarcoid
    • Nodular/solid: metastasis, meningioma (dural tail), lymphoma, medulloblastoma
    • Vascular enhancement: cortical vein thrombosis, AVM draining veins
    • Cranial nerve enhancement: CN VII (Bell palsy), CN V (perineural tumor spread, neurosarcoid)
    • Pituitary enhancement: microadenoma (hypointense relative to normal gland)
  • 8
    MRA (if included)
    • Circle of Willis: vessel caliber, flow-related signal
    • Aneurysm: focal outpouching; correlate with CTA if large
    • Stenosis or occlusion: flow void absence or signal reduction
    • Vertebrobasilar system: basilar artery patency
  • 9
    MRV (if included)
    • Dural venous sinus patency: SSS, transverse, sigmoid, straight, torcula
    • CVST: filling defect, delta sign on T1+C
    • Developmental variant: hypoplastic transverse sinus (left > right) — bilateral assessment
    • Cortical vein thrombosis: adjacent parenchymal injury
  • 10
    Extracranial Structures
    • Orbits: optic nerve signal, globe
    • Paranasal sinuses: fluid level, mucosal thickening, opacification (especially sphenoid near pituitary)
    • Mastoid air cells: opacification
    • Skull: lytic/sclerotic calvarial lesions, marrow signal
    • Scalp and subgaleal fat: masses
    • Cavernous sinus: asymmetry, thrombosis
  • 11
    Last Checks
    • Coronal and sagittal T1/FLAIR for corpus callosum lesions (MS), cerebellar tonsil position (Chiari)
    • Measure any lesion in 3 planes
    • Report total number of white matter lesions if MS protocol
    • Compare with all priors — new vs. old lesion critical distinction

  • 1
    History & Indication
    • Seizure type: focal (temporal vs. extratemporal) vs. generalized
    • Duration of epilepsy: new-onset vs. refractory
    • Prior MRI (standard vs. epilepsy protocol), prior brain surgery, EEG lateralization
    • Family history: tuberous sclerosis, neurofibromatosis
  • 2
    Adequacy
    • Epilepsy protocol sequences present: thin coronal T2 (3 mm), coronal FLAIR, 3D T1 volumetric (MPRAGE/BRAVO), DWI
    • Hippocampal-axis alignment of coronal sequences (oblique coronal perpendicular to long axis of hippocampus)
    • T1+C if first episode or focal neurologic deficit
  • 3
    Coronal T2 — Hippocampi (Primary Epilepsy Target)
    • Hippocampal sclerosis (MTS): unilateral T2 brightness, volume loss, loss of internal architecture (digitations)
    • Symmetry: compare CA1, CA3, CA4, subiculum
    • Hippocampal rotation (incomplete inversion): abnormal orientation — epilepsy association
    • Bilateral lesions (bilateral MTS or dual pathology)
  • 4
    T2 / FLAIR — Cortical Dysplasia & Other Substrates
    • Focal cortical dysplasia (FCD): T2/FLAIR bright cortex, blurred gray-white junction, transmantle sign (type II — extends to ventricle)
    • Cortical thickening and gyral abnormalities
    • Heterotopia: gray matter signal tissue in abnormal location (periventricular — smooth band, nodular; subcortical)
    • Tubers (tuberous sclerosis): T2 bright subcortical and cortical tubers, subependymal nodules
    • Cortical malformations: polymicrogyria, lissencephaly, schizencephaly
  • 5
    3D T1 Volumetric (MPRAGE)
    • Hippocampal volumetry: asymmetry ≥10–15% clinically significant
    • Gyral morphology: polymicrogyria (irregular sulcal pattern), pachygyria (simplified gyri)
    • Cortical thickness asymmetry: thinning (post-encephalitic, chronic ischemia)
    • Subcortical heterotopia: T1 dark gray matter bands
    • Cavernoma or other T1 bright lesion
  • 6
    DWI / ADC
    • Post-ictal restricted diffusion: DWI bright in ipsilateral cortex after prolonged seizure (pseudo-stroke — usually reversible)
    • Acute encephalitis: bilateral limbic T2/DWI changes
    • Acute ischemia if clinically indeterminate (stroke vs. seizure)
  • 7
    T1 Post-Contrast (if performed)
    • Low-grade glioma: subtle enhancement (FLAIR bright, ±enhancement)
    • DNET or ganglioglioma: typical temporal lobe; bubbly FLAIR, ±enhancement
    • Cavernoma: no enhancement; hemosiderin ring on SWI
    • Active encephalitis: cortical/meningeal enhancement
    • Hypothalamic hamartoma: no enhancement, isointense T1
  • 8
    SWI / GRE
    • Cavernoma: hallmark "popcorn" with hemosiderin ring (epileptogenic)
    • Calcifications in FCD type IIb, tubers
    • Small hemorrhagic lesion not visible on other sequences
  • 9
    Last Checks
    • Sagittal FLAIR: corpus callosum lesions (callosal dysgenesis, MS)
    • Cerebellar tonsil position: Chiari I (>5 mm below McRae line)
    • Compare with any prior standard brain MRI
    • If hippocampal sclerosis: lateralize (ipsilateral to EEG focus)
⚠ Negative epilepsy MRI does not exclude surgically remediable epilepsy — may need 7T or advanced post-processing.
MRI Orbits MRI
Fat saturation essential for optic nerve and post-contrast assessment. Coronal and axial planes required. DWI helpful for dense lesions. T1+C fat-sat in both planes.
MRI Orbits — Sequence-Based Review
  • 1
    History & Indication
    • Vision loss, proptosis, diplopia, pain, pulsatile tinnitus, eyelid swelling, post-trauma
    • Prior imaging: CT orbits, prior MRI orbits
  • 2
    Adequacy
    • Sequences: T2 fat-sat axial + coronal, T1, DWI, T1 fat-sat post-contrast axial + coronal
    • Coverage: apex of orbit through entire globe and periorbital structures
    • Fat saturation uniformity
  • 3
    T2 Fat-Sat — Soft Tissue Assessment (Start Here)
    • Optic nerve: T2 signal (optic neuritis — T2 bright, enlarged); perineural subarachnoid space enlargement (papilledema, NF-1, meningioma)
    • Globe: posterior coat thickening (uveal melanoma, choroidal effusion, retinal detachment — subretinal vs. choroidal fluid)
    • Extraocular muscles: thickening — thyroid eye disease (inferior > medial; apex sparing), myositis (tendinous insertion involvement)
    • Lacrimal gland: enlargement — sarcoid (most common cause of bilateral enlargement), lymphoma, Sjögren's, adenoid cystic Ca
    • Orbital fat: T2 stranding — cellulitis, pseudotumor, post-traumatic
    • Subperiosteal space: subperiosteal abscess (sinus origin), hematoma
    • Intraconal vs. extraconal space: location determines differential
  • 4
    DWI / ADC
    • Lymphoma: restricted diffusion (dense cellularity)
    • Retinoblastoma (children): dense, restricted diffusion
    • Schwannoma vs. meningioma: DWI usually unrestricted
    • Epidermoid: DWI bright (rare in orbit)
  • 5
    T1 Pre-Contrast
    • T1-bright lesions: fat (dermoid — anterior, superonasal), hemorrhage (blood cyst), melanoma (intrinsic T1 bright)
    • Normal structures: optic nerve, eye anatomy
    • Globe lesion morphology: choroidal melanoma (T1 bright, T2 variable)
  • 6
    T1 Fat-Sat Post-Contrast
    • Optic nerve enhancement: optic neuritis, optic nerve sheath meningioma (tram-track enhancement)
    • Extraocular muscle enhancement: active thyroid orbitopathy, myositis
    • Lacrimal gland enhancement: solid vs. cystic lesion
    • Orbital apex: compression of optic nerve at apex (surgical emergency)
    • Intracranial extension: cavernous sinus, optic chiasm, perineural spread along CN V1
    • Orbital wall masses: rhabdomyosarcoma (children), lymphoma, metastasis
  • 7
    Vascular Assessment
    • Cavernous sinus: symmetric enhancement, AV fistula (dilated superior ophthalmic vein)
    • Superior ophthalmic vein: dilation (orbital AV fistula, cavernous sinus thrombosis)
    • Orbital varix: enhancing dilated vein, increases with Valsalva
  • 8
    Intracranial Extension
    • Optic chiasm involvement: craniopharyngioma, pituitary macroadenoma, chiasmal glioma
    • Sphenoid wing meningioma: orbital extension
    • Skull base invasion: rhabdomyosarcoma, NPC extension
    • Cavernous sinus invasion: perineural spread
  • 9
    Last Checks
    • Sinuses: mucocele extending into orbit, orbital floor fracture (trauma)
    • Intracranial: brain parenchyma, visual pathway
    • Contralateral orbit: bilateral disease (lymphoma, sarcoid, TED, thyroid)
    • Children: retinoblastoma (calcification on CT), optic pathway glioma (NF-1)
MRI IAC / Posterior Fossa MRI
Key sequence: FIESTA/CISS/DRIVE thin-section T2 (0.8 mm isotropic) for nerve detail within IAC and CP angle cistern. Always bilateral and symmetrical comparison.
MRI IAC — Sequence-Based Review
  • 1
    History & Indication
    • Unilateral SNHL, tinnitus, vertigo (Ménière's), facial nerve palsy, hemifacial spasm, CP angle mass
    • Prior MRI IAC, audiogram, and caloric testing results
  • 2
    Adequacy
    • Sequences: FIESTA/CISS thin-section, T2 axial, T1, T1 fat-sat post-contrast
    • Coverage: bilateral IAC from fundus to brainstem; cerebellopontine angles
    • Fat saturation on T1+C (labyrinthine enhancement)
  • 3
    FIESTA / CISS / DRIVE — Cranial Nerve Detail (Start Here)
    • IAC contents: superior vestibular nerve (anterior superior), inferior vestibular nerve, cochlear nerve (anteroinferior), facial nerve (anterosuperior) — SAFE mnemonic
    • Cochlear nerve diameter: agenesis/hypoplasia (congenital SNHL workup)
    • Cochlea: modiolus, 2.5 turns, cochlear aperture patency
    • Semicircular canals: lateral, superior, posterior
    • Endolymphatic duct and sac: dilation (Ménière's)
    • CP angle cistern: CSF signal — mass (soft tissue displacing nerves vs. CSF-like signal = epidermoid or arachnoid cyst)
  • 4
    DWI / ADC
    • Epidermoid cyst: DWI bright (most important distinguishing feature from arachnoid cyst)
    • Cholesteatoma: DWI bright
    • Arachnoid cyst: DWI dark (CSF signal)
  • 5
    T1 Pre-Contrast
    • Labyrinthine hemorrhage: T1 bright in cochlea or vestibule (hemorrhagic labyrinthitis)
    • Lipoma: T1 bright fat along CN VII–VIII (rare, benign)
    • Posterior fossa hemorrhage: hyperacute blood on T1
  • 6
    T1 Fat-Sat Post-Contrast
    • Acoustic neuroma / vestibular schwannoma (VS): enhancing mass centered at IAC fundus or CP angle; "ice cream cone" shape; bilateral = NF-2
    • Facial nerve schwannoma: follows facial nerve canal (labyrinthine, geniculate, tympanic, mastoid segments)
    • Labyrinthine enhancement: acute labyrinthitis, viral (SNHL), suppurative labyrinthitis
    • Meningeal enhancement: meningitis, leptomeningeal carcinomatosis
    • Posterior fossa meningioma: dural-based, broad base, dural tail, CP angle
  • 7
    CP Angle Cistern
    • 3 most common CP angle masses: acoustic neuroma/VS (55%), meningioma (35%), epidermoid (5%)
    • Meningioma: obtuse angle with petrous bone, calcification, no IAC extension
    • Epidermoid: follows CSF on T1/T2, DWI bright, lobulated "cauliflower" shape
    • Arachnoid cyst: CSF signal on all sequences, mass effect
  • 8
    Brainstem & Cerebellum
    • Facial nerve nucleus: brainstem T2/FLAIR
    • Trigeminal nerve: neurovascular conflict (hemifacial spasm / trigeminal neuralgia — superior cerebellar artery or AICA contact)
    • Cerebellar hemisphere/tonsil: atrophy, lesion
    • Fourth ventricle: compression, displacement
  • 9
    Last Checks
    • Coronal views for superior semicircular canal dehiscence assessment
    • Compare IAC diameter bilaterally (>2 mm asymmetry = study posterior fossa)
    • Mastoid opacification on sequences
MRI Sella / Pituitary MRI
Thin-section (3 mm or less) coronal + sagittal T1 and T2. Dynamic coronal T1+C (early frames 20–30 s) essential for microadenoma detection. Coverage: cavernous sinuses to optic chiasm.
MRI Sella/Pituitary — Sequence-Based Review
  • 1
    History & Indication
    • Hormone excess (Cushing's — ACTH; acromegaly — GH; hyperprolactinemia) vs. hormone deficiency (panhypopituitarism)
    • Visual field defects (bitemporal hemianopia — suprasellar extension, chiasm compression)
    • Headache; incidental on brain MRI; post-surgical assessment
    • Prior pituitary MRI, hormone levels, visual field testing
  • 2
    Adequacy
    • Sequences: thin coronal T2, coronal + sagittal T1 pre-contrast, dynamic coronal T1+C (key), standard T1+C coronal + sagittal
    • Slice thickness ≤3 mm through sella
    • Coverage: clivus to suprasellar cistern; include cavernous sinuses
  • 3
    T2 — Pituitary Anatomy & Lesion Signal
    • Pituitary gland size: height — normal ≤8 mm women, ≤6 mm men (physiologic enlargement in pregnancy up to 12 mm)
    • Gland shape: symmetric convex vs. asymmetric bulge (microadenoma side)
    • Stalk: midline vs. deviation (deviation toward normal side suggests adenoma on opposite side)
    • Optic chiasm: position (prefixed, normal, postfixed); elevation or compression
    • Hypothalamus: mass, hamartoma (isointense T2, no enhancement)
    • Cavernous sinuses: T2 signal, symmetric enhancement
    • T2 signal of lesion: bright = Rathke cleft cyst (unilocular, follows CSF), craniopharyngioma (heterogeneous, cystic), cystic adenoma
  • 4
    T1 Pre-Contrast — Fat & Bright Signal
    • Posterior pituitary bright spot (neurohypophysis): normally T1 bright — absence = central DI or infiltration
    • T1 bright lesion: craniopharyngioma (cholesterol-rich cyst), Rathke cleft cyst (protein-rich), hemorrhagic adenoma (apoplexy)
    • Fat: dermoid, lipoma
  • 5
    Dynamic T1+C (Coronal, Early Frames) — Microadenoma Detection
    • Normal pituitary enhances quickly and uniformly
    • Microadenoma (<10 mm): hypointense relative to brightly enhancing normal gland in early frames
    • Stalk wash-in: normal stalk enhancement on early frames
    • Later frames: adenoma may fill in (isointense) — early timing critical
    • ACTH-producing microadenoma (Cushing's): often very small (<6 mm), may require inferior petrosal sinus sampling if not seen
  • 6
    Standard T1+C (Coronal + Sagittal) — Macroadenoma & Extent
    • Macroadenoma (≥10 mm): solid enhancement, may be heterogeneous (necrosis, hemorrhage)
    • Suprasellar extension: degree of chiasm elevation/compression (bitemporal hemianopia threshold)
    • Cavernous sinus invasion: Knosp grading (0–4); grade ≥3 = true invasion, not surgically curable
    • Sphenoid sinus extension: surgical route planning (transsphenoidal)
    • Apoplexy: heterogeneous enhancement, hemorrhage or infarction within macroadenoma
  • 7
    Parasellar & Suprasellar Lesions (Differential)
    • Craniopharyngioma: suprasellar, calcified (adamantinomatous type), cystic, cholesterol crystals
    • Meningioma: parasellar or tuberculum sellae; homogeneous enhancement, dural tail, calcification
    • Rathke cleft cyst: intrasellar/suprasellar, T2 bright, no enhancement
    • Pituitary hyperplasia: diffuse gland enlargement (hypothyroidism, pregnancy, puberty) — diagnosis of exclusion
    • Aneurysm: pulsatile, flow void on T2, risk of rupture — CTA or MRA to confirm
  • 8
    Optic Pathway
    • Chiasm: compression, signal change (T2 bright = edema/ischemia)
    • Optic nerves: sheath thickening (meningioma)
    • Optic tracts: compression leading to homonymous hemianopia
  • 9
    Extratarget
    • Sphenoid sinus: mucocele, inflammatory disease (can elevate pituitary floor)
    • Clivus: chordoma (T2 very bright, destructive)
    • Temporal lobes: herniation risk with large macroadenoma
  • 10
    Last Checks
    • Measure adenoma in 3 dimensions
    • Note Knosp grade for macroadenoma
    • Note stalk position
    • Compare with prior pituitary MRI carefully for interval change in size
MRI Soft Tissue Neck MRI
Standard: T1, T2 fat-sat or STIR, DWI, T1 fat-sat post-contrast. Axial primary plane + sagittal/coronal for longitudinal extent. Coverage: skull base to thoracic inlet/clavicles.
MRI Soft Tissue Neck — Sequence-Based Review
  • 1
    History & Indication
    • Neck mass: duration, growth, pain, voice changes, dysphagia, B symptoms
    • Prior surgery or radiation (post-treatment changes, recurrence vs. fibrosis)
    • Primary SCC of pharynx/larynx — staging vs. nodes only
    • Prior CT or PET-CT for comparison; gadolinium planned
  • 2
    Adequacy
    • Sequences: T2 fat-sat axial + coronal, STIR sagittal, DWI, T1 axial, T1 fat-sat post-contrast axial + coronal
    • Coverage: skull base to clavicles
    • Fat saturation uniformity, motion artifact, metallic artifact from dental hardware
  • 3
    T2 Fat-Sat / STIR — Tissue Characterization (Start Here)
    • Primary tumor signal: T2 intermediate-to-bright (SCC), T2 bright (minor salivary gland mucoepidermoid)
    • Nodal signal: necrotic nodes (T2 bright center, thick walls), extranodal extension (ill-defined margins into fat)
    • Thyroid gland: T2 signal — heterogeneous nodular gland; cysts (T2 very bright)
    • Salivary glands: parotid (predominantly fatty T2), submandibular, sublingual masses
    • Pharyngeal walls: thickness, symmetry, mucosal asymmetry
    • Larynx: aryepiglottic folds, glottis — T2 signal of cartilages (T2 bright = edema; after radiation — expected change vs. recurrence)
    • Pre/paravertebral muscles: edema (T2 bright) — direct invasion vs. reactive
    • Bone marrow (clivus, C-spine): T2 signal — normal fatty marrow vs. T2 bright edema
  • 4
    DWI / ADC
    • Primary tumor: restricted diffusion correlates with tumor cellularity; SCC is hypointense on ADC
    • Metastatic nodes: restricted diffusion + necrosis (central T2 bright + rim enhancement)
    • Lymphoma: markedly restricted diffusion (high cellularity)
    • Post-treatment: scar (no restriction) vs. recurrence (restriction)
  • 5
    T1 — Fat Planes & Marrow
    • Fat planes: preserved = no invasion; obliterated fat = suspect invasion of adjacent structure
    • Parapharyngeal fat (key landmark): displaced toward mass (indicates origin; intrinsic parapharyngeal mass = fat centered)
    • Bone marrow signal: vertebral body fat = normal; T1 dark = metastatic replacement, infection
    • Carotid arterial signal: flow void = patent; T1 bright thrombus
  • 6
    T1 Fat-Sat Post-Contrast
    • Primary tumor enhancement: solid, heterogeneous; extent of enhancement defines gross tumor volume (GTV) for radiation planning
    • Perineural spread (critical finding): enlargement and enhancement of foramina (foramen ovale CN V3, stylomastoid CN VII, jugular foramen CN IX/X/XI), retrograde tracking along nerve
    • Nodal enhancement: necrotic core (dark) + enhancing rim = necrotic nodal metastasis
    • Vascular invasion: carotid encasement (≥270° contact = unresectable)
    • Post-treatment: mucosal enhancement suggests active disease vs. mucosal/submucosal fibrosis (T1 dark, no enhancement)
  • 7
    Lymph Node Levels
    • Level I: submandibular, submental
    • Level II: upper jugular (IIA — anterior to IJV; IIB — posterior)
    • Level III: mid-jugular
    • Level IV: lower jugular
    • Level V: posterior cervical triangle
    • Level VI: anterior/central compartment (paratracheal, prelaryngeal, pretracheal)
    • Retropharyngeal nodes: lateral ≥8 mm SAX, medial (Rouvière) ≥5 mm
    • Note: size, necrosis, extranodal extension (ENE) for each suspicious node
  • 8
    Deep Neck Spaces
    • Parapharyngeal: fat preserved/displaced
    • Masticator: pterygoid muscle invasion, mandible cortex
    • Retropharyngeal/danger spaces: abscess, mass — potential mediastinal extension
    • Carotid space: ICA/IJV, paraganglioma (splaying ICA/ECA), schwannoma
    • Visceral space: thyroid, parathyroid, trachea, esophagus
  • 9
    Skull Base & Intracranial
    • Foramina: ovale, spinosum, rotundum, stylomastoid — perineural spread tracking
    • Clivus, petrous apex, cavernous sinus invasion
    • Intracranial extension: subdural, parenchymal
    • Nasopharynx apex: Rosenmuller fossa asymmetry, NPC extension
  • 10
    Extratarget
    • Lung apices: metastasis, lymphoma
    • Thyroid: incidental nodules, substernal extension
    • Cervical spine: marrow signal
    • Superior mediastinum: lymphadenopathy
  • 11
    Last Checks
    • Sagittal for longitudinal extent and retropharyngeal space
    • Coronal for paramedian structures, skull base foramina
    • Measure primary tumor in 3D
    • Nodal levels: complete mapping for staging
    • Assign T, N stage if oncologic study
MRI Cervical Spine MRI
Standard: sagittal T2, sagittal STIR (or fat-sat T2), sagittal T1, axial T2 (GRE/MEDIC in trauma for cord detail). T1+C if infection, tumor, or post-surgical. Cover C1–T1 (or T2).
MRI Cervical Spine — Sequence-Based Review
  • 1
    History & Indication
    • Myelopathy (hand clumsiness, gait, bowel/bladder), radiculopathy (dermatomal pain/numbness/weakness), trauma, known malignancy, inflammatory arthritis
    • Prior MRI cervical spine: compare level-by-level
    • Gadolinium given? (infection, tumor, demyelination, post-op)
  • 2
    Adequacy
    • Sequences: sagittal T2, sagittal STIR or fat-sat T2, sagittal T1, axial T2 or GRE
    • T1+C sagittal and axial if indicated
    • Coverage: brainstem/craniocervical junction through C7–T1
  • 3
    Sagittal T2 — Primary Survey (Start Here)
    • Spinal cord signal: T2 bright intramedullary lesion — myelopathy (chronic degenerative — central T2 bright behind disk), demyelination (MS plaque — short-segment, eccentric, peripheral), tumor (ependymoma — central with hemosiderin caps; astrocytoma — fusiform), syrinx (T2 bright fluid-filled cavity within cord)
    • Cord diameter: focal thinning = chronic myelomalacia; swelling = acute cord injury or tumor
    • Disk herniations: central, paracentral, foraminal, far-lateral; disc signal (dark = degenerated)
    • Vertebral body alignment: anterolisthesis/retrolisthesis
    • Canal diameter: anterior-posterior; ligamentum flavum buckling posterior
  • 4
    Sagittal STIR — Edema-Sensitive Survey
    • Vertebral body marrow edema: T2 bright (acute fracture vs. metastasis vs. infection — Modic 1 change)
    • Disk endplate signal: Modic type 1 (T2 bright = active degeneration), type 2 (T1 bright fat), type 3 (T1/T2 dark = sclerosis)
    • Paraspinal soft tissue edema: infection, ligamentous injury, post-traumatic
    • Posterior ligamentous complex: STIR bright = ligamentous injury (trauma)
  • 5
    Sagittal T1 — Marrow & Fat Anatomy
    • Normal vertebral marrow: T1 bright (fat-containing)
    • Pathologic marrow replacement: T1 dark = metastasis, lymphoma, myeloma, infection, edema (low specificity on T1 alone — needs STIR correlation)
    • Fatty Schmorl nodes and end-plate changes (T1 bright = Modic 2)
    • Foraminal fat: T1 bright preservation = no foraminal stenosis; obliteration = stenosis
  • 6
    Axial T2 / GRE — Level-by-Level Canal Assessment
    • Central canal: AP diameter; spinal cord compression grading (Kang/modified JOA for clinical significance)
    • Cord morphology: central gray butterfly, size, signal per level
    • Disk herniation: type (central, subarticular, foraminal, extraforaminal), contact with nerve root, cord contact
    • Uncovertebral joint osteophytes: lateral canal/foraminal narrowing
    • Ligamentum flavum: hypertrophy = posterior canal compromise
    • Facet joints: arthropathy, hypertrophic changes
  • 7
    T1 Post-Contrast (if performed)
    • Cord enhancement: demyelination (active MS plaque enhances with Gd — ring or solid), ependymoma/astrocytoma
    • Epidural enhancement: epidural abscess (rim-enhancing fluid collection), granulation tissue (post-surgical)
    • Disk/endplate enhancement: diskitis-osteomyelitis (end plates + disk)
    • Meningeal enhancement: leptomeningeal metastases, arachnoiditis
    • Paraspinal/pre/retro-vertebral enhancement: phlegmon vs. abscess
  • 8
    Extratarget Structures
    • Posterior fossa and craniocervical junction: Chiari malformation (tonsils >5 mm below McRae line), os odontoideum, pannus (RA)
    • Lung apices: Pancoast tumor (correlate with radiculopathy)
    • Thyroid and prevertebral soft tissues
    • Vertebral arteries in foramina transversaria (flow voids)
  • 9
    Last Checks
    • Note spinal cord level of injury/lesion
    • Measure any disk herniation or canal compromise in mm
    • Ensure C7-T1 junction included
    • Report cord signal change (myelopathy) prominently — impacts surgical decision
⚠ Myelopathy: T2 bright cord signal + clinical symptoms = surgical emergency. Report cord compression grade at each relevant level.
MRI Thoracic Spine MRI
Sagittal T2 is the key sequence. Conus medullaris at T12–L1 is critical. T1+C for cord lesions, metastases, infection. Axial T2 through levels of cord signal change or suspected disk herniation.
MRI Thoracic Spine — Sequence-Based Review
  • 1
    History & Indication
    • Myelopathy (bilateral leg weakness, gait, bowel/bladder — thoracic cord lesion until proven otherwise), back pain with cord signs, known malignancy/staging, scoliosis MRI, suspected infection/demyelination
    • Prior MRI thoracic spine
  • 2
    Adequacy
    • Sequences: sagittal T2, sagittal STIR, sagittal T1, axial T2 (at levels of interest)
    • Coverage: cervicothoracic junction through conus (T12–L1)
    • T1+C if cord signal change, infection, or tumor
  • 3
    Sagittal T2 — Primary Survey
    • Spinal cord signal: entire length of thoracic cord; T2 bright intramedullary signal
    • Cord lesion characterization: ependymoma (central, hemosiderin caps), astrocytoma (fusiform, ill-defined), MS plaques (short-segment, ≤2 vertebral segments, peripheral), NMO (>3 segments, central — holospinal), syringomyelia (fluid cavity)
    • Conus position: normal T12–L1; low-lying conus = tethered cord
    • Disk herniations: less common in thoracic spine; calcified disks
    • Vertebral body alignment and height
    • Canal diameter
  • 4
    Sagittal STIR — Bone Marrow & Soft Tissue Edema
    • Vertebral body edema: metastases (T2/STIR bright, round vs. confluent), acute compression fracture (T2 bright = edema), infection
    • Paraspinal signal: abscess (T2 bright collection), inflammatory change
    • Posterior elements: stress reactions, fracture edema
    • Ligamentous injury (trauma): posterior ligamentous complex T2/STIR bright
  • 5
    Sagittal T1 — Marrow Signal
    • Normal marrow: T1 bright fat throughout
    • Metastatic replacement: T1 dark lesions (focal or diffuse — marrow replacement pattern)
    • Hemangioma: T1 bright "corduroy" or stippled pattern (fat-containing trabeculae)
    • Degenerative changes: Schmorl nodes, end plate irregularity
    • Fracture morphology: T1 signal within vertebral body — acute (T1 dark edema) vs. chronic (T1 bright fat)
  • 6
    Axial T2 — Level-by-Level (Levels of Interest)
    • Cord morphology and diameter
    • Disk herniation morphology and cord contact
    • Canal diameter
    • Epidural abnormality: abscess, hematoma, metastasis
  • 7
    T1 Post-Contrast (if performed)
    • Cord enhancement: active demyelination (MS), ependymoma enhancement, intramedullary abscess (rare)
    • Leptomeningeal enhancement: carcinomatosis, TB, CMV (HIV)
    • Epidural enhancement: metastatic disease, abscess, post-surgical granulation tissue
    • Vertebral/disk enhancement: diskitis-osteomyelitis (end plates + disk)
  • 8
    Extratarget Structures
    • Visualized lungs and pleura: pleural effusion, consolidation, mediastinal mass
    • Aorta and great vessels: thoracic aortic aneurysm, atherosclerosis
    • Heart: pericardial effusion
    • Liver dome, spleen, adrenals on inferior images
  • 9
    Last Checks
    • Count vertebral levels from cervicothoracic junction (T1)
    • Note cord signal change level precisely
    • Measure any epidural collection or canal compromise
    • Conus position: report if low (below L2 in adults = tethered cord)
⚠ Myelopathy localizing to thoracic cord: T2 signal ≥3 segments = NMO spectrum (AQP4/MOG); <3 segments eccentric = MS; central enhancement = ependymoma.
MRI Lumbar Spine MRI
Most common MRI study. Sagittal T2 is workhorse. Axial T2 at every disk level is mandatory. STIR for edema. T1+C for infection, tumor, post-operative. Cover L1–S1 (include T12 and sacrum).
MRI Lumbar Spine — Sequence-Based Review
  • 1
    History & Indication
    • Low back pain, radiculopathy (dermatomal pattern, which level?), neurogenic claudication, known malignancy, cauda equina syndrome, prior spine surgery
    • Prior MRI lumbar spine — compare level by level
    • Gadolinium given? (post-op: scar vs. recurrent disk; infection; tumor)
  • 2
    Adequacy
    • Sequences: sagittal T2, sagittal STIR or fat-sat T2, sagittal T1, axial T2 at each disk level
    • T1+C sagittal + axial if post-surgical or infection
    • Coverage: T12/L1 junction through sacrum including S1 nerve roots

    Sagittal T2 — Primary Survey (Start Here)
    • Disk signal: T2 bright (hydrated) vs. dark (desiccated/degenerated); note each level

    • Posterior disk morphology: central vs. paracentral protrusion/extrusion (size, direction, relationship to level)

    Sagittal STIR — Marrow Edema & Soft Tissue
    • Vertebral body edema: acute fractures (T2/STIR bright), metastases (multiple lesions), infection (end plate STIR bright)

MSK MRI — Infection / Osteomyelitis — Sequence-Based Review
  • 1
    History & Indication
    • Diabetes, immunocompromise, IV drug use, prior hardware, open wound, post-surgery
    • Fever, elevated WBC/ESR/CRP, bacteremia

    Adequacy
    • Sequences: STIR or T2 fat-sat (coronal + axial), T1 (coronal + axial), T1 fat-sat post-contrast (coronal + axial)

    • DWI if abscess suspected; note metallic hardware artifact limitation
  • 3
    T2 / STIR — Extent of Edema (Start Here)
    • Bone marrow edema: T2/STIR bright; single bone vs. multi-bone vs. contiguous spread from joint
    • Joint effusion: T2 bright fluid + thickened T2 bright synovium = septic arthritis
    • Soft tissue edema: diffuse (cellulitis) vs. compartmentalized (early abscess)
    • Fascial plane edema: compartment-by-compartment spread; multi-compartmental = necrotizing fasciitis pattern
    • Sinus tract: T2 bright linear channel through soft tissues to bone
    • Tenosynovitis in tendon sheaths; flexor tendons in diabetic foot
  • 4
    T1 — Marrow Signal (Critical for Osteomyelitis)
    • Normal marrow: T1 bright fat throughout
    • Osteomyelitis: focal T1 dark marrow replacement — most sensitive criterion (requires STIR correlation)
    • Extent: metaphysis, diaphysis, epiphysis
    • Cortical disruption: loss of hypointense cortical line
    • Sequestrum: T1 dark devascularized bone fragment
    • Involucrum: reactive new bone around sequestrum
  • 5
    T1 Fat-Sat Post-Contrast — Abscess vs. Phlegmon
    • Abscess: rim-enhancing fluid collection — thick irregular wall, non-enhancing T2 bright center
    • Phlegmon: diffuse heterogeneous enhancement without discrete fluid collection
    • Subperiosteal abscess: rim-enhancing between periosteum and cortex
    • Brodie's abscess: round T2 bright, rim enhancement, Penumbra sign (inner T1 bright granulation + outer dark sclerosis)
    • Sinus tract: enhancing tract from bone to skin
    • Necrotizing fasciitis: minimal fascial enhancement (ischemic fascia) + gas on CT
    • Septic arthritis: thick enhancing synovium, cartilage destruction
  • 6
    DWI / ADC (if performed)
    • Abscess: restricted diffusion (high cellularity, viscous pus)
    • Phlegmon: less restricted than abscess
    • Epidural abscess (spine): restricted diffusion of epidural collection
  • 7
    Hardware Assessment (if present)
    • Metal artifact reduction (MARS/MAVRIC/SEMAC) if available
    • Periimplant T2 halo: expected vs. pathologic (thickened)
    • Loosening vs. infection: loosening = T1 dark interface >2 mm; infection = periimplant enhancement + soft tissue involvement
    • Sinus tracts to hardware surface
  • 8
    Anatomic Extent & Surgical Planning
    • Compartmental involvement: which compartment? (surgical drainage)
    • Neurovascular proximity: artery, vein, nerve within infected zone
    • Proximal extent for debridement planning
    • Joint space involvement (septic arthritis — cartilage destruction)
    • Bone viability: dead bone = no enhancement (sequestrum)
  • 9
    Extratarget
    • Adjacent joint involvement
    • Proximal regional lymph nodes
    • Vascular structures: arteritis, pseudoaneurysm
  • 10
    Last Checks
    • Multi-compartment = necrotizing fasciitis? (surgical emergency)
    • Spine: epidural abscess extent and cord compression level
    • Diskitis-osteomyelitis: end plate enhancement + disk enhancement + STIR bright = classic triad
    • Compare with prior MRI for response to treatment
