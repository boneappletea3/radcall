Fluoroscopy Procedure Guide
Step-by-step protocols for GI/GU and special fluoroscopic examinations
Reference & Educational Use Only. Always correlate with institutional protocols and attending guidance. Patient safety and clinical judgment take precedence over any reference document.

Equipment
Thin barium (60% w/v) · Thick barium (250% w/v) · 12.5 mm barium tablet · Cup and straw · Scout capability
Step-by-Step Protocol
  1. Review history, prior imaging, operative reports. Know what you're looking for before patient enters.
  2. Obtain PA chest radiograph scout before any contrast.
  3. Explain procedure; remove artifacts from field (jewelry, ECG pads, monitoring leads).
  4. Position upright in LPO (~30°) — obliques esophagus off the spine for best visualization.
  5. Thick barium (250% w/v): 3 spot images during continuous swallowing — proximal cervical, mid-thoracic, distal thoracic. Time shots to active swallowing (esophagus should be distended).
  6. Right lateral supine: thin barium (60% w/v). Multiple sips. Image proximal, mid, and distal esophagus including open LES. Capturing the open LES is mandatory.
  7. RPO reflux evaluation: contrast pools at the cardia. Provocative maneuvers — Valsalva, cough, water siphon test (2 sips water, watch GEJ real-time). Document level and clearance time.
  8. Pharynx standing: gargle thick barium. AP — say "EEEEE," puff cheeks. Lateral — repeat. If abnormal pharynx: cine at 3 fr/sec.
  9. Barium tablet (12.5 mm): administer, document passage. If it catches: have patient drink water. Strictures <13 mm are typically symptomatic.
  10. Left lateral decubitus: demonstrates hiatal hernia and gastric cardia anatomy.
  11. Review with attending; repeat incomplete views before releasing patient.

Imaging Pearls
⚡ Always capture the open LES. Missed distal esophageal strictures and Schatzki rings are the most common error in esophagrams.
⚡ Stricture persistence: if narrowing is seen, have patient keep drinking to confirm it persists vs. a transient peristaltic wave.
⚡ Schatzki ring (B-ring): mucosal ring ≥2 cm above diaphragm. B-ring <13 mm typically symptomatic. A-ring = muscular (dynamic/normal); B-ring = mucosal (pathologic).
⚡ NEVER use Gastrografin (high-osmolar ionic) if any aspiration risk — causes severe pulmonary edema/chemical pneumonitis. Use Omnipaque (low-osmolar IOCM) orally.
⚡ Water siphon test: 2 sips water immediately after contrast — most sensitive provocative maneuver for reflux.

No hiatal hernia or gastroesophageal reflux is identified.
Pediatric Esophagram — Adapted technique for neonates, infants, and children. Radiation minimization and age-appropriate positioning are essential.
Pediatric Indications
GERD / recurrent vomiting · Suspected vascular ring (dysphagia lusoria) · TEF post-repair surveillance · Foreign body evaluation · Aspiration workup · Malrotation screening (DJJ assessment)
Key Technique Differences
🟣 Fluoroscopy grabs and last-image-hold ONLY — no full-exposure spot films. Minimizes dose significantly.
🟣 No double contrast (no effervescent granules) — thin barium only.
🟣 No barium tablet — not used in pediatric patients.
🟣 No pharyngeal exam unless specifically requested — separate MBS with speech pathology if swallowing evaluation needed.
Equipment
Thin barium (60% w/v) · Age-appropriate cup or bottle nipple for infants · NG tube if infant cannot swallow cooperatively · Parent/guardian in room with lead apron
Step-by-Step Protocol (Pediatric)
  1. Have parent/guardian remain in room; provide lead apron. Children cooperate better with a familiar adult present.
  2. Position:
    • Infants: supine or upright held by parent
    • Toddlers/children: upright standing or sitting preferred
  3. Lateral view first — the lateral projection is most sensitive for detecting reflux and posterior esophageal impressions (vascular ring).
  4. Administer thin barium via bottle (infants) or cup. Watch barium pass from pharynx through esophagus to stomach using fluoroscopy grabs/last-image-hold.
  5. CRITICAL — Document the DJJ: Normal = at or LEFT of spine, at the level of the pylorus. If DJJ is to the RIGHT of the spine or below the pylorus → malrotation until proven otherwise.
  6. Assess for GEJ reflux: in infants, gentle leg-raise or Trendelenburg position can provoke reflux if clinically relevant.
  7. AP and lateral spot grabs of any areas of concern. Minimize fluoroscopy time throughout.
Pediatric Imaging Pearls
⚡ Vascular ring: look for posterior esophageal impression on the LATERAL view. Bilateral impressions (anterior + posterior) = double aortic arch. Posterior impression only = aberrant right subclavian artery.
⚡ H-type TEF: inject contrast through NG tube at low pressure with patient lateral. The fistula fills only under gentle injection pressure. Have a low threshold to repeat in various obliquities — H-type TEFs are easily missed.
⚡ Physiologic reflux in infants is common — focus on the height of reflux (above carina = aspiration risk), frequency, and gastric emptying time.
⚡ DJJ is the most important landmark in pediatric UGI/esophagram. Never end the exam without documenting where the duodenum crosses the midline. Midgut volvulus is a surgical emergency.

Mucosal Disease Barrett's Esophagus Early Carcinoma Esophagitis Varices Webs Effervescent granules + dense barium 250% w/v NPO 2+ hours; NO WATER after granules
Contraindications
AVOIDBarium if perforation suspected — use water-soluble contrast

Equipment
Effervescent granules (1 packet) · Dense barium 250% w/v · Cold water to wash down granules · Cup
Step-by-Step Protocol
  1. Review history; identify the specific clinical question (mucosal disease evaluation vs. motility).
  2. PA chest scout.
  3. Give effervescent granules first — patient takes them dry or with the very smallest sip of water. Instruct patient NOT to burp — this is the most important instruction.
  4. Immediately give small amount of dense barium (2–3 oz) — patient drinks quickly.
  5. Position patient upright in LPO — get images immediately while CO2 is still distending the esophagus and barium is coating the mucosa.
  6. Mucosal coating images (upright LPO): 3 spot images — proximal, mid, distal esophagus. The esophageal mucosa should appear as a fine, uniform white coat. Look for plaques, erosions, nodularity.
  7. Add single contrast component: have patient take additional thick barium swallows. LPO standing × 3 (proximal, mid, distal) during continuous swallowing.
  8. Right lateral supine: thin barium, multiple sips, document open LES.
  9. RPO reflux evaluation with provocative maneuvers.
  10. Pharynx evaluation if indicated.

Imaging Pearls
⚡ CO2 distension is temporary — work fast. Get mucosal coating images within 60 seconds of granules.
⚡ True double contrast = air + barium simultaneously. If patient burps, you've lost it — the study becomes single contrast.
⚡ Candida esophagitis: discrete plaques (parallel white lines on en-face view — "shaggy" esophagus in severe cases).
⚡ Barrett's esophagus: reticular mucosal pattern in distal esophagus. Sensitivity is limited — endoscopy is gold standard.
⚡ Varices: best seen on mucosal relief images — longitudinal, serpiginous, "worm-like" filling defects that change with respiration and position.

Esophagram — Suspected Perforation GI
Esophageal Perforation Anastomotic Leak Post-Procedure Evaluation Boerhaave Syndrome Water-soluble ONLY — Omnipaque 240–300 preferred NPO; IV access; may need emergent evaluation
Contraindications
NEVERBarium if perforation suspected — barium mediastinitis/peritonitis has >50% mortality
NEVERGastrografin (high-osmolar ionic) if ANY aspiration risk — causes fatal chemical pneumonitis. Acceptable ONLY if perforation is strongly suspected AND aspiration risk is negligible
Equipment
Omnipaque 240–300 (preferred) or Gastrografin (only if zero aspiration risk) · NG tube (if patient cannot swallow) · Large-bore IV access · Surgical/thoracic team alerted before starting
Step-by-Step Protocol
  1. Alert the attending and surgical team BEFORE beginning — esophageal perforation is a surgical emergency. Do not work this up solo.
  2. Review clinical context: timing (Boerhaave = sudden violent vomiting; iatrogenic = post-endoscopy/dilation), location of pain, CXR findings (pneumomediastinum, pleural effusion, subcutaneous emphysema).
  3. PA and lateral chest fluoroscopy scout — document any pneumomediastinum, pleural effusion, air tracking.
  4. Contrast selection:
    • If aspiration risk present: Omnipaque 240–300 orally — low osmolar, non-ionic, safe if aspirated
    • If no aspiration risk and perforation strongly suspected: Gastrografin is acceptable
    • In most cases: default to Omnipaque — safer in all situations
  5. Patient position: upright if able; right lateral decubitus is also excellent (contrast pools dependently, enhancing detection).
  6. Give small test sip of contrast first — observe under continuous fluoroscopy as contrast enters the pharynx and proximal esophagus.
  7. Lateral chest view is critical — perforation site often seen best on lateral (posterior mediastinal extravasation, pleural fistula).
  8. Have patient take several swallows, image at each level:
    • Pharynx/cervical esophagus: AP and lateral
    • Mid-thoracic esophagus: AP and lateral
    • GEJ/distal: AP and lateral; RPO supine if patient can lie down
  9. If water-soluble study is NEGATIVE but clinical suspicion remains high: proceed with thin barium (more sensitive for small leaks — sensitivity >90%).
  10. Document: site of extravasation, extent of mediastinal contamination, communication with pleural space, size of leak.
🖼
Example: Esophageal perforationWater-soluble contrast extravasating from the left posterolateral esophagus at the GEJ level (classic Boerhaave location). Contrast tracks into the left pleural space. Leak best seen on the lateral view.
🖼
Example: Anastomotic leak post-esophagectomyLinear extravasation of water-soluble contrast from the esophagogastric anastomosis. Contrast pools in the mediastinum adjacent to the anastomosis. Correlate with CT chest for full extent.
Imaging Pearls
⚡ Negative water-soluble study does NOT exclude perforation — sensitivity is ~50–75%. If clinical suspicion persists, proceed with thin barium (sensitivity >90%).
⚡ Gastrografin in the lungs = bilateral pulmonary edema within minutes. Even a small aspiration is dangerous. Default to Omnipaque.
⚡ Lateral view catches more leaks than AP alone — posterior mediastinal extravasation is frequently missed on AP.
⚡ Right lateral decubitus is optimal for demonstrating GEJ perforation — contrast pools at the leak site.
⚡ Timing matters: small early leaks managed endoscopically (stent); large leaks or mediastinal contamination need surgery. Your report guides this decision.
⚡ Cervical vs. thoracic perforation: cervical (Zenker's, post-scope) = typically more contained, better prognosis. Thoracic (Boerhaave) = high mortality, urgent surgical consultation.
Normal Dictation Template
Esophagram for suspected perforation was performed using Omnipaque 300 water-soluble contrast. PA and lateral chest scout demonstrates no pneumomediastinum or pleural effusion. Sequential views of the pharynx, cervical, thoracic, and distal esophagus were obtained. No extravasation of contrast is identified. The esophageal lumen is intact without evidence of perforation, fistula, or anastomotic dehiscence.
Modified Barium Swallow (MBS)
GI — Esophageal
Indications
Dysphagia, aspiration, neurogenic swallowing dysfunction, post-stroke, post-surgical swallowing evaluation. Performed with speech-language pathologist (SLP) present.
Patient Prep
NPO 4 hours. SLP coordinates the study — they bring the patient, set up food/liquid textures, and direct timing of images. Your role is real-time fluoroscopic imaging and radiation dose management.
Contrast & Equipment
  • Varibar barium products (thin, nectar, honey, pudding) — or crush Varibar pudding into food items.
  • Chair at 90° upright or SLP-specified position. Lateral and AP views.
Protocol
  1. Lateral view first (primary view for MBS). Includes lips anteriorly to cervical spine posteriorly; skull base superiorly to below cricopharyngeus.
  2. SLP presents thin liquid (5 mL, then cup sip). Fluoro in pulse mode during each swallow. Document oral, pharyngeal, and esophageal phases.
  3. Nectar-thick liquid (5 mL, then cup sip) if thin liquid study is complete.
  4. Honey-thick liquid (5 mL) if indicated by SLP.
  5. Pudding consistency (teaspoon) — tests solid/semi-solid bolus transport.
  6. Cookie (Lorna Doone) coated with pudding barium — tests mastication and oral preparation.
  7. AP view if SLP wants to assess vallecular or piriform sinus asymmetry, or to evaluate vocal cord adduction.
  8. SLP may request compensatory strategies (chin tuck, head rotation, supraglottic swallow) — fluoro during these maneuvers.
🖼
Example: Normal lateral MBSSmooth bolus propulsion, complete epiglottic inversion, no laryngeal penetration or aspiration. Contrast clears valleculae and piriform sinuses after swallow.
🖼
Example: Silent aspirationContrast passes below true vocal cords without cough reflex. Identified only by fluoroscopy — clinically silent.
Imaging Pearls
⚡ Penetration vs. aspiration: Penetration = contrast enters laryngeal vestibule above vocal cords. Aspiration = contrast passes below true vocal cords into trachea.
⚡ Silent aspiration (no cough) is common post-stroke — fluoro is the only way to detect it.
⚡ Pulse fluoro: 15 fps or 7.5 fps. Record everything — SLP will review the video after.
⚡ FEES vs. MBS: FEES (endoscopic) done bedside, no radiation; MBS gives better pharyngeal/esophageal anatomy and radiation-free timing can be prolonged.
⚡ Radiation dose matters. This study is often repeated serially. Minimize fluoro time; pulse mode required.
Normal Dictation Template

Thin liquid, nectar-thick, honey-thick, and pudding consistencies were evaluated in the lateral and AP projections. The oral preparatory and oral transport phases are intact. Pharyngeal swallow is triggered promptly. Epiglottic inversion is complete. No laryngeal penetration or aspiration is identified with any consistency. The cricopharyngeus relaxes appropriately. No residue in valleculae or piriform sinuses after swallow. Esophageal inlet is patent.
Peds MBS is performed with a pediatric SLP. Neonates and infants are evaluated in feeding position (semi-reclined or upright). Use age-appropriate nipple/bottle or cup. Breast-milk consistency barium (Varibar thin) is standard for infants.
Age-Specific Notes
Neonates/Infants: Hold in caregiver arms or infant seat. Use Varibar thin in bottle with appropriate nipple flow rate. Do not exceed 3–5 swallows per consistency — radiation dose is paramount.

Toddlers (1–3y): Challenging cooperation. SLP leads. Cup sipping of thin/nectar. Cracker with pudding barium if developmentally appropriate.

Older children: Same as adult protocol adapted for cooperation level.
Key Findings in Peds
Laryngeal cleft (type I–IV), laryngomalacia, tracheoesophageal fistula (H-type), pharyngeal dysmotility, nasal regurgitation, vallecular residue. Premature infants may have disorganized suck-swallow-breathe coordination.

Upper GI Series — Single Contrast (SC)
GI — Stomach/Small Bowel
Indications
Suspected pyloric obstruction, gastric outlet obstruction, peptic ulcer disease (initial eval), post-operative anatomy check, hiatal hernia, GERD evaluation, suspected stricture/mass when EGD not available.
Patient Prep
NPO after midnight. No smoking or gum (stimulates gastric acid and motility). Remove metallic objects from field.
Protocol
  1. Scout film of abdomen.
  2. Patient drinks 100–200 mL of thin barium (low density, ~40–60% w/v) through a straw.
  3. Esophagus: LPO standing (3 images during continuous swallowing), right lateral, AP — same as SC esophagram.
  4. Stomach/duodenum upright: RAO standing — best view for gastric antrum and pylorus. PA erect. Right lateral — gastric body and fundus in profile.
  5. Have patient roll prone then supine to coat all mucosal surfaces. Spot images throughout.
  6. Supine RAO: duodenal bulb en face. Document duodenal sweep to ligament of Treitz.
  7. Assess for reflux: prone Trendelenburg with water siphon test or gentle abdominal compression.
  8. Additional images as needed for specific findings.
🖼
Example: Normal UGI SCSmooth gastric folds, intact antrum and pylorus, patent duodenal bulb. Barium flows freely to ligament of Treitz.
Imaging Pearls
⚡ RAO supine is the money view for the duodenal bulb — fills antrum well and opens the bulb en face.
⚡ Pyloric channel ulcer can mimic pyloric stenosis — scarring causes narrowing. Look for ulcer crater en face or in profile.
⚡ Hiatal hernia is best seen in the Trendelenburg position — gastric rugae above the diaphragm.
⚡ Document ligament of Treitz position (should be at or left of left pedicle at L1-L2 level) — malrotation workup.

The gastroesophageal junction is normally positioned below the diaphragm. No hiatal hernia. The gastric mucosa demonstrates normal fold pattern. The pylorus opens freely. The duodenal bulb and sweep are normal without filling defect or ulceration. Barium passes freely to the ligament of Treitz which is in normal position at the level of L1-2, left of midline. No evidence of malrotation.
Pediatric UGI is one of the most common fluoroscopic studies in children. Malrotation/volvulus is the primary concern in neonates and infants with bilious vomiting — this is a surgical emergency.
Age-Specific Protocol
Neonates/Infants: Omnipaque or dilute barium via bottle or NG. Lateral and AP views. Document esophagus, stomach, duodenal bulb, and ligament of Treitz (LoT) position.

Toddlers/Children: Same as adult single contrast. Use age-appropriate contrast volume.
Malrotation Protocol
  1. AP scout. Give small amount of contrast (10–20 mL for neonates; more for older children).
  2. AP view of duodenum: Document the duodenojejunal junction (DJ junction / ligament of Treitz). Should be at or left of left pedicle, at the level of the duodenal bulb (L1-2).
  3. Lateral view to confirm retroperitoneal position of 3rd portion of duodenum (passes posterior to SMA).
  4. If LoT is low or right of midline → malrotation. Urgent surgical consultation.
  5. "Corkscrew" duodenum = midgut volvulus — emergent.
If you see a "corkscrew" duodenum or the duodenum fails to cross midline — call the referring team immediately. Do not wait for the radiologist to read it.

Upper GI Series — Double Contrast (DC)
GI — Stomach/Small BowelAdult
Indications
Suspected gastric carcinoma, early gastric cancer screening (Japan), gastric lymphoma, subtle mucosal lesions not seen on SC study, peptic ulcer disease with prior negative SC, NSAID gastropathy.
Patient Prep
NPO after midnight. No anticholinergics (glucagon optional — 0.1 mg IV to reduce peristalsis). Effervescent granules + dense barium (250% w/v).
Protocol
  1. Scout film of abdomen.
  2. Give effervescent granules dry (or with 1 tsp water). Patient must not burp.
  3. Immediately give 60–90 mL dense barium (250% w/v). Patient drinks quickly.
  4. Roll patient to coat all mucosal surfaces: supine → right lateral → prone → left lateral → supine (full rotation).
  5. Supine RAO: body of stomach and antrum en face — best for detecting early gastric cancer, ulcers, rugal fold pattern.
  6. Supine LPO: fundus and cardia en face.
  7. Right lateral — posterior gastric wall profile.
  8. Add single contrast (additional barium) for duodenum and esophagus — same as SC UGI steps.
🖼
Example: Gastric ulcer on DC UGIEn-face view shows discrete collection of barium (ulcer crater) with smooth surrounding mound and radiating folds.
Imaging Pearls
⚡ Benign vs. malignant ulcer: Benign = smooth, round crater projecting beyond lumen, Hampton line, radiating folds tapered to edge. Malignant = crater within lumen, nodular folds, irregular margin.
⚡ Early gastric cancer (type IIc/III) — subtle depression or ulcer; DC technique critical for detection.
⚡ Linitis plastica: diffuse thickening, loss of distensibility, rigid "leather bottle" stomach — scirrhous carcinoma or metastasis (breast).
⚡ MALT lymphoma: thickened folds, multiple nodules, without the rigidity of carcinoma.

The gastric folds are of normal thickness and taper smoothly to the antrum. The pylorus opens freely. The duodenal bulb and sweep are normal. Small Bowel Follow-Through (SBFT)
GI — Stomach/Small BowelAdult
Indications
Crohn's disease evaluation/follow-up, small bowel obstruction (partial, chronic), malabsorption workup, suspected small bowel mass or stricture. Largely replaced by CT enterography/MR enterography but still used in some centers.
Patient Prep
Low-residue diet day before. NPO 8–12 hours. No laxatives unless specified. This is a time-intensive study (1–4 hours).
Protocol
  1. Scout film of abdomen.
  2. Patient drinks 450–600 mL of thin barium (40% w/v). Some centers add methylcellulose to improve mucosal coating.
  3. Serial overhead films at 15–30 minute intervals: 15 min, 30 min, 45 min, 60 min, then every 30–60 min until barium reaches terminal ileum/cecum.
  4. Fluoroscopic spot views of any area of concern (narrowing, separation, flocculation).
  5. Terminal ileum: fluoroscopic compression spot views in RPO and supine positions. This is the most important area — Crohn's, lymphoma, carcinoid.
  6. Document transit time from stomach to cecum (normal: 30–120 min).
  7. Overhead when barium reaches cecum for full-column image.
🖼
Example: Terminal ileum Crohn'sString sign (severe luminal narrowing due to spasm or fibrosis), cobblestone mucosa, skip lesions, prestenotic dilatation.
Imaging Pearls
⚡ String sign of Kantor — severe terminal ileal narrowing in Crohn's. May be reversible (spasm) or fixed (fibrosis).
⚡ Separation of bowel loops suggests mesenteric thickening — Crohn's, carcinoid desmoplastic reaction, lymphoma.
⚡ Flocculation/segmentation of barium — celiac disease, malabsorption. Diluted barium and dilated loops.
⚡ Jejunoileal fold pattern: Jejunum = 3–5 folds/cm ("stacked coins"). Ileum = fewer folds (1–2/cm), smooth.
⚡ Hypomotility: prolonged transit (>4 hours) suggests pseudo-obstruction, scleroderma, or hypothyroid.

GI — Colon / Rectum
Barium Enema — Single Contrast (SC)
GI — Colon/RectumAdult
Indications
Suspected large bowel obstruction, diverticulosis, volvulus, fistula assessment, colostomy evaluation, post-surgical anastomosis check (contrast enema). SC is preferred when evaluating obstruction or when patient cannot cooperate with DC prep.
Patient Prep
Full bowel prep (Miralax + bisacodyl or similar). Clear liquids day before. NPO 4 hours. No enemas day-of (removes residual coating). Warm the barium to body temperature.
Protocol
  1. Scout film of abdomen (supine ± prone).
  2. Place rectal tube with balloon inflated (30–60 mL). Confirm position below levators on lateral scout.
  3. Administer 20–25% w/v barium by gravity from 90 cm above table. Flow rate ~100–150 mL/min.
  4. Fluoroscopic spot views as colon fills: rectum, sigmoid, descending, splenic flexure, transverse, hepatic flexure, ascending, cecum.
  5. Overhead films: AP supine, PA prone, bilateral decubiti (for rectosigmoid), lateral rectum (for rectal masses/fistula).
  6. Document cecal filling and ileocecal valve.
  7. Post-evacuation film to assess mucosal pattern and completeness of evacuation.
🖼
Example: Sigmoid diverticulosis on SC BEMultiple flask-shaped outpouchings along antimesenteric border of sigmoid. Note smooth luminal contour between diverticula.
Imaging Pearls
⚡ Apple core lesion — carcinoma causing shouldered circumferential narrowing. Abrupt transition from normal to narrowed lumen.
⚡ Sigmoid volvulus: "bent inner tube" / coffee-bean sign on scout. SC enema may detorse it.
⚡ Diverticulitis: SC enema is CONTRAINDICATED in acute diverticulitis. Use CT.
⚡ Anastomotic leak: use water-soluble contrast (Omnipaque) — NOT barium — if leak is suspected.
Normal Dictation Template
Single contrast barium enema was performed. The rectum, sigmoid, descending, transverse, ascending colon, and cecum fill completely. No filling defects, strictures, or mucosal abnormality. Haustral pattern is normal throughout. The ileocecal valve is competent. Post-evacuation images confirm complete evacuation without significant residual. No evidence of obstruction or perforation.
Barium Enema — Double Contrast (DC)
GI — Colon/Rectum
Indications
Colorectal cancer screening (largely replaced by colonoscopy/CT colonography), polyp detection, inflammatory bowel disease evaluation (Crohn's colitis, UC), evaluation of mucosal lesions.
Patient Prep
Full bowel prep essential — any residual stool creates false-positive findings. Same prep as SC BE. Glucagon 0.1 mg IV optional to reduce colonic spasm (give just before air insufflation).
Protocol
  1. Scout film.
  2. Rectal tube with balloon (30–60 mL). Lateral scout to confirm position.
  3. Infuse 250–350 mL dense barium (80–100% w/v) slowly. Fill to splenic flexure only — do not fill entire colon with barium.
  4. Clamp tube. Roll patient (right lateral → prone → left lateral → supine) to coat all mucosal surfaces with barium.
  5. Drain barium back into bag (unclamp tube, tilt table head-up).
  6. Insufflate air (or CO2) via rectal tube — 1000–1500 mL — until colon fully distended. Fluoroscopic monitoring.
  7. Overhead films in standard projections: PA prone, AP supine, bilateral decubiti, bilateral obliques of rectosigmoid, lateral rectum.
  8. Spot views of any suspicious area.
🖼
Example: Sessile polyp on DC BEFilling defect coated by thin barium with "etched in white" ring on dependent wall. Compare air-contrast interface on non-dependent wall.
Imaging Pearls
⚡ "Etched in white" polyp — ring of barium around a sessile polyp on the dependent wall. On non-dependent wall, polyp projects as a filling defect into air column.
⚡ UC vs. Crohn's: UC — continuous from rectum, lead-pipe colon (late), loss of haustra. Crohn's — skip lesions, terminal ileum, transmural (fistulae, aphthous ulcers).
⚡ Pseudopolyps in UC — islands of inflamed mucosa between ulcers. Not true polyps.
⚡ Residual stool moves with patient repositioning; true polyps stay fixed. Always get two views perpendicular to each other.

Defecography (Fluoroscopic Proctography)
GI — Colon/RectumAdult
Indications
Obstructed defecation, rectal prolapse, rectocele, intussusception, anismus (pelvic floor dysfunction), post-surgical evaluation of rectal repair. MR defecography is increasingly preferred when available.
Patient Prep
Cleansing enema 2 hours before. Vaginal opacification with contrast (for females — to identify enterocele). Small bowel opacification optional (dilute oral barium 1–2 hours prior to show enterocele). Bladder opacification optional.
Protocol
  1. Patient seated on radiolucent commode on the fluoroscopic table.
  2. Instill 150–200 mL thick barium paste (potato starch + barium) per rectum via catheter.
  3. Lateral fluoroscopic images throughout (primary view — sagittal plane).
  4. Phases: rest, squeeze (maximum voluntary contraction), strain (Valsalva), defecation.
  5. Document: anorectal angle (ARA) at rest and squeeze. Normal ARA at rest = 90–110°. During squeeze = ARA decreases (puborectalis tightens). During defecation = ARA increases (>130°).
  6. Measure pelvic floor descent from pubococcygeal line at rest vs. straining. >3.5 cm descent = abnormal.
  7. Document rectocele (anterior rectal wall herniation) size and whether it empties.
  8. Document any rectal intussusception (infolding of rectal wall during evacuation) or complete prolapse.
Imaging Pearls
⚡ Anismus (non-relaxing puborectalis): ARA fails to open during defecation, narrow anorectal junction, incomplete evacuation. Paradoxical puborectalis contraction.
⚡ Rectocele: anterior herniation >2 cm is significant. Trapping of contrast = functional impairment. Most rectoceles are asymptomatic.
⚡ Enterocele: small bowel loops herniate between vagina and rectum during straining — opacified with oral contrast.
⚡ This is an inherently uncomfortable and potentially embarrassing study. Explain to patient beforehand that they should defecate normally regardless of how it seems.

Air Enema (Intussusception Reduction)
GI — Colon/RectumPeds
Indications
Ileocolic intussusception (most common cause: idiopathic in ages 6 mo–3 yr). Also used for reduction of colonic intussusception. Air is the preferred reduction medium at most centers (vs. barium or water-soluble contrast).
Contraindications
Free air on scout (perforation) — absolute contraindication. Peritonitis, hemodynamic instability, complete small bowel obstruction, obvious lead point (Meckel's, lymphoma) — relative contraindications. Always confirm with Surgery before starting.
Pre-Procedure
Surgical team must be on standby or immediately available. IV access and resuscitation equipment present. Confirm patient hemodynamically stable. Get informed consent. Scout film to exclude free air and confirm intussusception location.
Protocol
  1. Scout film of abdomen — exclude free air; look for soft tissue mass in RUQ/transverse colon; confirm absence of small bowel obstruction.
  2. Place rectal catheter (Foley 16–18 Fr). Inflate balloon (30–60 mL). Tape buttocks together to minimize air leak.
  3. Begin air insufflation with pressure-controlled device. Maximum pressure: 120 mmHg (most centers use 80–100 mmHg initially).
  4. Fluoroscopic monitoring — watch intussusceptum (soft tissue mass) being pushed retrograde toward ileocecal valve.
  5. Reduction endpoint: sudden rush of air into terminal ileum with disappearance of soft tissue mass. Reflux of air into terminal ileum confirms complete reduction.
  6. Allow up to 3 attempts (1–3 min each). Brief intervals between attempts allow bowel wall to rest.
  7. Post-reduction: deflate balloon, remove catheter. Post-reduction scout film.
  8. If reduction fails after 3 attempts → surgical reduction.
Imaging Pearls
⚡ Success rate: ~80–90% for ileocolic intussusception. Duration of symptoms <24h correlates with higher success.
⚡ Pneumoperitoneum during enema = perforation → stop immediately, call surgery, upright or decubitus film to confirm.
⚡ Air into terminal ileum = success. Without TI reflux, reduction may be incomplete — intussusception can reform.
⚡ Recurrence rate: ~10% within 24–48 hrs. Parents should be counseled to return with recurrent symptoms.
⚡ "Dancing" mass — intussusception moving during insufflation = good sign of reducibility.
Successful Reduction Dictation

Air enema was performed with maximum pressure of [X] mmHg. Successful reduction was achieved after [1/2/3] attempt(s), confirmed by reflux of air into the terminal ileum and disappearance of the soft tissue mass. No pneumoperitoneum on post-reduction film. Findings consistent with complete reduction.

Indications

Standard Whipple (PD): resection of head of pancreas, duodenum, distal common bile duct, gallbladder, ± distal stomach. Reconstruction: pancreaticojejunostomy → hepaticojejunostomy → gastrojejunostomy (all on same jejunal limb, classic Child reconstruction).

  5. Erect and supine overhead films to document anatomy.
Imaging Pearls
⚡ Delayed gastric emptying (DGE) — most common complication. Gastric remnant distended, poor emptying through gastrojejunostomy. May persist weeks. Managed conservatively.
⚡ Afferent limb obstruction — biliopancreatic limb obstruction causes RUQ pain, jaundice, amylase elevation. Fluoro may show dilated afferent limb; CT more useful.
⚡ Marginal ulcer — peptic ulceration at gastrojejunostomy. Look for crater at anastomosis.

GI — Post-SurgicalAdult
Indications
Post-RYGB complications: anastomotic leak (gastrojejunostomy or jejunojejunostomy), stomal stenosis/stricture (most common complication requiring dilation), marginal ulcer, internal hernia, band erosion.
Anatomy After RYGB
Gastric pouch (small — 15–30 mL) at top. Gastrojejunostomy (GJ) — pouch connects to Roux limb (alimentary limb). Jejunojejunostomy (JJ) — Roux limb reconnects to biliopancreatic limb ~75–150 cm distal.

Excluded stomach, duodenum, and proximal jejunum (biliopancreatic limb) are NOT opacified by oral contrast on UGI.
Protocol
  1. Scout film. Note band/staple line location.
  2. Water-soluble contrast first (suspected leak). Patient drinks 60–100 mL. Upright position.
  3. Fluoroscopic spot views: esophagus, gastric pouch, GJ anastomosis, proximal Roux limb.
  4. Document GJ anastomosis caliber (<10 mm = stricture). Stomal stenosis is the #1 reason for UGI post-RYGB.
  5. Follow contrast into Roux limb distally. Note JJ anastomosis if visualized.
  6. If no leak on water-soluble: add thin barium for better mucosal detail.
Imaging Pearls
⚡ Stomal stenosis: GJ anastomosis <10 mm. Patient presents with dysphagia and vomiting. Treated with endoscopic balloon dilation.
⚡ GJ leak: extravasation at anastomosis — surgical emergency post-operatively. Late leaks may be contained.
⚡ Marginal ulcer: ulcer at or just distal to GJ anastomosis. May cause GJ fistula (connect to excluded stomach or duodenum).
⚡ Internal hernia: NOT visible on fluoro. If suspected → CT. Intermittent abdominal pain post-RYGB = think internal hernia (Petersen's, trans-mesenteric, JJ mesenteric defect).

Voiding Cystourethrogram (VCUG)
GU
Indications
Stress urinary incontinence, urethral stricture, post-trauma urethral evaluation, bladder outlet obstruction, neurogenic bladder, vesicoureteral reflux (VUR) in adults, post-operative bladder evaluation, urethral diverticulum.
Patient Prep
Verify no active UTI (check UA/culture). Confirm pregnancy status in women of childbearing age. Explain procedure — patient will need to void on the table. Confirm consent.
Protocol
  1. Scout film of pelvis.
  2. Place urethral catheter (14–16 Fr Foley). Drain residual urine — document volume.
  3. Instill dilute contrast (Omnipaque 300 diluted 1:3 in saline) by gravity from 60–80 cm above table. Fill to capacity or patient urge.
  4. Filling phase spots: AP pelvis (early, mid, full), oblique views for ureteral orifices (VUR), lateral for posterior bladder wall (cystocele).
  5. Remove catheter when bladder is full.
  6. Voiding phase (critical): Patient voids in oblique position (males) or lateral/oblique (females). Continuous fluoroscopy during voiding. Document urethra in its entirety.
  7. Post-void film — assess residual, any late VUR.
Imaging Pearls
⚡ Male urethra oblique: right posterior oblique to open the entire urethra (prostatic, membranous, bulbous, penile).
⚡ VUR grading: Grade I = ureter only. II = ureter + renal pelvis, no dilation. III = mild dilation. IV = moderate dilation + blunted calyces. V = severe, tortuous.
⚡ Posterior urethral valves (PUV): males — dilated posterior urethra, abrupt cutoff at verumontanum, trabeculated bladder. This is a VCUG diagnosis.
⚡ Bladder diverticulum: smooth outpouching. May retain contrast post-void.

Pediatric VCUG is primarily performed to evaluate vesicoureteral reflux (VUR) after febrile UTI and to diagnose posterior urethral valves in male infants. Radiation minimization is critical — cyclic VCUG or contrast-enhanced voiding urosonography (ceVUS) may be preferred at some centers.
Indications
Febrile UTI (first or recurrent) per AAP guidelines, suspected posterior urethral valves, ureterocele, hydronephrosis evaluation, renal duplication.
Protocol Differences
Catheter size: 6–8 Fr for infants, 8–10 Fr for toddlers, 10–12 Fr for older children.

Fill volume: estimated capacity = (age in years + 2) × 30 mL. Do not overfill.

Cyclic VCUG: fill → void → refill → void again to increase VUR detection sensitivity.

Pulse fluoro mode. Minimize images to the voiding phase only when possible. Do not image continuously during filling.
VUR Management Context
Low-grade VUR (I–II) in young children often resolves spontaneously. High-grade (IV–V) or bilateral: nephrology/urology referral. Dilating VUR (III–V) = higher risk for renal scarring.

GUAdult / Male
Indications
Suspected urethral stricture, post-traumatic urethral injury (pelvic fracture), urethral disruption, urethral diverticulum in males, failed urethral catheterization, evaluation prior to urethroplasty.
Patient Prep
No specific prep. Verify no active UTI. Patient supine, then oblique (right posterior oblique, 30–45°) for voiding urethrogram positioning. Penile clamp or Brodney clamp needed.
Protocol
  1. Scout film of pelvis.
  2. Patient positioned in right posterior oblique (RPO) ~30–45° — this profiles the entire male urethra in one plane.
  3. Place a 12–14 Fr Foley catheter tip at the fossa navicularis (just inside the meatus). Inflate balloon only 1–2 mL (just enough to hold — do not fully inflate in anterior urethra).

  5. Spot images: oblique urethra (entire length), bladder base. AP pelvis if needed.
  6. Document: stricture location, length, caliber, extravasation, fistula.
Imaging Pearls
⚡ Anterior urethra: penile + bulbous urethra (ventral, below perineal membrane). Posterior urethra: membranous + prostatic (above/at perineal membrane).
⚡ Bulbous urethra is the most common site for post-inflammatory stricture (STI-related, especially gonorrhea).
⚡ Membranous urethra injury — pelvic fracture urethral injury (PFUI). RUG shows disruption at membranous urethra with extravasation.
⚡ Stricture vs. spasm: Spasm at external sphincter may mimic stricture. Complete the injection and assess contrast transit — true stricture shows abrupt narrowing with prestenotic dilation.

GUAdult / Female
Indications
Infertility evaluation (tubal patency), recurrent pregnancy loss (uterine anomaly), uterine synechiae (Asherman's syndrome), müllerian duct anomaly characterization, tubal ligation confirmation, post-myomectomy evaluation.
Patient Prep
Schedule days 7–10 of menstrual cycle (post-menses, pre-ovulation). Confirm not pregnant. Pre-medicate with ibuprofen 600 mg PO 1 hour prior. Prophylactic doxycycline 100 mg BID × 5 days if tubes are blocked (reduces risk of pelvic infection). Pelvic exam/speculum required — nursing/tech assist needed.
Protocol
  1. Scout AP pelvis.
  2. Patient in lithotomy position. Speculum placed. Cervix visualized and cleaned.
  3. HSG catheter (balloon or acorn-tip) placed in cervical os. Balloon inflated 1.5–2 mL in lower uterine segment — do not over-inflate.

  5. Continue injection (total 10–15 mL) — document bilateral tubal filling and peritoneal spillage.
  6. Oblique views if tubal anatomy unclear. Spot images in multiple projections.

⚡ Peritoneal spillage = tubal patency. Free spillage bilaterally = both tubes open.
⚡ Synechiae (Asherman's): irregular filling defects within uterine cavity, linear or irregular.
⚡ Submucosal fibroids: smooth filling defects indenting the endometrial cavity.

  5. If injecting corticosteroid after aspiration: confirm no clinical concern for infection first. Use separate needle or flush needle. Inject methylprednisolone 40–80 mg ± 0.5–1% lidocaine.
  6. Post-procedure: apply bandage. Instruct patient to avoid strenuous activity for 24–48 hours post-injection.

Myelogram (Fluoroscopic)
Neuro/MSKAdult
Indications
CSF leak / intracranial hypotension localization, post-surgical spinal evaluation (metallic hardware degrading MRI), radiculopathy/myelopathy when MRI is contraindicated (pacemaker), arachnoiditis, nerve root avulsion, CT myelogram for higher resolution than MRI.
Contraindications
Infection at puncture site. Coagulopathy (INR >1.5, platelets <50K). Increased intracranial pressure (papilledema). Allergy to contrast. Avoid Omnipaque at cisterna magna if seizure disorder (cervical myelogram). Hold NSAIDs 5 days, anticoagulants per protocol before procedure.
Protocol (Lumbar Myelogram)
  1. Prone on fluoroscopic table. Scout film of lumbar spine.
  2. Sterile prep and drape. Local anesthetic (1% lidocaine) at puncture site (L2-3 or L3-4 preferred — avoid L4-5 in degenerative disease).
  3. Advance 22-gauge spinal needle under lateral fluoroscopy to thecal sac. Confirm intrathecal position: free CSF flow, contrast mixes freely with CSF.
  4. Collect CSF sample (2–5 mL) if clinically indicated.
  5. Inject Omnipaque 300 (8–12 mL for lumbar; 6–8 mL for cervical via lumbar approach). Injection must be intrathecal — NO epidural contrast.
  6. Tilt table to distribute contrast. Fluoroscopic spot images: lumbar, thoracic, and cervical (as indicated). Obliques for nerve root sleeves.
  7. Transfer directly to CT for CT myelogram (axial and sagittal reconstructions).
  8. Patient remains at 30° head elevation for 4–6 hours post-procedure (reduces headache).
Imaging Pearls
⚡ Epidural vs. intrathecal: intrathecal = contrast mixes with CSF immediately, nerve roots visible within contrast column. Epidural = contrast outlines dural sac from outside — do NOT inject myelographic dose epidurally.
⚡ Nerve root cut-off: disc herniation or stenosis compresses/effaces nerve root sleeves — seen as blunted or absent filling of root sleeve on oblique views.
⚡ Post-myelogram headache (PDPH): positional, worse upright, improves supine. Managed with hydration, caffeine, analgesics. Epidural blood patch if persistent.
⚡ CSF leak (intracranial hypotension): fluoroscopic myelogram with positional imaging or CT myelogram can identify extradural contrast extravasation at the leak site.
⚡ Arachnoiditis: clumping of nerve roots ("empty thecal sac" — roots clumped peripherally), no individual root sleeves visible.

Sniff Test (Diaphragmatic Fluoroscopy)
Special ProceduresAdult
Indications
Suspected hemidiaphragm paralysis or paresis, elevated hemidiaphragm on chest X-ray, phrenic nerve palsy (post-cardiac surgery, cervical spine disease, tumor, trauma), unexplained dyspnea, pre-operative phrenic nerve assessment.
Patient Prep
No prep required. Upright PA chest fluoro preferred. If bedside: AP supine (less sensitive). Explain the sniff maneuver to the patient before starting.
Protocol
  1. Position patient upright (PA) or supine under fluoroscope. Include both hemidiaphragms in the field.
  2. Observe diaphragm motion with quiet breathing first. Document baseline position and symmetric motion.
  3. Instruct patient to perform a sharp, forceful sniff (rapid nasal inspiration). Observe both hemidiaphragms simultaneously.
  4. Normal: both hemidiaphragms descend symmetrically with sniff.
  5. Paradoxical motion: affected hemidiaphragm rises (ascends) during sniff — characteristic of phrenic nerve palsy. The negative intrathoracic pressure is generated by the contralateral normal diaphragm, causing the flaccid side to be sucked up.
  6. Record fluoroscopic video clip (minimum 3–5 sniffs). Document in multiple positions if equivocal.
  7. Measure diaphragmatic excursion if quantification requested (normal: >2 cm).
Imaging Pearls
⚡ Paradoxical motion is diagnostic — the paralyzed diaphragm rises on sniff (moves opposite to normal). This is pathognomonic of phrenic nerve palsy when unilateral.
⚡ Bilateral paralysis is harder to detect — both diaphragms may show reduced or absent motion. Suspect with severe unexplained dyspnea, especially post-cardiac surgery (ice slush phrenic injury).
⚡ False negative: partial paralysis (paresis) may show minimal paradox. Always compare to the contralateral side — asymmetric excursion is the key.
⚡ Eventration vs. paralysis: eventration = permanent elevation with reduced but not paradoxical motion. Paralysis = paradoxical motion on sniff. Clinically similar; management differs.
⚡ Sensitivity ~90% for unilateral phrenic palsy when performed upright.
Normal Sniff Test Dictation

During sharp sniff maneuvers, both hemidiaphragms descend symmetrically without paradoxical motion. No evidence of hemidiaphragm paralysis or paresis. Diaphragmatic excursion is symmetric and within normal limits bilaterally.
Dobhoff / Nasoenteric Tube Placement (Fluoroscopic)
Special ProceduresAdult
Indications
Failed bedside NG/nasoduodenal tube placement, gastroparesis requiring post-pyloric feeding, aspiration risk requiring duodenal/jejunal feeding, altered anatomy (post-surgical), patient intolerance of bedside placement, tube malposition confirmation and correction.
Patient Prep
Confirm tube type (NG vs. nasoduodenal vs. nasojejunal — length matters). Have tube at bedside (Dobhoff 8 or 10 Fr, with stylet). Lubricate tube. Position patient if possible (semi-upright preferred, or RAO for better pyloric view). Glucagon 0.5–1 mg IV optional to relax pylorus and facilitate post-pyloric passage.
Protocol
  1. Scout AP abdomen/chest.
  2. Advance tube nasally (or orally if nasal access failed) while fluoroscoping. Watch tube course through oropharynx, esophagus, into stomach.
  3. Coil tube in gastric body. Position patient RAO or right lateral decubitus to pool contrast/air at pylorus.
  4. Inject 10–20 mL Omnipaque 300 through tube to opacify stomach and identify pylorus. Advance tube tip to pylorus under fluoroscopic guidance.
  5. Guide tube through pylorus with gentle torque and patient positioning. This is the technically challenging step — patience and tube manipulation required.
  6. Advance to desired position: duodenal bulb (D1) for nasoduodenal, ligament of Treitz or beyond for nasojejunal.
  7. Remove stylet. Confirm final position with spot image (AP abdomen, label tube tip level).
  8. Secure tube at nose. Document tube tip position in report.
Imaging Pearls
⚡ Glucagon trick: 0.5 mg IV glucagon relaxes the pylorus and often allows the tube to pass with minimal manipulation. Particularly helpful in gastroparesis.
⚡ RAO position pools gastric contents at the pylorus — gravity assists tube passage. Right lateral decubitus is an alternative.
⚡ Tube coiling in stomach — always straighten tube by withdrawing before attempting pyloric passage. A coiled tube has no directionality.
⚡ Post-pyloric position: tube should cross midline to right, then curve inferiorly (duodenal C-loop), then cross back left toward ligament of Treitz for post-pyloric feeding.
⚡ Nasojejunal vs. nasoduodenal: jejunal (past ligament of Treitz) is preferred for high aspiration risk — further from pylorus and reflux-prone proximal stomach.
