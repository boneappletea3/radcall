Fluoroscopy Procedure Guide
Step-by-step protocols for GI/GU and special fluoroscopic examinations
GI — Esophageal
Esophagram (Single Contrast) GI
Dysphagia Odynophagia Motility Disorders Post-op Follow-up Globus Thin barium 60% w/v + thick barium 250% w/v NPO 2+ hours
Contraindications
AVOIDBarium if perforation suspected — use water-soluble contrast instead
NEVERHigh-osmolar ionic contrast (Gastrografin) if aspiration risk — causes severe pulmonary edema/chemical pneumonitis. Use Omnipaque (low-osmolar non-ionic) orally instead
Equipment
Thin barium (60% w/v) · Thick barium (250% w/v) · 12.5 mm barium tablet · Cup and straw · Scout capability
Step-by-Step Protocol
  1. Review history, prior imaging, operative reports. Know what you're looking for before patient enters.
  2. Obtain PA chest radiograph scout before any contrast.
  3. Explain procedure; remove artifacts from field (jewelry, ECG pads, monitoring leads).
  4. Position upright in LPO (~30°) — obliques esophagus off the spine for best visualization.
  5. Thick barium (250% w/v): 3 spot images during continuous swallowing — proximal cervical, mid-thoracic, distal thoracic. Time shots to active swallowing (esophagus should be distended).
  6. Right lateral supine: thin barium (60% w/v). Multiple sips. Image proximal, mid, and distal esophagus including open LES. Capturing the open LES is mandatory.
  7. RPO reflux evaluation: contrast pools at the cardia. Provocative maneuvers — Valsalva, cough, water siphon test (2 sips water, watch GEJ real-time). Document level and clearance time.
  8. Pharynx standing: gargle thick barium. AP — say "EEEEE," puff cheeks. Lateral — repeat. If abnormal pharynx: cine at 3 fr/sec.
  9. Barium tablet (12.5 mm): administer, document passage. If it catches: have patient drink water. Strictures <13 mm are typically symptomatic.
  10. Left lateral decubitus: demonstrates hiatal hernia and gastric cardia anatomy.
  11. Review with attending; repeat incomplete views before releasing patient.

Imaging Pearls
⚡ Always capture the open LES. Missed distal esophageal strictures and Schatzki rings are the most common error in esophagrams.
⚡ Stricture persistence: if narrowing is seen, have patient keep drinking to confirm it persists vs. a transient peristaltic wave.
⚡ Schatzki ring (B-ring): mucosal ring ≥2 cm above diaphragm. B-ring <13 mm typically symptomatic. A-ring = muscular (dynamic/normal); B-ring = mucosal (pathologic).
⚡ NEVER use Gastrografin (high-osmolar ionic) if any aspiration risk — causes severe pulmonary edema/chemical pneumonitis. Use Omnipaque (low-osmolar IOCM) orally.
⚡ Water siphon test: 2 sips water immediately after contrast — most sensitive provocative maneuver for reflux.

The lower esophageal sphincter opens normally. The pharynx is unremarkable without pooling or aspiration. A 12.5 mm barium tablet passed freely without obstruction. No hiatal hernia or gastroesophageal reflux is identified.
Pediatric Esophagram — Adapted technique for neonates, infants, and children. Radiation minimization and age-appropriate positioning are essential.
Pediatric Indications
GERD / recurrent vomiting · Suspected vascular ring (dysphagia lusoria) · TEF post-repair surveillance · Foreign body evaluation · Aspiration workup · Malrotation screening (DJJ assessment)
Key Technique Differences
🟣 Fluoroscopy grabs and last-image-hold ONLY — no full-exposure spot films. Minimizes dose significantly.
🟣 No double contrast (no effervescent granules) — thin barium only.
🟣 No barium tablet — not used in pediatric patients.
🟣 No pharyngeal exam unless specifically requested — separate MBS with speech pathology if swallowing evaluation needed.
Equipment
Thin barium (60% w/v) · Age-appropriate cup or bottle nipple for infants · NG tube if infant cannot swallow cooperatively · Parent/guardian in room with lead apron
Step-by-Step Protocol (Pediatric)
  1. Have parent/guardian remain in room; provide lead apron. Children cooperate better with a familiar adult present.
  2. Position:
    • Infants: supine or upright held by parent
    • Toddlers/children: upright standing or sitting preferred
  3. Lateral view first — the lateral projection is most sensitive for detecting reflux and posterior esophageal impressions (vascular ring).
  4. Administer thin barium via bottle (infants) or cup. Watch barium pass from pharynx through esophagus to stomach using fluoroscopy grabs/last-image-hold.
  5. CRITICAL — Document the DJJ: Normal = at or LEFT of spine, at the level of the pylorus. If DJJ is to the RIGHT of the spine or below the pylorus → malrotation until proven otherwise.
  6. Assess for GEJ reflux: in infants, gentle leg-raise or Trendelenburg position can provoke reflux if clinically relevant.
  7. AP and lateral spot grabs of any areas of concern. Minimize fluoroscopy time throughout.
Pediatric Imaging Pearls
⚡ Vascular ring: look for posterior esophageal impression on the LATERAL view. Bilateral impressions (anterior + posterior) = double aortic arch. Posterior impression only = aberrant right subclavian artery.
⚡ H-type TEF: inject contrast through NG tube at low pressure with patient lateral. The fistula fills only under gentle injection pressure. Have a low threshold to repeat in various obliquities — H-type TEFs are easily missed.
⚡ Physiologic reflux in infants is common — focus on the height of reflux (above carina = aspiration risk), frequency, and gastric emptying time.
⚡ DJJ is the most important landmark in pediatric UGI/esophagram. Never end the exam without documenting where the duodenum crosses the midline. Midgut volvulus is a surgical emergency.
Esophagram (Double Contrast) GI
Mucosal Disease Barrett's Esophagus Early Carcinoma Esophagitis Varices Webs Effervescent granules + dense barium 250% w/v NPO 2+ hours; NO WATER after granules
Contraindications
AVOIDBarium if perforation suspected — use water-soluble contrast
CAUTIONDo NOT administer fizzies if gastroparesis or complete obstruction (CO2 has nowhere to go → vomiting/aspiration risk)
NOTEDouble contrast not used in pediatric patients — adult-only protocol
Equipment
Effervescent granules (1 packet) · Dense barium 250% w/v · Cold water to wash down granules · Cup
Step-by-Step Protocol
  1. Review history; identify the specific clinical question (mucosal disease evaluation vs. motility).
  2. PA chest scout.
  3. Give effervescent granules first — patient takes them dry or with the very smallest sip of water. Instruct patient NOT to burp — this is the most important instruction.
  4. Immediately give small amount of dense barium (2–3 oz) — patient drinks quickly.
  5. Position patient upright in LPO — get images immediately while CO2 is still distending the esophagus and barium is coating the mucosa.
  6. Mucosal coating images (upright LPO): 3 spot images — proximal, mid, distal esophagus. The esophageal mucosa should appear as a fine, uniform white coat. Look for plaques, erosions, nodularity.
  7. Add single contrast component: have patient take additional thick barium swallows. LPO standing × 3 (proximal, mid, distal) during continuous swallowing.
  8. Right lateral supine: thin barium, multiple sips, document open LES.
  9. RPO reflux evaluation with provocative maneuvers.
  10. Pharynx evaluation if indicated.

Imaging Pearls
⚡ CO2 distension is temporary — work fast. Get mucosal coating images within 60 seconds of granules.
⚡ True double contrast = air + barium simultaneously. If patient burps, you've lost it — the study becomes single contrast.
⚡ Candida esophagitis: discrete plaques (parallel white lines on en-face view — "shaggy" esophagus in severe cases).
⚡ Barrett's esophagus: reticular mucosal pattern in distal esophagus. Sensitivity is limited — endoscopy is gold standard.
⚡ Varices: best seen on mucosal relief images — longitudinal, serpiginous, "worm-like" filling defects that change with respiration and position.

Esophagram — Suspected Perforation GI
Esophageal Perforation Anastomotic Leak Post-Procedure Evaluation Boerhaave Syndrome Water-soluble ONLY — Omnipaque 240–300 preferred NPO; IV access; may need emergent evaluation
Contraindications
NEVERBarium if perforation suspected — barium mediastinitis/peritonitis has >50% mortality
NEVERGastrografin (high-osmolar ionic) if ANY aspiration risk — causes fatal chemical pneumonitis. Acceptable ONLY if perforation is strongly suspected AND aspiration risk is negligible
Equipment
Omnipaque 240–300 (preferred) or Gastrografin (only if zero aspiration risk) · NG tube (if patient cannot swallow) · Large-bore IV access · Surgical/thoracic team alerted before starting
Step-by-Step Protocol
  1. Alert the attending and surgical team BEFORE beginning — esophageal perforation is a surgical emergency. Do not work this up solo.
  2. Review clinical context: timing (Boerhaave = sudden violent vomiting; iatrogenic = post-endoscopy/dilation), location of pain, CXR findings (pneumomediastinum, pleural effusion, subcutaneous emphysema).
  3. PA and lateral chest fluoroscopy scout — document any pneumomediastinum, pleural effusion, air tracking.
  4. Contrast selection:
    • If aspiration risk present: Omnipaque 240–300 orally — low osmolar, non-ionic, safe if aspirated
    • If no aspiration risk and perforation strongly suspected: Gastrografin is acceptable
    • In most cases: default to Omnipaque — safer in all situations
  5. Patient position: upright if able; right lateral decubitus is also excellent (contrast pools dependently, enhancing detection).
  6. Give small test sip of contrast first — observe under continuous fluoroscopy as contrast enters the pharynx and proximal esophagus.
  7. Lateral chest view is critical — perforation site often seen best on lateral (posterior mediastinal extravasation, pleural fistula).
  8. Have patient take several swallows, image at each level:
    • Pharynx/cervical esophagus: AP and lateral
    • Mid-thoracic esophagus: AP and lateral
    • GEJ/distal: AP and lateral; RPO supine if patient can lie down
  9. If water-soluble study is NEGATIVE but clinical suspicion remains high: proceed with thin barium (more sensitive for small leaks — sensitivity >90%).
  10. Document: site of extravasation, extent of mediastinal contamination, communication with pleural space, size of leak.

Pediatric VCUG is primarily performed to evaluate vesicoureteral reflux (VUR) after febrile UTI and to diagnose posterior urethral valves in male infants. Radiation minimization is critical — cyclic VCUG or contrast-enhanced voiding urosonography (ceVUS) may be preferred at some centers.
Indications
Febrile UTI (first or recurrent) per AAP guidelines, suspected posterior urethral valves, ureterocele, hydronephrosis evaluation, renal duplication.
Protocol Differences
Catheter size: 6–8 Fr for infants, 8–10 Fr for toddlers, 10–12 Fr for older children.

Fill volume: estimated capacity = (age in years + 2) × 30 mL. Do not overfill.

Cyclic VCUG: fill → void → refill → void again to increase VUR detection sensitivity.

Pulse fluoro mode. Minimize images to the voiding phase only when possible. Do not image continuously during filling.
VUR Management Context
Low-grade VUR (I–II) in young children often resolves spontaneously. High-grade (IV–V) or bilateral: nephrology/urology referral. Dilating VUR (III–V) = higher risk for renal scarring.

GU Fluoroscopy
Retrograde Urethrogram (RUG)
GUAdult / Male
Indications
Suspected urethral stricture, post-traumatic urethral injury (pelvic fracture), urethral disruption, urethral diverticulum in males, failed urethral catheterization, evaluation prior to urethroplasty.
Patient Prep
No specific prep. Verify no active UTI. Patient supine, then oblique (right posterior oblique, 30–45°) for voiding urethrogram positioning. Penile clamp or Brodney clamp needed.
Protocol
  1. Scout film of pelvis.
  2. Patient positioned in right posterior oblique (RPO) ~30–45° — this profiles the entire male urethra in one plane.
  3. Place a 12–14 Fr Foley catheter tip at the fossa navicularis (just inside the meatus). Inflate balloon only 1–2 mL (just enough to hold — do not fully inflate in anterior urethra).
  4. Inject undiluted Omnipaque 300 (20–30 mL) by hand while acquiring fluoroscopic images. Inject steadily — watch for contrast to fill posterior urethra and bladder.
  5. Spot images: oblique urethra (entire length), bladder base. AP pelvis if needed.
  6. Document: stricture location, length, caliber, extravasation, fistula.
Imaging Pearls
⚡ Anterior urethra: penile + bulbous urethra (ventral, below perineal membrane). Posterior urethra: membranous + prostatic (above/at perineal membrane).
⚡ Bulbous urethra is the most common site for post-inflammatory stricture (STI-related, especially gonorrhea).
⚡ Membranous urethra injury — pelvic fracture urethral injury (PFUI). RUG shows disruption at membranous urethra with extravasation.
⚡ Stricture vs. spasm: Spasm at external sphincter may mimic stricture. Complete the injection and assess contrast transit — true stricture shows abrupt narrowing with prestenotic dilation.
Normal RUG Dictation
Retrograde urethrogram was performed in the right posterior oblique projection. The penile urethra, bulbous urethra, membranous urethra, and prostatic urethra are of normal caliber throughout. No stricture, extravasation, or filling defect identified. Contrast fills the bladder freely. No evidence of urethral disruption or fistula.
Hysterosalpingogram (HSG)
GUAdult / Female
Indications
Infertility evaluation (tubal patency), recurrent pregnancy loss (uterine anomaly), uterine synechiae (Asherman's syndrome), müllerian duct anomaly characterization, tubal ligation confirmation, post-myomectomy evaluation.
Patient Prep
Schedule days 7–10 of menstrual cycle (post-menses, pre-ovulation). Confirm not pregnant. Pre-medicate with ibuprofen 600 mg PO 1 hour prior. Prophylactic doxycycline 100 mg BID × 5 days if tubes are blocked (reduces risk of pelvic infection). Pelvic exam/speculum required — nursing/tech assist needed.
Protocol
  1. Scout AP pelvis.
  2. Patient in lithotomy position. Speculum placed. Cervix visualized and cleaned.
  3. HSG catheter (balloon or acorn-tip) placed in cervical os. Balloon inflated 1.5–2 mL in lower uterine segment — do not over-inflate.
  4. Inject undiluted Omnipaque 300 slowly (3–5 mL) — document uterine cavity filling. Spot image.
  5. Continue injection (total 10–15 mL) — document bilateral tubal filling and peritoneal spillage.
  6. Oblique views if tubal anatomy unclear. Spot images in multiple projections.
  7. Remove catheter. Post-procedure film (delayed 15 min) to document free peritoneal spillage (confirms patency).
Imaging Pearls
⚡ Normal uterine cavity: triangular, smooth margins, fills uniformly. Fundal concavity >1 cm = bicornuate vs. septate (MRI for differentiation).
⚡ Tubal occlusion: contrast does not fill beyond the obstruction. Proximal (cornual) vs. distal (hydrosalpinx). Cornual spasm can mimic proximal occlusion — reposition and reinject.
⚡ Peritoneal spillage = tubal patency. Free spillage bilaterally = both tubes open.
⚡ Synechiae (Asherman's): irregular filling defects within uterine cavity, linear or irregular.
⚡ Submucosal fibroids: smooth filling defects indenting the endometrial cavity.
Normal HSG Dictation
Hysterosalpingogram was performed. The uterine cavity is of normal triangular morphology with smooth margins. No filling defects, synechiae, or uterine anomaly identified. Both fallopian tubes fill completely with contrast. Bilateral free peritoneal spillage is confirmed, consistent with bilateral tubal patency. No hydrosalpinx or tubal dilation. Findings are normal.
Fistulogram / Sinogram
GU / GeneralAdult
Indications
Evaluate cutaneous fistula or sinus tract (post-surgical wound, Crohn's perianal fistula, enterocutaneous fistula, vesicocutaneous fistula, wound drainage). Define tract origin, extent, and communication with bowel/bladder/joint.
Patient Prep
No specific prep. Identify external opening. Have water-soluble contrast (Omnipaque 300) ready. Angiocath or small-caliber Foley may be needed to cannulate the tract.
Protocol
  1. Scout film of region of interest.
  2. Place angiocath or small Foley into external opening. Secure with purse-string suture or tape to prevent backflow.
  3. Inject undiluted Omnipaque 300 slowly under fluoroscopic guidance. Watch tract fill in real time.
  4. Spot images in multiple planes (AP, lateral, obliques) to define the full extent of the tract and any communications.
  5. Document: tract origin, branching, communication with bowel/bladder/joint/peritoneum.
  6. If bowel communication suspected: look for contrast outlining bowel loops with mucosal pattern.
Imaging Pearls
⚡ Enterocutaneous fistula — complex post-surgical complication. Contrast outlines small bowel mucosa (fold pattern visible). Output >500 mL/day = high-output fistula.
⚡ Always use water-soluble contrast — peritoneal contamination or mediastinal tracking would be catastrophic with barium.
⚡ Perianal fistula (Crohn's): inject through external opening. MRI is gold standard for complex perianal disease, but sinogram defines external anatomy.
⚡ Tract may be very small — use a 22 or 20-gauge angiocath and inject slowly. High resistance suggests tight tract or false passage.

Biliary
T-Tube Cholangiogram
BiliaryAdult
Indications
Post-cholecystectomy biliary drainage tube evaluation, confirm ductal clearance before T-tube removal (typically POD 7–10), assess anastomotic patency after biliary-enteric anastomosis, evaluate for retained calculi.
Patient Prep
No prep. Confirm tube is not clamped. Have undiluted and diluted Omnipaque 300 ready. Do not inject if patient has active cholangitis or sepsis without surgical discussion.
Protocol
  1. Scout AP abdomen. Identify T-tube position and surgical clips.
  2. Connect tubing to T-tube using sterile technique. Flush dead space (note: do not inject air bubbles — simulate calculi).
  3. Inject dilute Omnipaque 300 (1:1 saline) slowly by gravity or gentle hand injection (2–5 mL/min). Do not over-pressurize — risk of cholangitis.
  4. Spot images: CBD, right and left hepatic ducts, intrahepatic biliary tree, flow into duodenum.
  5. Oblique and lateral views if needed to profile ductal anatomy or filling defects.
  6. Document: CBD caliber, presence of filling defects (calculi), flow through ampulla of Vater into duodenum, any leak at T-tube insertion site.
  7. Post-drainage film (after allowing contrast to drain) to confirm clearance.
Imaging Pearls
⚡ Air bubbles look like calculi — always purge air from tubing before injecting. Stones are fixed; bubbles float and move.
⚡ Retained stone: round filling defect with meniscus sign. Manage with ERCP stone extraction after T-tube removal.
⚡ Free flow into duodenum = no distal obstruction. Failure to fill duodenum = ampullary stenosis or retained stone at ampulla.
⚡ CBD caliber: post-cholecystectomy CBD may be up to 10 mm (mild dilation expected). >10 mm suggests obstruction.
⚡ Sepsis risk: over-pressurized injection → bacteremia. Inject slowly and stop if patient develops rigors or fever during injection.
Normal T-Tube Cholangiogram Dictation
T-tube cholangiogram was performed via injection of dilute water-soluble contrast. The common bile duct measures [X] mm in caliber. The right and left hepatic ducts and intrahepatic biliary tree fill without filling defects or stricture. No retained calculi identified. Contrast flows freely through the ampulla of Vater into the duodenum without obstruction. No extravasation at the T-tube insertion site. Findings are consistent with a patent biliary system without retained calculi.
MSK / Neuro
Fluoroscopic Arthrogram
MSKAdult
Indications
Joint injection for MR arthrogram (shoulder, hip, wrist, ankle, elbow, knee), diagnostic arthrogram for suspected internal derangement, joint aspiration with arthrogram, post-arthroplasty joint evaluation (loosening/infection).
Contraindications
Active joint infection (septic arthritis) — do not inject contrast or gadolinium; perform aspiration only. Allergy to contrast or gadolinium. Coagulopathy (relative). Overlying skin infection.
Protocol (Shoulder Example)
  1. Scout AP of joint.
  2. Position and prep: shoulder in external rotation (hand supinated). Mark injection site. Sterile prep and drape.
  3. Anesthetize skin with 1% lidocaine.
  4. Advance 22-gauge spinal needle under fluoroscopy to the anterior glenohumeral joint space (inferior glenohumeral joint, rotator interval approach).
  5. Confirm intra-articular position: inject 0.5–1 mL Omnipaque 300 — contrast should flow freely into joint without resistance or soft tissue pooling.
  6. Inject MR arthrogram mixture: 12–15 mL (0.1 mM/mL gadolinium in saline + small amount Omnipaque 300 for fluoroscopic confirmation).
  7. Confirm distension on fluoroscopy. Remove needle. Apply bandage. Patient goes directly to MRI.

Imaging Pearls
⚡ Intra-articular confirmation: contrast flows freely along articular cartilage, fills joint recesses. Extravasation into soft tissue = extra-articular — reposition.
⚡ Rotator cuff tear: contrast fills the subacromial/subdeltoid bursa = full-thickness tear. Partial tear = mucosal irregularity without bursal fill.
⚡ MR arthrogram vs. direct arthrogram: fluoroscopic arthrogram confirms intra-articular position; MRI provides the diagnostic information. Fluoro is the tool, MRI is the study.
⚡ Post-arthroplasty: use dilute contrast only (no gadolinium). Document filling of any prosthesis-bone interface gaps or fistulous tracts.

Fluoroscopic Joint Aspiration
MSKAdult
Indications
Suspected septic arthritis, crystal arthropathy diagnosis (gout, CPPD), post-arthroplasty infection workup, diagnostic aspiration before arthrogram if effusion present, therapeutic aspiration for symptom relief.
Contraindications
Overlying cellulitis at aspiration site. Coagulopathy (relative — INR >1.5, platelets <50K). Bacteremia without directed joint source (relative). Prosthetic joint aspiration requires careful sterile technique and orthopedic awareness.
Protocol
  1. Scout film of joint. Confirm effusion on clinical exam or prior imaging.
  2. Sterile prep and drape. Local anesthetic (1% lidocaine) to skin and subcutaneous tissue.
  3. Advance 18–20 gauge needle to joint space under fluoroscopic guidance. Confirm intra-articular position with 0.5 mL Omnipaque if uncertain.
  4. Aspirate fluid — note color, clarity, volume. Send for cell count with differential, Gram stain, culture, crystal analysis (in separate heparinized tube).
  5. If injecting corticosteroid after aspiration: confirm no clinical concern for infection first. Use separate needle or flush needle. Inject methylprednisolone 40–80 mg ± 0.5–1% lidocaine.
  6. Post-procedure: apply bandage. Instruct patient to avoid strenuous activity for 24–48 hours post-injection.

Imaging Pearls
⚡ Septic arthritis threshold: WBC >50,000 with >75% PMN is highly suggestive but not absolute. Culture is definitive. Always send cultures before antibiotics.
⚡ Gout: monosodium urate crystals — needle-shaped, negatively birefringent under polarized light. CPPD: rhomboid, weakly positively birefringent.
⚡ Prosthetic joint aspiration: always alert orthopedics. Use strict sterile technique. Alpha-defensin and leukocyte esterase assays have high sensitivity for PJI.
⚡ Do NOT inject corticosteroid if septic arthritis is clinically possible — empiric steroid injection can worsen infection dramatically.

Myelogram (Fluoroscopic)
Neuro/MSKAdult
Indications
CSF leak / intracranial hypotension localization, post-surgical spinal evaluation (metallic hardware degrading MRI), radiculopathy/myelopathy when MRI is contraindicated (pacemaker), arachnoiditis, nerve root avulsion, CT myelogram for higher resolution than MRI.
Contraindications
Infection at puncture site. Coagulopathy (INR >1.5, platelets <50K). Increased intracranial pressure (papilledema). Allergy to contrast. Avoid Omnipaque at cisterna magna if seizure disorder (cervical myelogram). Hold NSAIDs 5 days, anticoagulants per protocol before procedure.
Protocol (Lumbar Myelogram)
  1. Prone on fluoroscopic table. Scout film of lumbar spine.
  2. Sterile prep and drape. Local anesthetic (1% lidocaine) at puncture site (L2-3 or L3-4 preferred — avoid L4-5 in degenerative disease).
  3. Advance 22-gauge spinal needle under lateral fluoroscopy to thecal sac. Confirm intrathecal position: free CSF flow, contrast mixes freely with CSF.
  4. Collect CSF sample (2–5 mL) if clinically indicated.
  5. Inject Omnipaque 300 (8–12 mL for lumbar; 6–8 mL for cervical via lumbar approach). Injection must be intrathecal — NO epidural contrast.
  6. Tilt table to distribute contrast. Fluoroscopic spot images: lumbar, thoracic, and cervical (as indicated). Obliques for nerve root sleeves.
  7. Transfer directly to CT for CT myelogram (axial and sagittal reconstructions).
  8. Patient remains at 30° head elevation for 4–6 hours post-procedure (reduces headache).
Imaging Pearls
⚡ Epidural vs. intrathecal: intrathecal = contrast mixes with CSF immediately, nerve roots visible within contrast column. Epidural = contrast outlines dural sac from outside — do NOT inject myelographic dose epidurally.
⚡ Nerve root cut-off: disc herniation or stenosis compresses/effaces nerve root sleeves — seen as blunted or absent filling of root sleeve on oblique views.
⚡ Post-myelogram headache (PDPH): positional, worse upright, improves supine. Managed with hydration, caffeine, analgesics. Epidural blood patch if persistent.
⚡ CSF leak (intracranial hypotension): fluoroscopic myelogram with positional imaging or CT myelogram can identify extradural contrast extravasation at the leak site.
⚡ Arachnoiditis: clumping of nerve roots ("empty thecal sac" — roots clumped peripherally), no individual root sleeves visible.
Normal Lumbar Myelogram Dictation

The thecal sac fills symmetrically from [level] to [level]. The nerve root sleeves are normally formed bilaterally at all lumbar levels. No nerve root compression, cutoff, or filling defect. Special ProceduresAdult
Indications
Suspected hemidiaphragm paralysis or paresis, elevated hemidiaphragm on chest X-ray, phrenic nerve palsy (post-cardiac surgery, cervical spine disease, tumor, trauma), unexplained dyspnea, pre-operative phrenic nerve assessment.
Patient Prep
No prep required. Upright PA chest fluoro preferred. If bedside: AP supine (less sensitive). Explain the sniff maneuver to the patient before starting.
Protocol
  1. Position patient upright (PA) or supine under fluoroscope. Include both hemidiaphragms in the field.
  2. Observe diaphragm motion with quiet breathing first. Document baseline position and symmetric motion.
  3. Instruct patient to perform a sharp, forceful sniff (rapid nasal inspiration). Observe both hemidiaphragms simultaneously.
  4. Normal: both hemidiaphragms descend symmetrically with sniff.
  5. Paradoxical motion: affected hemidiaphragm rises (ascends) during sniff — characteristic of phrenic nerve palsy. The negative intrathoracic pressure is generated by the contralateral normal diaphragm, causing the flaccid side to be sucked up.
  6. Record fluoroscopic video clip (minimum 3–5 sniffs). Document in multiple positions if equivocal.
  7. Measure diaphragmatic excursion if quantification requested (normal: >2 cm).
Imaging Pearls
⚡ Paradoxical motion is diagnostic — the paralyzed diaphragm rises on sniff (moves opposite to normal). This is pathognomonic of phrenic nerve palsy when unilateral.
⚡ Bilateral paralysis is harder to detect — both diaphragms may show reduced or absent motion. Suspect with severe unexplained dyspnea, especially post-cardiac surgery (ice slush phrenic injury).
⚡ False negative: partial paralysis (paresis) may show minimal paradox. Always compare to the contralateral side — asymmetric excursion is the key.
⚡ Eventration vs. paralysis: eventration = permanent elevation with reduced but not paradoxical motion. Paralysis = paradoxical motion on sniff. Clinically similar; management differs.
⚡ Sensitivity ~90% for unilateral phrenic palsy when performed upright.
Normal Sniff Test Dictation
Fluoroscopic evaluation of diaphragm motion (sniff test) was performed in the upright PA position. With quiet breathing, both hemidiaphragms demonstrate symmetric excursion. During sharp sniff maneuvers, both hemidiaphragms descend symmetrically without paradoxical motion. No evidence of hemidiaphragm paralysis or paresis. Diaphragmatic excursion is symmetric and within normal limits bilaterally.
Dobhoff / Nasoenteric Tube Placement (Fluoroscopic)
Special ProceduresAdult
Indications
Failed bedside NG/nasoduodenal tube placement, gastroparesis requiring post-pyloric feeding, aspiration risk requiring duodenal/jejunal feeding, altered anatomy (post-surgical), patient intolerance of bedside placement, tube malposition confirmation and correction.
Patient Prep
Confirm tube type (NG vs. nasoduodenal vs. nasojejunal — length matters). Have tube at bedside (Dobhoff 8 or 10 Fr, with stylet). Lubricate tube. Position patient if possible (semi-upright preferred, or RAO for better pyloric view). Glucagon 0.5–1 mg IV optional to relax pylorus and facilitate post-pyloric passage.
Protocol
  1. Scout AP abdomen/chest.
  2. Advance tube nasally (or orally if nasal access failed) while fluoroscoping. Watch tube course through oropharynx, esophagus, into stomach.
  3. Coil tube in gastric body. Position patient RAO or right lateral decubitus to pool contrast/air at pylorus.
  4. Inject 10–20 mL Omnipaque 300 through tube to opacify stomach and identify pylorus. Advance tube tip to pylorus under fluoroscopic guidance.
  5. Guide tube through pylorus with gentle torque and patient positioning. This is the technically challenging step — patience and tube manipulation required.
  6. Advance to desired position: duodenal bulb (D1) for nasoduodenal, ligament of Treitz or beyond for nasojejunal.
  7. Remove stylet. Confirm final position with spot image (AP abdomen, label tube tip level).
  8. Secure tube at nose. Document tube tip position in report.
Imaging Pearls
⚡ Glucagon trick: 0.5 mg IV glucagon relaxes the pylorus and often allows the tube to pass with minimal manipulation. Particularly helpful in gastroparesis.
⚡ RAO position pools gastric contents at the pylorus — gravity assists tube passage. Right lateral decubitus is an alternative.
⚡ Tube coiling in stomach — always straighten tube by withdrawing before attempting pyloric passage. A coiled tube has no directionality.
⚡ Post-pyloric position: tube should cross midline to right, then curve inferiorly (duodenal C-loop), then cross back left toward ligament of Treitz for post-pyloric feeding.
⚡ Nasojejunal vs. nasoduodenal: jejunal (past ligament of Treitz) is preferred for high aspiration risk — further from pylorus and reflux-prone proximal stomach.
Dobhoff Placement Dictation
Fluoroscopically-guided nasoenteric feeding tube placement was performed. The tube was advanced nasally under fluoroscopic guidance through the oropharynx and esophagus into the stomach. With patient positioning and gentle tube manipulation, the tube tip was advanced through the pylorus and the duodenal C-loop. Final tube tip position is at the [duodenal bulb / third portion of duodenum / ligament of Treitz / proximal jejunum] as confirmed by fluoroscopic spot imaging. The tube is in satisfactory post-pyloric position suitable for enteral feeding.
