Fluoroscopy Procedure Guide
Step-by-step protocols for GI/GU and special fluoroscopic examinations
Reference & Educational Use Only. Always correlate with institutional protocols and attending guidance. Patient safety and clinical judgment take precedence over any reference document.
GI Fluoroscopy
Esophagram / Barium Swallow GI
Dysphagia / Odynophagia Globus Post-op Esophagus Suspected Perforation Thin + Thick Barium; Water-soluble if perforation NPO 2+ hours
Contraindications
AVOIDBarium if suspected perforation — use water-soluble (Gastrografin/Omnipaque)
NEVERHigh-osmolar ionic contrast (Gastrografin) if aspiration risk — causes pulmonary edema/pneumonitis. Use low-osmolar IOCM (Omnipaque) orally instead
Equipment
Thin barium (60% w/v) · Thick barium (200–250% w/v) · 12.5 mm barium tablet · Barium cup · Straw · Fluoroscopy unit with spot film capability
Step-by-Step Protocol
  1. Review clinical history, prior imaging, and operative reports before the patient enters the room.
  2. Explain procedure to patient; remove artifacts (ECG leads, jewelry, clips from field).
  3. Obtain scout chest radiograph before any contrast administration.
  4. Position patient standing/sitting in LPO (this obliques the esophagus off the spine). Use supine or reverse Trendelenburg if patient unable to stand.
  5. Give patient thick barium and observe motility before capturing images — test swallow first.
  6. LPO standing esophagus × 3: obtain 3 spot images during continuous swallowing — one each at proximal (cervical), mid-thoracic, and distal thoracic esophagus.
    • Have patient take a full mouthful and swallow continuously (3 swallows → 3 shots)
    • Time exposures during active swallowing (lumen should be distended)
  7. Right lateral supine: lay patient supine; use thin barium (multiple sips). Obtain upper, mid, and distal esophagus including open LES. Magnify LES region.
    • Thin barium flows more slowly and better demonstrates mucosal detail
    • Capture open LES — this is mandatory
  8. Reflux evaluation in RPO: contrast pools at cardia in this position. Perform provocative maneuvers:
    • Valsalva / cough maneuver
    • Roll to right lateral and back
    • Water siphon test (2 sips water, watch GEJ in real-time)
    • Document: maneuver performed, level of reflux, clearance time
  9. Pharynx evaluation standing: have patient gargle thick barium.
    • AP scout → say "EEEEE" → image; puff cheeks → image
    • Repeat lateral view with same maneuvers
    • If abnormal pharynx: acquire cine at 3 fr/sec
  10. Barium tablet (12.5 mm): administer tablet and document passage through esophagus.
    • If tablet catches: have patient drink water to attempt to clear
    • Strictures <13 mm are typically symptomatic
  11. Review images with attending; repeat any incomplete views before releasing patient.

Imaging Pearls
⚡ Always image the open LES. Missing a distal esophageal stricture or ring is the most common error in esophagrams.
⚡ Stricture persistence: if a narrowing is seen, have the patient keep drinking to confirm it persists vs. a transient peristaltic wave.
⚡ Schatzki ring: mucosal (B-ring) ≥2 cm above diaphragm indicates sliding hiatal hernia. Rings <11 mm are typically symptomatic. A-ring = muscular contractile ring (normal/dynamic); B-ring = mucosal ring (pathologic if <13 mm).
⚡ NEVER use high-osmolar ionic contrast (Gastrografin) if aspiration risk — causes severe pulmonary edema and pneumonitis. Use low-osmolar, non-ionic contrast (Omnipaque) orally instead.
Normal Dictation Template
Single contrast esophagram was obtained. Normal esophageal motility was observed. The esophagus is of normal caliber throughout without evidence of stricture, mass, or ulceration. The lower esophageal sphincter opens normally. No hiatal hernia or gastroesophageal reflux is identified. The pharynx is unremarkable.
Pediatric Esophagram — Adapted technique for neonates, infants, and children. Radiation minimization and age-appropriate positioning are essential.
Pediatric Indications
GERD / recurrent vomiting · Suspected vascular ring (dysphagia lusoria) · TEF post-repair surveillance · Foreign body evaluation · Aspiration workup · Malrotation screening (DJJ assessment)
Key Technique Differences
🟣 Fluoroscopy grabs and last-image-hold ONLY — no full-exposure spot films. Minimizes dose significantly.
🟣 No double contrast (no effervescent granules/fizzies) — thin barium only.
🟣 No barium tablet — not used in pediatric patients.
🟣 No pharyngeal exam unless specifically requested — separate MBS with speech pathology if swallowing evaluation needed.
Equipment
Thin barium (60% w/v) · Age-appropriate cup or bottle nipple for infants · NG tube if infant cannot swallow cooperatively · Parent/guardian in room with lead apron
Step-by-Step Protocol (Pediatric)
  1. Have parent/guardian remain in room; provide lead apron. Children cooperate better with a familiar adult present.
  2. Position:
    • Infants: supine or upright held by parent
    • Toddlers/children: upright standing or sitting preferred
  3. Lateral view first — the lateral projection is most sensitive for detecting reflux and posterior esophageal impressions (vascular ring).
  4. Administer thin barium via bottle (infants) or cup. Watch the barium pass from pharynx through esophagus to the stomach using fluoroscopy grabs/last-image-hold.
  5. CRITICAL — Document the DJJ (duodenojejunal junction): Normal = at or LEFT of spine, at the level of the pylorus. If DJJ is to the RIGHT of the spine or below the pylorus → malrotation until proven otherwise.
  6. Assess for GEJ reflux: in infants, gentle leg-raise or Trendelenburg position can provoke reflux if clinically relevant.
  7. AP and lateral spot grabs of any areas of concern. Minimize fluoroscopy time throughout.
Pediatric Imaging Pearls
⚡ Vascular ring: look for posterior esophageal impression on the LATERAL view. Bilateral impressions (anterior + posterior) = double aortic arch. Posterior impression only = aberrant right subclavian artery (dysphagia lusoria — typically mild symptoms).
⚡ H-type TEF: inject contrast through NG tube at low pressure with patient lateral. The fistula fills only under gentle injection pressure. Have a low threshold to repeat with patient in various obliquities — H-type TEFs are easily missed.
⚡ Physiologic reflux in infants is common — focus on the height of reflux (above carina = aspiration risk), frequency, and gastric emptying time, not just presence/absence.
⚡ DJJ is the most important landmark in pediatric UGI/esophagram. Never end the exam without documenting where the duodenum crosses the midline. Midgut volvulus is a surgical emergency.
Modified Barium Swallow (MBS) with Speech Pathologist GI
Dysphagia / Aspiration Eval Post-stroke Swallowing Aspiration Pneumonia Workup Neuromuscular Disease Barium 60% + 200–250% w/v; thickened liquids; barium paste No prep required
Contraindications
CAUTIONPharyngeal perforation suspected → use water-soluble contrast. Airway fistula → avoid ionic contrast.
Step-by-Step Protocol
  1. Introduce yourself to the speech pathologist; discuss expected findings and the study plan before beginning.
  2. Introduce yourself to the patient as the assisting radiologist; explain the procedure.
  3. Position: right lateral decubitus, tabletop vertical. Collimate to head and neck. Do NOT include the eyes in the radiation field. Use 1–2× magnification.
  4. Speech pathologist administers progressively challenging consistencies: thin liquid → nectar-thick → honey-thick → pudding → solid.
  5. Record entire exam (cine/video) at minimum 4 frames/sec. Coordinate swallow with count: "1…2…3…swallow."
  6. Lateral view first: document:
    • Oral transit time
    • Nasopharyngeal reflux
    • Vallecular and pyriform sinus pooling
    • Laryngeal penetration (above cords)
    • Tracheal aspiration (below cords)
    • Cricopharyngeal muscle function
  7. AP view: at least one liquid barium swallow. Collimate just wider than the cervical spine.
  8. If abnormal pharynx is identified: acquire cine at 3 fr/sec (patient holds contrast, swallows on count of 3).
  9. Phonation assessment if requested: center on vocal cords with magnification; record during sustained phonation.
  10. Discuss findings with speech pathologist after exam; note which consistencies caused aspiration or penetration.
  11. Not necessary to review with attending before releasing patient — this study is interpreted jointly with speech pathology.

Imaging Pearls
⚡ Penetration vs Aspiration: Penetration = contrast enters larynx above true vocal cords. Aspiration = contrast passes below true cords into trachea.
⚡ Significant aspiration: STOP THE EXAM immediately. Alert speech pathologist; suction if needed; ensure patient is safe before continuing.
⚡ Silent aspiration (no cough reflex) is common in neurologic patients — watch carefully below the cords even if patient does not react.
⚡ Vallecular pooling + cricopharyngeal bar is a common finding in elderly patients. Assess whether compensatory strategies (chin tuck, head turn) reduce pooling.
⚡ Radiation safety: DO NOT include the eyes in the radiation field — always collimate tightly to the pharynx and cervical esophagus.
Normal Dictation Template
Videofluoroscopic swallowing evaluation was performed in conjunction with the speech pathology department. Various liquid and solid food substances were used to assess swallowing. Normal oral transit was observed. Both regular and dense barium, as well as semisolid and solid food substances, were swallowed without difficulty. No tracheal aspiration, laryngeal penetration, nasopharyngeal reflux, or significant pooling in the valleculae or pyriform sinuses was observed.
Pediatric Modified Barium Swallow — Feeding evaluation in infants and children requires age-appropriate techniques and mandatory parent involvement.
Pediatric Key Differences
🟣 Parents MUST be present for the entire exam — children feed better in the parent's presence. Parent holds and feeds the child.
🟣 Age-appropriate food consistencies and utensils — bottle + nipple for infants; sippy cup for toddlers; regular cup/spoon for older children.
🟣 Explain the procedure to the parents, not the child — the child will follow the parent's lead and routine feeding pattern.
🟣 Minimize fluoroscopy time — use fluoroscopy grabs and last-image-hold. Infants swallow rapidly; capture in short bursts.
Step-by-Step Protocol (Pediatric)
  1. Meet with the speech-language pathologist before the exam. Discuss the child's specific feeding concerns, baseline diet, and what consistencies to test.
  2. Greet the parent and child. Provide lead apron to parent. Explain to the parent (not the child) what will happen — children take cues from parents' demeanor.
  3. Position:
    • Infants: held in parent's arms or in an infant seat, upright or semi-reclined as normally fed
    • Toddlers: seated in a high chair or parent's lap
    • Older children: seated upright in a chair, lateral to the beam
  4. Lateral view — set up in right lateral projection. Collimate tightly to pharynx and cervical esophagus. DO NOT include the eyes in the radiation field.
  5. Speech pathologist administers barium-mixed food/liquid in the child's normal feeding utensil. Use thin barium first; progress to thickened as needed.
  6. Capture swallows in short fluoroscopy bursts (2–3 second clips). Use last-image-hold for single swallow documentation.
  7. AP view: obtain at least one AP swallow — documents asymmetric pharyngeal pooling or unilateral aspiration.
  8. If significant aspiration is observed: STOP immediately. Alert speech pathologist. Suction if needed. Discuss with parents and clinical team before continuing.
  9. Document findings for each consistency trialed and discuss with speech pathologist before releasing the patient.
Pediatric Imaging Pearls
⚡ Infant swallowing is physiologically different — the larynx sits higher in infants and the epiglottis overlaps the soft palate, providing greater airway protection. Swallowing rate is faster than adults (1–2 per second during suckling).
⚡ Silent aspiration is common in neurologically impaired children — watch carefully below the cords even without a cough response.
⚡ Laryngomalacia — dynamic collapse of the supraglottic larynx on inspiration. Can look alarming but is not aspiration. The arytenoids collapse inward; contrast passes below without tracheal entry.
⚡ Cricopharyngeal achalasia in neonates — failure of the cricopharyngeus to relax during swallowing. Contrast pools in the pyriform sinuses and may reflux or overflow into the larynx. Treated with dilation or botox injection.
⚡ Behavioral feeding difficulties are common in children — if the child refuses to swallow during the exam, this itself is valuable information for the speech pathologist.
Upper GI Series (UGI) GI
Epigastric Pain / GERD Hiatal Hernia Eval Post-surgical Anatomy Suspected Obstruction Thin + Thick Barium; water-soluble if post-op; optional effervescent granules NPO after midnight or ≥4–6 hours
Contraindications
AVOIDBarium if suspected perforation — use water-soluble contrast
CAUTIONComplete obstruction — avoid barium (impaction risk); use water-soluble contrast or omit entirely
Step-by-Step Protocol
  1. Review clinical history; note any prior surgery — post-surgical anatomy dramatically changes what you're evaluating. Review op notes if available (Nissen, Billroth I/II, Roux-en-Y, Whipple).
  2. Scout the abdomen fluoroscopically — check for: full stomach (cannot evaluate → reschedule), bowel obstruction, pneumoperitoneum, prior retained barium.
  3. If double contrast (fizzies): administer effervescent granules first (patient takes like a shot, chases with water). Instruct patient NOT to burp — this is critical for maintaining gastric distension.
  4. Standing LPO esophagus × 3: thick barium, same technique as esophagram — proximal, mid, distal thoracic views.
  5. Double contrast stomach coating (if fizzies used): with patient supine, roll through left lateral → prone → right lateral → back ("cement mixer") to coat gastric mucosa uniformly.
  6. Supine stomach views — key positions:
    • LPO: optimizes gastric body and antrum
    • RPO: optimizes lesser curvature; check for reflux here
    • Left lateral: cardia "rosette" and fundus; anterior gastric wall
  7. Duodenal bulb (spade-shaped): LPO position optimizes visualization. If double contrast: try to capture air-filled bulb (may need left lateral decubitus roll to LPO while coughing).
  8. Follow contrast through duodenum (C-loop) and duodenojejunal junction. Document any abnormalities.
  9. Upright AP: overall stomach anatomy, positional change in anatomy, check for free reflux into esophagus.
  10. Confirm/document GERD in RPO position with provocative maneuvers if clinically relevant.
  11. If SBFT is also ordered: begin tracking contrast through small bowel (see SBFT protocol below).

Imaging Pearls
⚡ Position optimization: RPO → lesser curvature (ulcers). LPO → body/antrum. Left lateral → cardia "rosette" and anterior wall.
⚡ Post-Nissen fundoplication: look for slipped Nissen wrap, herniation of wrap above diaphragm, or dysphagia from excessively tight wrap (narrowing at wrap level).
⚡ Post-Roux-en-Y gastric bypass: trace the Roux limb; anastomotic leak is the critical early complication. Look for extravasation at gastrojejunal anastomosis.
⚡ Full stomach = uninterpretable study. Do not proceed — reschedule with appropriate bowel prep. Document findings and reason for rescheduling.
Normal Dictation Template
Single contrast upper gastrointestinal examination was performed. The esophagus demonstrates normal caliber and motility without stricture or mass. There is no evidence of gastroesophageal reflux. The gastric mucosa is normal without ulceration or mass. The duodenal bulb and sweep are normal. No gastric outlet obstruction is identified. No hiatal hernia is seen.
Pediatric Upper GI Series — In children, the primary goal is ruling out malrotation. The DJJ position is the most critical finding of the entire exam.
Pediatric Indications
Malrotation / midgut volvulus workup · Pyloric stenosis (UGI if US indeterminate) · Duodenal atresia / stenosis · Post-surgical anatomy · Antral web · Persistent vomiting in infancy
Critical Pediatric Difference — The DJJ
Normal DJJ (duodenojejunal junction / Ligament of Treitz): at or to the LEFT of the left vertebral pedicle, at the same horizontal level as the duodenal bulb (pylorus).

Malrotation: DJJ is to the RIGHT of the spine, or is below the level of the pylorus. Either finding = call the attending immediately. Midgut volvulus is a surgical emergency.
Step-by-Step Protocol (Pediatric)
  1. Have parent/guardian remain in room with lead apron. Prepare thin barium — no effervescent granules (no double contrast).
  2. Infant feeding technique: barium in a bottle with standard nipple. Toddlers/children: cup or straw. NG tube instillation if child cannot cooperate.
  3. Scout fluoroscopy of the abdomen — note bowel gas pattern, any distension.
  4. Watch barium travel from esophagus → stomach → pylorus → duodenal bulb → duodenum in real-time using fluoroscopy grabs.
  5. CRITICAL STEP — Document the DJJ:
    • Follow the duodenum as it crosses to the LEFT, ascending (3rd and 4th parts)
    • The DJJ should be at or left of the left pedicle of the spine
    • The DJJ should be at the same level as the pylorus (duodenal bulb)
    • Take a spot grab with DJJ and pylorus both visible on the same image
    • If the duodenum does not cross the spine → malrotation. Call attending immediately.
  6. Document the pyloric channel:
    • Normal: opens promptly with contrast passing through in <1 minute
    • Pyloric stenosis: elongated, narrow pyloric channel ("string sign"); "caterpillar" peristaltic waves in antrum; delayed/no gastric emptying
  7. AP and lateral spot grabs at pyloric channel level; AP image showing the DJJ position relative to spine.
Pediatric Imaging Pearls
⚡ DJJ is the single most important structure in the pediatric UGI. Never end the exam without a definitive image showing the DJJ position relative to the spine and pylorus.
⚡ Midgut volvulus = surgical emergency. If malrotation is suspected based on DJJ position: call the attending radiologist and surgical team immediately while the patient is still on the table.
⚡ Pyloric stenosis: ultrasound is the preferred first-line test (pyloric muscle thickness >3 mm, length >15 mm). UGI is confirmatory if US indeterminate — look for string sign and caterpillar waves. Presents in boys aged 2–8 weeks ("projectile vomiting").
⚡ Duodenal atresia: "double bubble" on plain radiograph (stomach + proximal duodenum only). UGI confirms complete obstruction at the duodenal level. Associated with Down syndrome in 30% of cases.
⚡ Antral web: thin membrane just proximal to the pylorus. Gives the appearance of a second "pylorus." Often missed — look carefully at the antrum for a linear filling defect.
Small Bowel Follow-Through (SBFT) GI
Crohn's Disease Eval / Surveillance Malabsorption Obstruction / Stricture GI Bleed Workup 16–24 oz thin barium (60% w/v) orally; Gastrografin to speed transit NPO after midnight; low-residue diet day prior
Step-by-Step Protocol
  1. Patient drinks 16–24 oz thin barium (can give in multiple cups; encourage patient to drink steadily).
  2. Obtain baseline overhead KUB immediately after drinking is complete.
  3. Serial overhead KUBs every 15–30 minutes tracking the barium front through the small bowel. Adjust interval based on transit speed.
  4. Fluoroscopic spot films of any abnormal-appearing loops — use compression paddle to separate overlapping loops and characterize mucosa.
  5. Document progression through:
    • Jejunum: feathery folds (valvulae conniventes), 3–4 folds/cm, left upper quadrant
    • Ileum: fewer/flatter folds, right lower quadrant
    • Terminal ileum and ileocecal valve: evaluate carefully with compression spot films
  6. Terminal ileum is the critical structure — Crohn's is most common here. Obtain AP and oblique compression spots of the TI.
  7. Document time when barium reaches the cecum (normal transit: 30 min – 2 hours). Obtain final overhead KUB when barium reaches terminal ileum/cecum.
Imaging Pearls
⚡ Normal jejunum: feathery folds (valvulae conniventes), 3–4 folds/cm, left upper quadrant. Fold thickening >3 mm suggests edema, inflammation, or infiltration.
⚡ Normal ileum: fewer and flatter folds, right lower quadrant. Terminal ileum lymphoid follicles (cobblestone pattern) in young patients are normal.
⚡ Crohn's disease: look for string sign (long segment severe narrowing), skip lesions with normal intervening bowel, rose-thorn ulcers, fistula tracts, and strictures.
⚡ Use compression paddle liberally — overlapping loops conceal pathology. This is the most important technique in SBFT.
⚡ Speed transit: cold water accelerates transit. If patient is slow: metoclopramide 10 mg IV or Gastrografin mixed with barium (acts as cathartic) can help.
Barium Enema — Single and Double Contrast GI
Colon Evaluation / Polyp Detection Post-surgical Colon Anatomy Obstruction Assessment Intussusception Reduction Single contrast: thin barium. Double contrast: thin barium + air. Water-soluble for perforation/post-op. Full bowel prep: clear liquids + laxatives + enemas; NPO night before
Contraindications
ABSOLUTEKnown/suspected perforation, toxic megacolon — use water-soluble contrast only or defer
RELATIVERecent deep biopsy (<7 days), suspected complete obstruction (use water-soluble)
Step-by-Step Protocol (Double Contrast)
  1. Review clinical and surgical history. Assess adequacy of prep on scout KUB — residual stool is a deal-breaker for double contrast.
  2. Scout abdominal KUB — confirm adequate prep (minimal/no residual stool visible).
  3. Rectal tube insertion: patient in Sims position (LAO with right knee and hip flexed). Lubricate tip; advance with patient bearing down gently.
  4. Instill thin barium with patient in left lateral decubitus (LLD); advance barium front to hepatic flexure under fluoroscopic guidance.
  5. Drain most barium, leaving a thin mucosal coat; insufflate air to distend the colon.
  6. Rotate patient through multiple positions to coat mucosa evenly (the "roll"):
    • LLD → supine → RLD → prone → standing
    • Each roll coats different colon segments with thin barium layer
  7. Spot films in each position — systematic:
    • Rectum: lateral view (mandatory — do not skip)
    • Sigmoid: bilateral obliques
    • Descending colon: LPO
    • Transverse colon: prone and supine
    • Hepatic and splenic flexures: decubitus views
    • Ascending colon: RPO
    • Cecum and appendix: AP + obliques
  8. Overhead films: prone, supine, LLD decubitus, RLD decubitus, AP upright.
  9. Double-check any areas of concern with additional targeted spot films.

Imaging Pearls
⚡ Lateral rectum is ESSENTIAL — anterior rectal masses and polyps are completely missed on AP-only views. Never omit this view.
⚡ Annular "apple core" lesion = carcinoma until proven otherwise. Needs urgent clinical follow-up.
⚡ Diverticula vs polyps: diverticula fill with barium and have a thin stalk; polyps are fixed filling defects. Double contrast: polyp = ring shadow or etched-in-white appearance.
⚡ Adequate bowel prep is critical — residual stool perfectly simulates polyps. Inadequate prep = uninterpretable double contrast study.
⚡ Post-anastomosis or suspected perforation: use water-soluble contrast (Gastrografin), NEVER barium — barium peritonitis is life-threatening.
Pediatric Enema — In children, the primary indication is intussusception reduction, which uses a completely different technique from the adult diagnostic barium enema.
Primary Pediatric Indication: Intussusception Reduction
Intussusception (ileocolic in 90% of cases) · Peak age: 6 months–3 years · Presents with colicky abdominal pain, currant jelly stool, palpable sausage-shaped mass · Confirm with ultrasound first (target/donut sign)
Contraindications to Enema Reduction
ABSOLUTEPeritonitis, pneumoperitoneum (bowel perforation) — go directly to surgery
RELATIVEShock/hemodynamic instability, small bowel obstruction pattern, prolonged symptoms >48h (increased perforation risk), suspected lead point (Meckel's, lymphoma)
Pre-procedure Requirements
🟣 Pediatric surgeon must be present or immediately available — perforation during reduction requires immediate operative intervention.
🟣 IV access established · Sedation/analgesia per anesthesia protocol · Consent from parent for both reduction and potential emergency surgery.
Air Reduction Enema Protocol (Preferred)
  1. Review US confirming intussusception. Check for free fluid, lead point, or signs of bowel ischemia.
  2. Insert soft rectal catheter (Foley or dedicated pneumatic reduction catheter). Tape the buttocks together to maintain a seal and prevent contrast/air leakage.
  3. Scout KUB: document baseline bowel gas pattern; confirm no free air.
  4. Insufflate air gradually under continuous fluoroscopy. Start at 80 mmHg; maximum pressure: 120 mmHg.
    • Never exceed 120 mmHg — perforation risk increases dramatically above this threshold
    • Watch the intussusceptum (soft tissue mass) retract proximally as air fills the colon
  5. Watch for the "meniscus sign" (concave leading edge of air column) to disappear as the intussusceptum reduces through the ileocecal valve.
  6. Successful reduction = air freely fills the terminal ileum through the ileocecal valve. Air entering the TI is the definitive sign — not just reaching the cecum.
  7. If first attempt unsuccessful: deflate, wait 3–5 minutes, and retry. Up to 3 attempts total are standard. After 3 failed attempts → surgical consultation for operative reduction.
  8. Post-reduction KUB: confirm adequate bowel gas pattern and absence of free air.
  9. Admit patient for observation — recurrence risk is 5–10% within 24–48 hours. Most recurrences can be re-reduced fluoroscopically.
Imaging Pearls — Pediatric
⚡ Terminal ileum filling = successful reduction — not just air reaching the cecum. The ileocecal valve must open and TI must fill with air.
⚡ Maximum pressure: 120 mmHg (air) or 100 cm H₂O (hydrostatic). Exceeding this threshold dramatically increases perforation risk.
⚡ Perforation during reduction = tension pneumoperitoneum. Immediate large-bore needle decompression (2nd ICS midclavicular line) + surgical team. Have needle decompression supplies ready before starting.
⚡ Lead points: in children <2 years, lead points are rare (Meckel's diverticulum, polyp). Children >5 years: higher suspicion for lymphoma as a lead point — if reduction fails, suspect lead point.
⚡ Recurrence: 5–10% recurrence rate, typically within 24–48h. First recurrence can usually be reduced fluoroscopically. Multiple recurrences or suspected lead point → surgical referral.
⚡ Hirschsprung's disease enema: different peds indication — use water-soluble contrast; look for transition zone (narrow aganglionic segment → dilated proximal bowel). Classic location: rectosigmoid junction.
GU Fluoroscopy
Voiding Cystourethrogram (VCUG) GU
Vesicoureteral Reflux (VUR) Urethral Abnormalities Neurogenic Bladder Post-traumatic Urethral Eval Dilute water-soluble IOCM — Omnipaque 150 (~30% concentration); dilution avoids obscuring subtle detail Clean urine; active UTI should be treated first
Contraindications
RELATIVEActive UTI (contrast can cause bacterial seeding) — treat first. Urethral obstruction (evaluate with RUG first).
Equipment
Foley catheter (8–10 Fr adult; 5–8 Fr pediatric) · Dilute contrast · Gravity drainage bag · Sterile drape and cleansing supplies
Step-by-Step Protocol
  1. Sterile technique: cleanse urethral meatus; sterile drape. Lubricate catheter tip.
  2. Insert Foley catheter; confirm correct bladder position by observing urine drainage.
  3. Scout KUB: note any calcifications, assess spine/pelvis, identify ureteral stents or foreign bodies.
  4. Fill bladder by gravity (bag ~1 meter above table). NEVER apply external pressure or force fill.
    • Adult capacity: ~300–400 mL (fill until patient reports urge to void)
    • Pediatric formula: [age in years + 2] × 30 mL
  5. Filling phase imaging: spot films AP pelvis at 1/3, 2/3, and full bladder capacity.
    • Watch for early VUR — early reflux during filling = worse grade
    • Document any reflux immediately as it appears
  6. Full bladder views: AP and bilateral posterior oblique to assess bladder wall, diverticula, and filling defects.
  7. Remove Foley catheter.
  8. Voiding phase (most important): image urethra during voiding — highest intravesical pressure, most likely to demonstrate VUR and urethral pathology.
    • Male: 30–45° oblique to profile the urethra without spine overlap
    • Female: AP or lateral during voiding
  9. Post-void AP KUB: check for residual reflux, residual urine volume.
  10. Document reflux grade (I–V per international classification) for each ureter.

GradeDescription

Imaging Pearls
⚡ Fill by gravity only — never pressurize. Forced filling causes elevated intravesical pressure → false-negative reflux AND risk of bladder injury.
⚡ Voiding phase is mandatory — highest intravesical pressure occurs during voiding. VUR and posterior urethral valves (boys) are most apparent during this phase.
⚡ Posterior urethral valves in boys: VCUG is the gold standard diagnostic test. Look for dilated posterior urethra with a "spinning top" appearance on voiding views.
⚡ Dilute contrast (1:3 to 1:4 dilution) is essential — concentrated contrast obscures subtle grade I–II reflux and urethral detail.
⚡ Bladder diverticula should drain post-void but retained contrast in a diverticulum post-void is an expected normal finding.
Pediatric VCUG — Smaller catheters, weight-based fill volumes, and particular attention to posterior urethral valves in boys. Parental presence is strongly recommended.
Pediatric Indications
Vesicoureteral reflux (VUR) after UTI · Suspected posterior urethral valves (boys) · Neurogenic bladder / spina bifida · Bladder/urethral anomalies · Post-surgical follow-up
Pediatric-Specific Equipment
Catheter size: 5–8 Fr (vs 8–10 Fr adult) · Dilute Omnipaque 150 (same as adult) · Gravity fill bag · Sterile technique
Bladder Fill Volume Formula
Pediatric bladder capacity = [Age in years + 2] × 30 mL
Examples: 1-year-old = 90 mL · 3-year-old = 150 mL · 5-year-old = 210 mL
Stop filling when child expresses strong urge to void, even if calculated volume not reached.
Step-by-Step Protocol (Pediatric)
  1. Have parent/guardian present. Explain the procedure to the parent and, in age-appropriate terms, to the child. Reassurance significantly improves cooperation.
  2. Catheter insertion (5–8 Fr): strict sterile technique. Use generous lubrication. Have parent reassure the child during catheter insertion.
  3. Scout KUB: assess spine (spina bifida occulta, sacral agenesis), pelvic bones, prior stents or hardware.
  4. Fill bladder by gravity only (bag 1 meter above table). Calculate fill volume using formula: [age + 2] × 30 mL. Never use external pressure or force fill in children.
  5. Filling phase: AP spot images at 1/3, 2/3, and full bladder. Watch for early VUR — early reflux (during filling) = worse grade prognosis.
  6. Full bladder: bilateral posterior oblique views to assess bladder wall, diverticula, trabeculation (suggests high-pressure bladder / PUV).
  7. Remove catheter. Have child void into a bedpan or diaper.
  8. Voiding phase — CRITICAL (especially in boys):
    • Boys: oblique view (30°) to profile the entire urethra. Watch for dilated posterior urethra = posterior urethral valves (PUV)
    • PUV appearance: dilated posterior urethra + prominent bladder neck + small caliber distal urethra ("spinning top" or "keyhole" urethra)
    • Girls: AP or lateral during voiding
  9. Post-void AP KUB: residual reflux, bladder residual volume estimation.
  10. Document VUR grade (I–V) per international classification for each ureter.

Pediatric Imaging Pearls
⚡ Posterior urethral valves (PUV) in boys: VCUG is the gold standard diagnostic test. Look for dilated posterior urethra ("spinning top"), prominent bladder neck, trabeculated thick-walled bladder. PUV is the most common cause of severe obstructive uropathy in male neonates.
⚡ Trabeculated bladder + bilateral VUR in a boy = PUV until proven otherwise. This combination demands urgent urological referral.
⚡ Cyclic VCUG: some institutions perform 2–3 fill-void cycles to catch intermittent VUR. Standard is single cycle, but if initial cycle shows no reflux and clinical suspicion is high, consider a second fill cycle.
⚡ Radiation minimization: use fluoroscopy grabs + last-image-hold during filling. Reserve continuous fluoroscopy for the voiding phase only (highest diagnostic yield).
⚡ When to do VCUG after UTI: current guidelines (AAP/NICE) recommend VCUG after the first febrile UTI in boys; after second UTI or abnormal renal US in girls. Discuss with the referring team if timing is unclear.
⚡ Neurogenic bladder (spina bifida): look for high bladder capacity, trabeculation, diverticula, and reflux. Spinal abnormality on scout KUB is a key clue. Coordinate with urology for management planning.

Step-by-Step Protocol (Male)

Imaging Pearls
⚡ Oblique position (30–45°) is mandatory — AP view superimposes the urethra directly on the symphysis pubis, making interpretation impossible.
⚡ Bulbomembranous junction = most common site of post-traumatic stricture and posterior urethral valve location. Examine this region carefully.
⚡ Anterior urethra (fossa navicularis through bulbar) = common site of stricture from instrumentation, catheter injury, or gonococcal infection.
⚡ Extravasation of contrast in trauma = urethral disruption. Do NOT pass a Foley catheter. Stop immediately and consult urology.
⚡ Balloon only 1–2 mL — fully inflating the balloon causes significant patient pain and prevents adequate filling of the distal urethra.

Step-by-Step Protocol
  1. Confirm LMP; ensure patient is not pregnant; verify timing is days 7–10 of cycle.
    • This timing avoids irradiating an early pregnancy and the endometrium is thinner (better mucosal detail)
  2. Scout pelvis KUB before contrast administration.
  3. Speculum inserted vaginally; cervix visualized and cleansed with antiseptic.
  4. Insert HSG catheter through cervical os; inflate balloon just inside the internal os (1–2 mL) to occlude the cervical canal.
  5. Remove speculum (reduces scatter radiation and artifact, and unobstructs the fluoroscopic view of the pelvis).
  6. Inject contrast under continuous fluoroscopy — watch in real-time for:
    • Uterine cavity filling (shape and contour)
    • Cornual patency (both sides)
    • Tubal filling (medial → lateral)
    • Peritoneal spill (free dispersion = patent)
  7. AP spot films: full uterine fill; bilateral cornua; tubes bilaterally; peritoneal spill.
  8. Oblique views as needed to profile each tube separately if superimposition limits assessment.
  9. Delayed film (5–10 min): confirm free intraperitoneal spill bilaterally — contrast should disperse around bowel loops freely.
  10. Deflate balloon; remove catheter. Patient may experience cramping — this is expected.
Imaging Pearls
⚡ Proximal tubal occlusion (cornual) artifact: a very common false positive. Cornual spasm can exactly mimic true occlusion. Try glucagon 1 mg IV, wait 5 minutes, reattempt. True occlusion persists; spasm relaxes.
⚡ Septate vs bicornuate uterus: HSG cannot reliably distinguish — the internal contour appears the same. MRI is needed to evaluate external fundal contour. External fundal notch >1 cm = bicornuate; flat/convex = septate.
⚡ Cavity filling defects: fibroid (smooth, round), polyp (smooth, pedunculated), Asherman synechiae (irregular, linear bands), or air bubbles (move with tilting).
⚡ Loculated peritoneal spill (contrast that does not disperse freely around bowel) suggests peritubal adhesions, even with tubal patency.
⚡ Premedicate with NSAIDs (ibuprofen 600–800 mg) 1 hour before the procedure to reduce uterine cramping during distension.

Hysterosalpingogram was performed. The uterine cavity is normal in size and configuration with smooth margins. Both fallopian tubes fill completely and demonstrate free peritoneal spill bilaterally. No intrauterine filling defects or synechiae are identified. The findings are consistent with bilateral tubal patency.

Step-by-Step Protocol
  1. Review clinical history thoroughly — know the suspected communication (bowel, bladder, pleura, skin). Review prior cross-sectional imaging.
  2. Scout the area of interest with fluoroscopy before injecting any contrast.
  3. Cannulate the tract opening or connect to the existing drain with an appropriately sized catheter (Foley balloon catheter or angiocatheter). Secure the connection.
  4. Inject contrast under real-time fluoroscopy — slow, steady injection. Note resistance (high resistance suggests narrowed tract or occlusion).
  5. Document extent of tract:
    • Length and direction
    • Branching or complex anatomy
    • Any undrained collections / cavities
  6. Document any communications: bowel loops, bladder, pleural space, skin surface.
  7. Multiple projections are essential — AP alone misses many tracts. Obtain obliques as needed to unravel complex or overlapping tracts.
Imaging Pearls
⚡ Always use water-soluble contrast — barium peritonitis is a surgical emergency with high mortality. Never use barium for fistulography.
⚡ Inject slowly — excessive pressure can rupture the tract or force contrast into unintended spaces (including venous absorption).
⚡ Oblique views are required — many complex tracts are completely invisible on AP projections. Always image in at least two planes.
⚡ If bowel communication is confirmed: ensure NPO precautions are in place; consult surgery/GI. Document the level of bowel involved.

Step-by-Step Protocol
  1. Scout fluoroscopic image of the right upper quadrant.
  2. Clamp the T-tube; aspirate any air or bile from the tube and connecting tubing.
  3. Connect contrast-filled tubing to the T-tube without injecting any air — purge all air bubbles from the tubing before connecting. Air = false-positive filling defect.
  4. Inject contrast slowly under fluoroscopy — watch for:
    • CBD filling and caliber
    • Intrahepatic ductal filling bilaterally
    • Flow into the duodenum (confirms no distal obstruction)
    • Filling defects (stones = round/faceted; move with gravity)
  5. Spot films: AP and RPO views of the biliary tree.
  6. Document:
    • CBD caliber (<8 mm normal; up to 10 mm after cholecystectomy)
    • Intrahepatic duct filling bilaterally
    • Presence/absence of filling defects
    • Flow into duodenum (may require tilting table)
  7. Delayed image to confirm duodenal flow — tilt table if needed to encourage contrast past the ampulla.
Imaging Pearls
⚡ PURGE ALL AIR from the tubing before injecting — air bubbles exactly mimic small bile duct stones (rounded filling defects). This is the most common error in T-tube cholangiography.
⚡ Dilute contrast: concentrated contrast is opaque and obscures small stones. Dilute 1:1 with saline for optimal stone detection.
⚡ Distinguishing stones from air: stones are gravity-dependent (settle to dependent portion); air floats. Tilting the table 30° helps differentiate.
⚡ Filling defect that moves = stone; fixed filling defect = stricture or tumor. Tilt the patient to confirm mobility.
⚡ Inject slowly — forceful injection causes spasm at the sphincter of Oddi, simulating a distal obstruction (false positive for obstruction).
⚡ Normal CBD after cholecystectomy: up to 10 mm is considered normal (vs. 8 mm in non-cholecystectomy patients).

General Principles
Always confirm intra-articular position with iodinated contrast BEFORE injecting gadolinium. Extravasation wastes expensive gadolinium, nullifies the exam, and requires rescheduling. Gadolinium injection is a one-way door — you cannot retrieve extravasated contrast.
Knee Arthrogram Protocol
  1. Patient supine, knee slightly externally rotated.
  2. Lateral approach: palpate the joint line; prep and drape the knee. Use a 20–22G needle.
  3. Under fluoroscopic guidance, advance needle into the lateral joint space (just medial to the lateral femoral condyle, through the bare cartilage area of the patellofemoral compartment).
  4. Inject a small amount of iodinated contrast under fluoroscopy to confirm intra-articular position — contrast should flow freely around cartilage surfaces into the joint recesses.
  5. If confirmed intra-articular: inject gadolinium/saline mixture (knee: 40–50 mL).
  6. Remove needle; send patient to MRI promptly. Scan within 30–60 minutes (joint resorption begins immediately).
Shoulder Arthrogram Protocol
  1. Patient supine, shoulder in neutral or slight external rotation.
  2. Anterior approach: fluoroscopic target is the inferior 1/3 of the humeral head (GH joint space).
  3. Under fluoroscopy: advance needle to bone (humeral articular surface); small iodinated test dose confirms intra-articular flow (contrast flows away from needle tip into the axillary recess and joint space).
  4. If confirmed: inject gadolinium mixture (shoulder: 12–15 mL). The axillary recess will distend visibly on fluoroscopy.
  5. Send to MRI within 30–60 minutes.
Hip Arthrogram Protocol
  1. Patient supine.
  2. Anterior approach: fluoroscopic target is the femoral head-neck junction (articular margin).
  3. Advance needle to bone; confirm intra-articular position with small iodinated test dose — contrast flows along the femoral neck into the joint space.
  4. If periosteal needle position (against bone but no joint flow): withdraw 1–2 mm and redirect slightly medially. Confirm again before injecting gadolinium.
  5. If confirmed intra-articular: inject gadolinium mixture (hip: 12–15 mL).
  6. Send to MRI within 30–60 minutes.

Imaging Pearls
⚡ Confirm intra-articular position BEFORE gadolinium — extravasated gadolinium cannot be aspirated, wastes contrast, and the exam must be rescheduled.
⚡ Shoulder: contrast flowing into the subscapularis bursa or biceps tendon sheath is normal intra-articular flow — do not mistake for extravasation.
⚡ Hip periosteal needle position: if needle is against cortical bone without joint flow on iodinated test dose → withdraw slightly and redirect. Never inject gadolinium without confirmed intra-articular flow.
⚡ Knee approach: enter through the bare area of the patellofemoral compartment laterally — avoid injecting directly through articular cartilage.
⚡ Timing is critical: MRI within 30–60 minutes of injection for best distension, gadolinium signal, and image quality.

Imaging Pearls
⚡ Non-ionic, water-soluble, isosmolar contrast ONLY — ionic contrast (e.g., diatrizoate/Hypaque) and oil-based contrast (Pantopaque) cause severe arachnoiditis. This is an absolute rule.
⚡ Target below conus medullaris — conus ends at L1 in adults (L2–3 in children). Use L2–3 or L3–4 to avoid cord injury. Never target above L2 without special indication.
⚡ No CSF flow after needle placement: try rotating needle 90°; advance 1–2 mm (may be in epidural space); if still no flow → check lateral view to confirm depth.
⚡ Whitacre (pencil-point) needle has a significantly lower post-LP headache rate vs. Quincke (cutting) needle. Use Whitacre when available, especially in younger patients.
⚡ Send patient to CT or MRI within 4 hours of injection — contrast is optimally distributed and at peak concentration. Delayed imaging (6+ hours) shows fading of nerve root detail.
⚡ Post-LP headache: positional and orthostatic in character. Treat with oral hydration, caffeine (400 mg), and flat rest. Persistent headache >48 hours → consider autologous blood patch (epidural).
