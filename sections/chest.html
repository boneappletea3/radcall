📋 Reporting Checklist — Traumatic Aortic Injury
  • AAST grade: I (minimal intimal tear) / II (intramural hematoma) / III (pseudoaneurysm) / IV (rupture)
  • Location: sinotubular junction / isthmus / descending (cm from left subclavian origin)
  • Periaortic / mediastinal hematoma extent; left hemothorax volume
  • Pseudoaneurysm size: max diameter (mm) vs adjacent normal aortic diameter
  • Active extravasation: present / absent
  • Branch vessel involvement: left subclavian / celiac / SMA / renals / iliacs
  • Aortic diameter proximal and distal to injury (for TEVAR sizing)
  • Hemothorax: bilateral / unilateral; estimated volume
  • Comparison to prior imaging if available

Traumatic Cardiac & Pericardial Injury
Hemopericardium: Pericardial fluid HU 40–70 = blood = hemopericardium until proven otherwise. Tamponade = clinical + echocardiographic diagnosis (right heart collapse, IVC plethora) — CT alone is insufficient. Any hemopericardium in trauma warrants urgent cardiothoracic/trauma surgery notification. Pneumopericardium (air in pericardial sac) = laceration until proven otherwise.
Functional assessment: Valvular injury and wall motion abnormality require echocardiography (TTE/TEE). Troponin + ECG complement imaging for myocardial contusion. MRI gold standard for myocardial injury characterization when stable.

Diaphragmatic Injury
Key concepts: Left-sided injuries more common than right (liver protective effect on right). Mechanism: blunt (MVA, crush) or penetrating. CT signs: organ herniation through defect (stomach, bowel, omentum), collar sign, dependent viscera sign, diaphragmatic discontinuity. Delayed diagnosis common — up to 30% missed on initial imaging. Reformats (coronal/sagittal) essential. Penetrating: any trajectory crossing diaphragm = suspect injury.
SignDescriptionSensitivity
Collar signWaist-like constriction of herniated organ (stomach, bowel, omentum) at the level of the diaphragmatic defect; best seen on coronal/sagittal reformats~54–85% (most specific sign for diaphragmatic laceration)
Dependent viscera signHerniated bowel or stomach resting directly on posterior ribs (right: liver; left: stomach/bowel) without intervening diaphragm; seen on axial images at lung base~55–90% for left-sided injury
Diaphragmatic discontinuityDirect visualization of gap or defect in diaphragmatic contour on CT; best on thin-slice coronal/sagittal reformats; obscured by hemorrhage or elevated hemidiaphragm~71% sensitivity; increased with thin-slice MDCT
Intrathoracic visceraStomach, bowel loops, omentum, or solid organ above diaphragm level in thorax; associated with shift of mediastinum away from herniated side; NGT coiling in thorax (stomach herniation)Highly specific when present; NGT in thorax = pathognomonic for gastric herniation
📄Sliker CW. Imaging of diaphragm injuries. Radiol Clin North Am. 2006;44(2):199-211.

Flail Chest
Definition: ≥3 consecutive ribs fractured in ≥2 places creating a free-floating chest wall segment that moves paradoxically (inward on inspiration, outward on expiration). Clinical diagnosis; CT defines extent of rib fractures and associated injuries.
FeatureDetails
MechanismHigh-energy blunt trauma (MVA, crush, fall from height); anterior flail chest from sternal fracture + bilateral rib fractures
CT findings≥3 consecutive rib fractures in ≥2 sites per rib; displaced or comminuted fractures; identify exact extent for surgical planning; 3D reconstruction helpful
Pulmonary contusionMost critical associated injury; peripheral GGO/consolidation underlying fractured ribs; CT extent predicts ventilator requirement; may lag on CXR by 6h
Associated injuriesPneumothorax / hemothorax; splenic/hepatic lacerations (lower rib fractures); TAI (upper rib fractures); brachial plexus injury (1st/2nd rib)
Management implicationsSignificant pulmonary contusion → positive pressure ventilation (PPV); surgical rib fixation (ORIF) considered for severe cases, ventilator-dependent patients; multi-disciplinary trauma surgery
Pearls: First rib fracture = high-energy marker → screen for TAI, vascular injury, brachial plexus. Bilateral anterior flail ("stove-in chest") from sternal + bilateral rib fractures = severe mechanism. Rib series CXR is inferior to CT for fracture counting — use CT.

Pulmonary Infection — CT Pattern Approach
CT pattern approach: Identify dominant pattern (consolidation, GGO, nodular, cavitary, interstitial) → correlate with clinical context (immune status, geography, acuity) → generate targeted differential. Patterns often overlap; clinical integration is essential.

Pleural Disease
Split pleura sign = empyema: Enhancement and thickening of both visceral and parietal pleural layers with fluid separating them. Loculated effusion + fever + elevated WBC = empyema until proven otherwise — requires drainage. Distinguish from lung abscess: empyema is lenticular, conforms to pleural space, acute angles; abscess is within parenchyma, surrounded by lung.

📋 Reporting Checklist — Aortic Dissection
  • Stanford type: A (involves ascending) / B (descending only); DeBakey I / II / IIIa / IIIb
  • Intimal flap: location (ascending / arch / descending) and longitudinal extent
  • Entry tear: most proximal location (cm from aortic valve / sinotubular junction)
  • Aortic root / annulus involvement (Type A): yes / no
  • Pericardial effusion / hemopericardium: present / absent; size if present
  • Coronary ostia involvement (Type A): right / left / both / none
  • Branch vessel involvement: celiac / SMA / renal arteries / iliac arteries
  • True vs false lumen: identify dominant flow lumen; false lumen thrombosis extent
  • End-organ malperfusion: bowel / renal infarct / spinal cord ischemia
  • Maximum aortic diameter and location; comparison to prior if available

📋 Reporting Checklist — Pulmonary Embolism
  • Clot location: saddle / main PA / lobar / segmental / subsegmental (list affected vessels)
  • Bilateral vs unilateral involvement
  • RV/LV diameter ratio on axial CT (>1.0 = RV strain)
  • Interventricular septal bowing: D-sign present / absent; direction
  • Main pulmonary artery diameter (normal <29mm)
  • Pulmonary infarct: present / absent; location
  • Pleural effusion: present / absent; laterality; size
  • Clinical risk stratification: massive / submassive (RV strain) / low-risk

Fleischner Society 2017 Pulmonary Nodule Guidelines
Applies to incidentally detected nodules in patients ≥35 years without known malignancy or immunocompromise. High-risk features: history of smoking, family history of lung cancer, emphysema on CT, upper lobe location, spiculated margins.

Foreign Body Aspiration
Key concepts: Right mainstem bronchus more commonly affected than left (more vertical, larger caliber, aligned with trachea). Adults (decreased consciousness, dental procedures): lower lobe predilection. Children: right > left. Organic/vegetable material (peanuts, nuts) is radiolucent on XR — CT required. Direct visualization of foreign body on CT is definitive; indirect signs more commonly encountered. Expiratory or lateral decubitus XR can reveal air trapping.
