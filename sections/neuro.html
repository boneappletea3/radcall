ASPECTS (MCA Stroke)
10-point score; subtract 1 point for each involved region. Score ≤6 associated with poor outcome. Regions: Caudate (C), Lentiform (L), Insula (I), Internal capsule (IC), M1–M6 (cortical MCA territory).

AO Spine Classification

Three injury types reflecting progressive instability. Type A = compression (A0–A4). Type B = distraction / tension band (B1–B3). Type C = translation / displacement — always surgical. Neurologic modifier N0–N4/NX appended; "+" added if continued spinal cord compression. Modifiers: M1 = indeterminate tension band injury (may determine need for operative stabilization); M2 = patient-specific comorbidity affecting surgical decision (e.g., ankylosing spondylitis, burns overlying injured spine).

Posterior Ligamentous Complex (PLC)

PLC = supraspinous ligament + interspinous ligaments + facet capsules + ligamentum flavum. Serves as the posterior tension band — disruption leads to instability, kyphotic progression, and vertebral collapse. Usually requires surgical stabilization due to poor healing potential.

CT signs of PLC disruption (indirect): Interspinous distance widening (spinous process splaying) · Facet joint widening · Empty / "naked" facet joints · Perched or dislocated facets · Spinous process avulsion fracture · Vertebral body subluxation or dislocation. Pearl: posterior endplate avulsion fracture → suspect flexion-distraction + PLC injury even with minimal kyphosis or height loss.
MRI — gold standard for PLC (direct visualization): Supraspinous ligament + ligamentum flavum → sagittal T1 or T2 (normal = continuous low-signal black stripe). Interspinous ligaments + facet capsules → STIR or fat-sat T2 (edema/disruption). Disruption = loss of low-signal stripe or fluid signal in its place. Indeterminate = edema without clear disruption. MRI mandatory before conservative management of burst fractures — osseous retropulsion alone does not indicate PLC injury or need for surgery. PLC must be assessed directly on MRI regardless of CT findings.

📋 Reporting Checklist — Thoracolumbar Trauma
  • CT — AO Spine type: A0–A4 / B1–B3 / C; degree of comminution
  • Vertebral body height loss: estimate % anterior compression relative to adjacent levels
  • Retropulsion: distance (mm); % spinal canal compromise = (1 − x/y) × 100, where x = midsagittal canal at injury, y = mean of levels above and below
  • Degree of kyphosis at injury level
  • PLC predictors on CT: interspinous widening · facet widening · naked/empty facets · perched or dislocated facets · spinous process avulsion · vertebral subluxation/dislocation
  • Contiguous and noncontiguous injuries: document all levels
  • MRI — PLC status (intact / indeterminate / disrupted): supraspinous ligament, ligamentum flavum, interspinous ligaments, facet capsules, ALL, PLL, intervertebral disk
  • MRI — Neurologic injuries: cord/conus signal change · cauda equina compression · nerve root injury · epidural hematoma
  • Neurologic modifier (N): N0 intact / N1 transient / N2 radiculopathy / N3 incomplete SCI or cauda equina / N4 complete SCI / NX cannot be examined / + continued compression
  • Modifiers: M1 indeterminate tension band injury / M2 patient-specific comorbidity (AS, burns)

Rigid Spine Injuries
Low-energy trauma can cause severe unstable fractures in the rigid spine. Fractures occur from minor falls (standing or sitting height). Delayed diagnosis in 10–50% of cases — secondary neurologic deterioration risk >80% with delayed diagnosis. Perform spine CT as first step for any rigid spine patient with new pain or suspected trauma. Perform whole-spine CT if any fracture detected (noncontiguous fractures in up to 20%). Low threshold for MRI — occult fracture, epidural hematoma, neurologic deficit. AOSpine M2 modifier designates rigid spine entity in reports.

Andersson Lesion (AS-specific)

Andersson lesion = pseudarthrosis from nonunion of a traumatic or insufficiency fracture in AS. Can mimic infection — key distinguishing features: fracture line extends to posterior elements (facets); minimal soft-tissue swelling (unlike infection); T1 hypointense fracture line with T2 hyperintense reactive changes in surrounding bone. Disk destruction + reactive sclerosis without significant soft-tissue mass. May be unstable and require surgery. Infection has more prominent soft-tissue swelling and abscess formation.

Imaging Strategy

CT: Always first step. Assess with spine windows (not body windows). Whole-spine CT mandatory after any fracture detected. For DISH without posterior element involvement and no neurologic deficit: fracture may be managed conservatively. MRI: Mandatory in AS (high rate of epidural hematoma + cord injury). Low threshold in all rigid spine with neurologic deficit. Assess PLC, epidural hematoma (may extend over long segment in AS), cord signal, nerve roots. DISH posterior element involvement → unstable → MRI for surgical planning.

Hyperacute SEH pitfall: Hematoma <24 h old appears T1 isointense and T2 hyperintense — nearly indistinguishable from CSF on routine sequences. Key clues: effacement of epidural fat; displacement/compression of thecal sac; peripheral enhancement on CE-T1; susceptibility artifact on GRE. GRE is the most sensitive sequence for acute blood.
Spinal Hematoma Reporting Checklist
  • Hematoma type: SEH / SSH / subarachnoid
  • Location: posterior epidural / anterior epidural / circumferential
  • Level and craniocaudal extent (vertebral levels involved)
  • Axial morphology: biconvex (SEH) vs crescentic (SSH)
  • Midline crossing: dark T2 septum (SEH) vs absent
  • Signal: T1 / T2 — estimate chronicity (hyperacute / acute / subacute / chronic)
  • Degree of cord or thecal sac compression
  • Intramedullary T2 signal — cord edema or injury
  • Epidural fat: effaced vs preserved
  • Enhancement: absent / peripheral / active extravasation foci
  • Associated spondylodiscitis (abscess vs hematoma key distinction)
  • Underlying vascular lesion — suggest MR angiography if no clear etiology

LearningHeadandNeck.com
Dental CT pearl: Always evaluate bone windows for periapical lucency and mandibular cortical dehiscence (lingual surface thinnest — breaks first). Lower 2nd/3rd molar roots extend below the mylohyoid muscle → submandibular space. More anterior teeth → sublingual space (roots above mylohyoid). Cellulitis without discrete abscess is the rule in early disease — IV contrast essential to delineate collections.

Peritonsillar & Retropharyngeal Abscess
Danger: Retropharyngeal abscess can extend inferiorly via the danger space (space 4) to the posterior mediastinum causing descending necrotizing mediastinitis — a surgical emergency with high mortality. Any retropharyngeal collection extending below C6 / thoracic inlet requires urgent surgical evaluation.

Epiglottitis / Supraglottitis
CT findings: Thickened epiglottis >8 mm (AP diameter); aryepiglottic fold thickening >3 mm; prevertebral soft tissue swelling; supraglottic airway narrowing; "thumb-shaped" epiglottis replaces normal thin curved epiglottis; periglottic fat stranding; enhancement with IV contrast. Vallecula effacement.
Airway warning: Do NOT attempt to visualize the epiglottis with tongue blade or laryngoscopy without immediate airway control available — can precipitate complete obstruction. Imaging only if diagnosis uncertain AND patient is stable with monitored airway. Any signs of drooling, stridor, or "tripod" positioning = urgent airway management first.
Imaging pearl: Thumbprint sign on lateral neck radiograph (thickened epiglottis resembling a thumb). Vallecula sign: loss of air-filled vallecula. Adults: polymicrobial or H. influenzae; also consider epiglottic abscess (requires CT). Children: H. influenzae type b (Hib) historically, now rare with vaccination. Adults more common presentation today. Supraglottitis = involvement of aryepiglottic folds, arytenoids, and supraglottic structures beyond epiglottis alone.

Salivary Gland Infections
Sialolithiasis pearl: 80–90% of stones occur in the submandibular gland (Wharton's duct — ascending course, thick mucus, slow flow rate). 10–20% in the parotid (Stensen's duct). Most submandibular calculi are radiopaque on CT; most parotid calculi are radiolucent. Always trace the duct along its full course for stones. Unenhanced CT series best for stone detection — IV contrast may obscure calculi.

Lemierre Syndrome
Life-threatening: Septic thrombophlebitis of the internal jugular vein (IJV) following pharyngeal infection (tonsillitis, peritonsillar abscess), with hematogenous dissemination producing septic pulmonary emboli and systemic abscesses. Causative organism: Fusobacterium necrophorum (normal oropharyngeal flora). Predominantly healthy adolescents and young adults. Significant mortality even with treatment — early recognition is critical.
CT findings — evaluate all three components:
  • IJV thrombosis: Expanded non-opacifying IJV; rimlike perivenous enhancement of vessel wall; surrounding inflammatory fat stranding; trace IJV superiorly to assess for sigmoid sinus extension
  • Sigmoid sinus involvement: If thrombus extends superiorly to sigmoid sinus → sigmoid sinus thrombosis → anticoagulation and neurologic monitoring required
  • Septic pulmonary emboli (lung apices on neck CT): Peripheral nodular opacities ± cavitation in lung apices — confirm Lemierre diagnosis
  • Primary source: Peritonsillar or retropharyngeal edema/abscess; tonsillitis; parapharyngeal spread
Reporting reflex: Any peritonsillar or retropharyngeal abscess should prompt search for IJV thrombosis and lung apex nodules. Document superior extent of thrombus (sigmoid sinus involvement?), presence of septic emboli, and source infection. Anticoagulation is generally indicated despite risk of septic hemorrhagic emboli.

Angioedema
Airway emergency: Angioedema causes transient swelling of the face, tongue, lips, and larynx. ACE inhibitors are the most common cause (up to 35% of cases), often occurring months to years after starting therapy. Laryngeal edema may cause complete airway obstruction — early airway assessment and management is critical.
CT findings: Focal or diffuse low-attenuation soft-tissue swelling of upper airway structures; infiltrative transpatial edema; circumferential mucosal thickening with varying degrees of airway narrowing; tongue swelling (especially ACE inhibitor–related); subcutaneous facial edema; no surrounding fat stranding or rim-enhancing collections (key distinction from infectious etiology). Airway narrowing may be asymmetric or focal.

Key MRI sequences for acute myelopathy:

Dural Sinus Thrombosis (CVST)
Key imaging signs: CT: hyperdense sinus (acute) — "dense sinus sign"; post-contrast: "empty delta sign" (filling defect in posterior superior sagittal sinus). MRI: T1 loss of flow void → T1 bright (subacute); T2/FLAIR hyperintensity in sinus; blooming artifact on SWI/GRE (deoxyhemoglobin/hemosiderin); restricted diffusion in venous infarct (often not typical wedge pattern). MRV: filling defect or absent sinus.
Treatment pearl: Anticoagulate even in the presence of intracranial hemorrhage (venous hemorrhage — anticoagulation reduces propagation and mortality). Do not withhold heparin based on hemorrhage alone. Consult neurology/neurosurgery urgently.

Pituitary Apoplexy
Pathophysiology & imaging: Hemorrhage and/or infarction into a pituitary adenoma (rarely normal gland). CT: hyperdense sellar/suprasellar mass (acute hemorrhage); may show expansion of sella. MRI: T1 bright signal (methemoglobin, subacute hemorrhage) within adenoma; T2 variable; surrounding edema. DWI: may show restricted diffusion (ischemic component). Optic chiasm compression → bitemporal hemianopia. Cavernous sinus invasion possible. Macroadenoma most commonly affected.
Endocrine emergency: Acute cortisol deficiency (secondary adrenal insufficiency) is life-threatening — hypotension, hypoglycemia, hyponatremia. Notify endocrinology immediately. Empirical hydrocortisone may be required before lab results. Ophthalmology if visual loss. Neurosurgery if chiasmal compression or deteriorating vision. MRI preferred over CT for full characterization.

PRES (Posterior Reversible Encephalopathy Syndrome)
MRI findings: FLAIR/T2 hyperintensity predominantly in posterior watershed zones (parieto-occipital most common) reflecting vasogenic edema. DWI: usually NOT restricted (distinguishes from infarct) — ADC elevated (T2 shine-through). Restricted diffusion, if present, indicates irreversible injury. Enhancement variable. Resolves with treatment of underlying cause.
Common etiologies: Hypertensive emergency, eclampsia/preeclampsia (most common), immunosuppressants (cyclosporine, tacrolimus), chemotherapy (cisplatin), sepsis/TTP, renal failure.
DistributionRegions Involved
Typical (posterior watershed)Bilateral parieto-occipital lobes (most common); posterior frontal; posterior temporal; superior cerebellum
Atypical (anterior)Frontal lobes, basal ganglia, brainstem, cerebellum; unilateral; may mimic other pathology
Complicated PRESHemorrhage (petechial to lobar), restricted diffusion (ischemia), sulcal FLAIR (SAH variant)
PRES vs posterior circulation infarct: DWI is the key differentiator. PRES = ADC elevated (vasogenic edema, no restriction). Posterior circulation infarct = DWI bright + ADC dark (cytotoxic edema, restricted). Both show FLAIR/T2 hyperintensity in posterior fossa — always check DWI + ADC map. Vascular territory distribution favors infarct; non-vascular confluent pattern favors PRES.
