📋 Reporting Checklist — Acute Ischemic Stroke
  • ASPECTS score (if MCA territory; score /10 — specify affected regions)
  • Vessel occlusion site: ICA / M1 / M2 / M3 / ACA / PCA / basilar (hyperdense vessel sign)
  • Affected vascular territory
  • Collateral grade: good / poor / absent (on CTA source images)
  • Early ischemic changes: grey-white differentiation loss / sulcal effacement / dense MCA sign
  • CT perfusion (if performed): core volume (mL), penumbra volume (mL), mismatch ratio
  • Hemorrhagic transformation: absent / HI1 / HI2 / PH1 / PH2
  • Prior infarcts: location, chronicity
ASPECTS (MCA Stroke)
10-point score; subtract 1 point for each involved region. Score ≤6 associated with poor outcome. Regions: Caudate (C), Lentiform (L), Insula (I), Internal capsule (IC), M1–M6 (cortical MCA territory).

Cervical Ligamentous Injury
MRI indications: CT-negative with persistent pain/neurological symptoms, or high clinical suspicion. STIR/fat-sat T2 most sensitive for ligamentous injury. Obtain within 48–72 h if cord injury suspected.

Apical ligamentTip of dens → basion (anterior foramen magnum)STIR: small midline structure; often difficult to resolve individually; loss of signal or discontinuity; best seen on sagittal fat-sat T2Weak structure; limited biomechanical role alone; injury usually concurrent with alar/TAL disruption; part of craniocervical complex
Cruciate ligamentVertical limb: C2 body → occiput; horizontal limb = TAL anchoring dens to C1 masses; together form a crossTAL (horizontal) best seen on axial T2; vertical bands on sagittal T2/STIR; disruption of vertical component rare but indicates severe craniocervical injuryCruciate = TAL + superior/inferior longitudinal bands; TAL is the critical horizontal component; injury to vertical limbs seen in severe atlanto-occipital dissociation

📋 Reporting Checklist — Blunt Cerebrovascular Injury (BCVI)
  • Denver grade: I (irregularity/dissection) / II (intraluminal thrombus) / III (pseudoaneurysm) / IV (occlusion) / V (transection)
  • Vessel(s) involved: ICA (specify segment: petrous / cavernous / supraclinoid) / VA (V1–V4)
  • Bilateral involvement: yes / no
  • Pseudoaneurysm: size (mm), location
  • Dissection extent: proximal and distal limits
  • Luminal reduction: estimate % stenosis
  • Intraluminal thrombus: present / absent
  • Associated injury: skull base fracture (ICA canal) / cervical spine fracture (transverse foramen)
  • Ischemic changes: present / absent on CT or CTP (territory at risk)

Peritonsillar & Retropharyngeal Abscess
Danger: Retropharyngeal abscess can extend inferiorly via the danger space (space 4) to the posterior mediastinum causing descending necrotizing mediastinitis — a surgical emergency with high mortality. Any retropharyngeal collection extending below C6 / thoracic inlet requires urgent surgical evaluation.
SpaceLocationImaging FindingsDanger / Key Points
Peritonsillar abscessBetween tonsillar capsule and superior pharyngeal constrictor muscleCT: hypodense fluid collection with peripheral enhancement lateral to tonsil; displacement of uvula away from affected side; mass effect on oropharynxMost common deep neck infection; typically unilateral; needle aspiration or I&D; can extend to parapharyngeal space
Retropharyngeal abscessPosterior to pharynx, anterior to prevertebral fascia (between visceral and alar fascia)CT: low-density fluid collection with rim enhancement posterior to pharynx; prevertebral soft tissue thickening >7 mm at C2 or >22 mm at C6; scalloped or lobulated margin; gas = pathognomonic abscessCan extend to mediastinum via danger space; airway compression; most common in children under 6; group A Strep, Staph aureus
Danger space (space 4)Between alar fascia (anterior) and prevertebral fascia (posterior); extends skull base to diaphragmCT: fluid tracking along fascial planes from skull base to posterior mediastinum; often contiguous with retropharyngeal collectionDirect conduit for descending necrotizing mediastinitis; no natural barriers to inferior spread; critical to identify extent on CT chest
Ludwig angina (submandibular)Bilateral submandibular, sublingual, and submental spacesCT: bilateral soft tissue swelling/cellulitis of floor of mouth; gas and/or abscess in submandibular space; displacement and elevation of tongue; airway compromiseRapidly progressive; dental origin (2nd/3rd molar); airway emergency — early intubation or surgical airway; gas-forming organisms; high mortality without aggressive management
📄Vieira F et al. Deep neck infection. Otolaryngol Clin North Am. 2008;41(3):459-83.
Epiglottitis / Supraglottitis
CT findings: Thickened epiglottis >8 mm (AP diameter); aryepiglottic fold thickening >3 mm; prevertebral soft tissue swelling; supraglottic airway narrowing; "thumb-shaped" epiglottis replaces normal thin curved epiglottis; periglottic fat stranding; enhancement with IV contrast. Vallecula effacement.
Airway warning: Do NOT attempt to visualize the epiglottis with tongue blade or laryngoscopy without immediate airway control available — can precipitate complete obstruction. Imaging only if diagnosis uncertain AND patient is stable with monitored airway. Any signs of drooling, stridor, or "tripod" positioning = urgent airway management first.
Imaging pearl: Thumbprint sign on lateral neck radiograph (thickened epiglottis resembling a thumb). Vallecula sign: loss of air-filled vallecula. Adults: polymicrobial or H. influenzae; also consider epiglottic abscess (requires CT). Children: H. influenzae type b (Hib) historically, now rare with vaccination. Adults more common presentation today. Supraglottitis = involvement of aryepiglottic folds, arytenoids, and supraglottic structures beyond epiglottis alone.
📄Shah RK et al. Epiglottitis in adults. Laryngoscope. 2004;114(9):1542-5.
📄Radiopaedia — Epiglottitis

Dural Sinus Thrombosis (CVST)
Key imaging signs: CT: hyperdense sinus (acute) — "dense sinus sign"; post-contrast: "empty delta sign" (filling defect in posterior superior sagittal sinus). MRI: T1 loss of flow void → T1 bright (subacute); T2/FLAIR hyperintensity in sinus; blooming artifact on SWI/GRE (deoxyhemoglobin/hemosiderin); restricted diffusion in venous infarct (often not typical wedge pattern). MRV: filling defect or absent sinus.
Treatment pearl: Anticoagulate even in the presence of intracranial hemorrhage (venous hemorrhage — anticoagulation reduces propagation and mortality). Do not withhold heparin based on hemorrhage alone. Consult neurology/neurosurgery urgently.

Pituitary Apoplexy
Pathophysiology & imaging: Hemorrhage and/or infarction into a pituitary adenoma (rarely normal gland). CT: hyperdense sellar/suprasellar mass (acute hemorrhage); may show expansion of sella. MRI: T1 bright signal (methemoglobin, subacute hemorrhage) within adenoma; T2 variable; surrounding edema. DWI: may show restricted diffusion (ischemic component). Optic chiasm compression → bitemporal hemianopia. Cavernous sinus invasion possible. Macroadenoma most commonly affected.
Endocrine emergency: Acute cortisol deficiency (secondary adrenal insufficiency) is life-threatening — hypotension, hypoglycemia, hyponatremia. Notify endocrinology immediately. Empirical hydrocortisone may be required before lab results. Ophthalmology if visual loss. Neurosurgery if chiasmal compression or deteriorating vision. MRI preferred over CT for full characterization.
📄Rajasekaran S et al. UK guidelines for the management of pituitary apoplexy. Clin Endocrinol (Oxf). 2011;74(1):9-20.

PRES (Posterior Reversible Encephalopathy Syndrome)
MRI findings: FLAIR/T2 hyperintensity predominantly in posterior watershed zones (parieto-occipital most common) reflecting vasogenic edema. DWI: usually NOT restricted (distinguishes from infarct) — ADC elevated (T2 shine-through). Restricted diffusion, if present, indicates irreversible injury. Enhancement variable. Resolves with treatment of underlying cause.
Common etiologies: Hypertensive emergency, eclampsia/preeclampsia (most common), immunosuppressants (cyclosporine, tacrolimus), chemotherapy (cisplatin), sepsis/TTP, renal failure.
DistributionRegions Involved
Typical (posterior watershed)Bilateral parieto-occipital lobes (most common); posterior frontal; posterior temporal; superior cerebellum
Atypical (anterior)Frontal lobes, basal ganglia, brainstem, cerebellum; unilateral; may mimic other pathology
Complicated PRESHemorrhage (petechial to lobar), restricted diffusion (ischemia), sulcal FLAIR (SAH variant)
PRES vs posterior circulation infarct: DWI is the key differentiator. PRES = ADC elevated (vasogenic edema, no restriction). Posterior circulation infarct = DWI bright + ADC dark (cytotoxic edema, restricted). Both show FLAIR/T2 hyperintensity in posterior fossa — always check DWI + ADC map. Vascular territory distribution favors infarct; non-vascular confluent pattern favors PRES.
📄Liman TG et al. Posterior Reversible Encephalopathy Syndrome. Curr Treat Options Neurol. 2019;21(11):55.
