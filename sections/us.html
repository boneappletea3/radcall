Appendix — Ultrasound
Technique: Graded compression at point of maximum RLQ tenderness, progressively displacing overlying bowel gas. Normal appendix: <6mm, compressible, no surrounding fat stranding. Non-visualization does NOT exclude appendicitis — proceed to CT in adults if clinically indicated.
FindingSensitivitySpecificity
Appendix >6mm, non-compressible, blind-ending~75–85%~90–95%
Periappendiceal hyperechoic fat stranding~70%~93%
Appendicolith (shadowing echogenic focus)~25–35%~99%
Perforation (loss of wall layers, free fluid)~50%~95%
Hyperemia on color Doppler~87%~96%
Operator and patient factors: Highly operator-dependent; best in thin patients and children. Non-visualization rate ~20–30% in adults. Algorithm: US first in children/pregnancy → non-diagnostic in adults → CT; in pregnancy → MRI preferred over CT.

Lower Extremity DVT
Technique — compression ultrasound (CUS): Non-compressibility of a vein with transducer pressure = DVT (primary criterion). Assess bilaterally: common femoral vein (CFV), femoral vein (FV), popliteal vein, posterior tibial and peroneal veins. Add color Doppler and augmentation.
FindingAcute DVTChronic DVT
CompressibilityNon-compressible; vein lumen does not collapse; may feel "spongy"Non-compressible; firm and fibrotic; may be partially compressible with recanalization
EchogenicityAnechoic or hypoechoic; fresh thrombus may be nearly invisible — Doppler essentialHeterogeneous; hyperechoic; echogenic organized thrombus; wall thickening and fibrosis
Vein caliberEnlarged and distendedContracted, narrowed, or normal with recanalization
Color DopplerAbsent or markedly reduced intraluminal flow; filling defectPartial recanalization with flow around organized thrombus; collateral vessels
AugmentationAbsent with distal compressionReduced or present with recanalization
Respiratory phasicity (CFV)Absent phasic variationMay be absent or reduced
Proximal DVT (CFV, FV, popliteal) = high PE risk — anticoagulation required. Report level of proximal extent explicitly. Isolated distal (infrapopliteal/calf) DVT: serial CUS in 1 week if anticoagulation withheld; anticoagulate if high PE risk or symptomatic.
Misnomer alert: The "superficial femoral vein" is a DEEP vein — DVT here carries the same PE risk and requires full anticoagulation. Use "femoral vein" in reports to avoid clinical confusion.

📋 Reporting Checklist — DVT
  • Veins involved: common femoral / femoral / popliteal / posterior tibial / peroneal / great saphenous (list all)
  • Laterality: right / left / bilateral
  • Proximal extent: document most cephalad level of thrombus
  • Free-floating thrombus: present / absent (increased PE risk — note prominently)
  • Acute vs chronic characteristics (echogenicity, collaterals, wall thickening)
  • Compression documented at all levels examined
  • IVC involvement: assessed / not assessed
OB / First Trimester / PUL
Approach: Transvaginal US (TVUS) is the primary modality for first-trimester evaluation. All thresholds below apply to TVUS. Discriminatory zone (β-hCG at which IUP should be visible) is institution-specific: approximately 1,500–3,500 mIU/mL. A single hCG value alone should not drive clinical decisions in a hemodynamically stable patient.

Retained Products of Conception (RPOC)
Clinical context: RPOC should be considered after any pregnancy event — spontaneous abortion, therapeutic abortion, delivery, or molar pregnancy. Presents with abnormal uterine bleeding, pelvic pain, or fever. Transvaginal ultrasound (TVUS) is the primary imaging modality. Color Doppler is essential to distinguish RPOC from blood clot (no flow) and to assess vascularity, which guides management.
Threshold for diagnosis: Endometrial thickness alone is insufficient. An endometrial thickness >10 mm with heterogeneous echogenicity and/or internal vascularity on Doppler is the most predictive combination. Thin, homogeneous endometrium (<10 mm, no flow) makes RPOC unlikely but does not exclude it — clinical correlation essential.
FindingDescriptionSignificance
Endometrial thickness >10 mmSingle-layer measurement on sagittal transvaginal image; measure at thickest point excluding fluidMost used threshold; sensitivity ~80%, specificity ~65% alone — must combine with echogenicity and Doppler
Heterogeneous endometrial echogenicityMixed echogenic/hypoechoic material within endometrial cavity; may contain echogenic foci (calcification from prior procedure)Highly suspicious when combined with vascularity; heterogeneous contents without flow = likely blood clot
Intraendometrial vascularity (color Doppler)Color flow within endometrial contents — most specific sonographic finding for RPOC; high-velocity, low-resistance waveform on spectral Doppler (RI < 0.4 suggests retained trophoblastic tissue)Most specific finding; presence of flow strongly supports RPOC over hematoma; absence of flow favors organized clot
Gestational sac remnantRecognizable GS, yolk sac, or embryonic tissue within the cavityDefinitive — always represents RPOC
Echogenic endometrial massWell-defined echogenic intracavitary mass; may show shadowing if calcification present (chronic RPOC)Chronic RPOC may show echogenic calcified foci from prior incomplete evacuation
Free fluid in cul-de-sacNon-specific; may accompany RPOC with hemorrhage or infectionCorrelate with clinical symptoms; large free fluid + fever → consider septic RPOC
Doppler pearls: Subinvolution of the placental site (SIPS) — an underdiagnosed cause of late postpartum hemorrhage — shows prominent myometrial vascularity at the implantation site without discrete tissue. Distinguished from arteriovenous malformation (AVM) by absence of a tangle of vessels; AVM shows turbulent high-velocity low-resistance flow in a myometrial nidus often with color aliasing. Always assess the entire myometrium, not just the endometrium.

📋 Reporting Checklist — RPOC
  • Endometrial thickness (mm) — single layer, at thickest point
  • Echogenicity of endometrial contents: homogeneous / heterogeneous / echogenic mass
  • Internal vascularity on color Doppler: present / absent
  • If flow present: waveform character (high-velocity, low-resistance RI <0.4 = trophoblastic)
  • Presence of recognizable gestational sac, yolk sac, or embryo
  • Myometrial vascularity: normal / increased (consider AVM or SIPS if prominent)
  • Free fluid: none / trace / moderate with location
  • Clinical context: post-SAB / post-TOP / post-delivery / post-molar — gestational age at event
  • Impression: findings consistent with / not suggestive of RPOC; recommend clinical correlation and β-hCG

Renal Transplant Evaluation
Doppler normals: Arterial RI 0.5–0.7 (up to 0.85 acceptable early post-transplant). RI >0.9 = graft dysfunction (ATN, rejection, obstruction, RVT). Evaluate for peritransplant fluid collections and assess anastomotic vessel velocities.

Testicular Torsion — Ultrasound
Surgical emergency — time is testis: <6h = >90% salvage · 6–12h = ~50% · 12–24h = ~10% · >24h = <10%. Do NOT delay surgical exploration if the clinical diagnosis is certain. Call the surgeon immediately when diagnosed on imaging.
FindingDescription
Absent/reduced Doppler flowMost sensitive finding; compare symmetrically to contralateral testis using identical settings; complete absence (complete torsion) or asymmetric reduction (partial/intermittent)
Heterogeneous testisHeterogeneous echotexture develops with infarction after ~6h; early torsion may have normal gray-scale — Doppler is critical
Whirlpool signTwisting of the spermatic cord as a whorled/spiraling appearance at the external ring or superior pole; highly specific (~99%)
Reactive hydroceleSimple ipsilateral hydrocele; common but nonspecific
Enlarged/edematous epididymisReactive; distinguish from epididymo-orchitis (increased epididymal Doppler flow)
Scrotal wall thickeningSecondary reactive edema; nonspecific
Critical pitfall: Normal or increased Doppler flow does NOT exclude torsion — intermittent/partial torsion and early torsion (<2h) can have preserved flow. If clinical suspicion is high, proceed to surgical exploration regardless of Doppler result.
