V/Q Scan — PE Detection
Radiopharmaceuticals: Tc-99m MAA (perfusion, 2–4 mCi) + Tc-99m DTPA aerosol or Xe-133 gas (ventilation). SPECT V/Q preferred over planar — reduces indeterminate rate from ~30% to ~5% and improves both sensitivity and specificity. Preferred over CTPA in: renal insufficiency, contrast allergy, pregnancy (lower fetal dose), young women (lower breast dose).
CategoryScintigraphic Criteria (PIOPED II)PE ProbabilityNext Step
Normal No perfusion defects; perfusion outlines normal lung borders <1% PE excluded; no further workup
Very Low Non-segmental defects only (pleural effusion, cardiomegaly, enlarged hilum, aortic impression); ≤3 small (<25% segment) matched defects with normal CXR ~3% Clinical correlation; CTPA if high pre-test probability
Low Probability Small segmental perfusion defects (<25% of segment); any matched V/Q defects with/without CXR abnormality; non-segmental defects with corresponding CXR changes ~14% Low pre-test prob → PE unlikely; high pre-test prob → CTPA
Intermediate Single moderate (25–75% of segment) unmatched defect without CXR abnormality; neither clearly high nor low; difficult to categorize ~30% CTPA or bilateral lower extremity Doppler; do not treat on V/Q alone
High Probability ≥2 large (>75% of segment) unmatched segmental perfusion defects without corresponding ventilation or CXR abnormality; V/Q mismatch pattern >85% Treat for PE; confirmatory CTPA only if anticoagulation very high-risk

V/Q Pitfalls
COPD / bullous diseaseCreates matched defects → pushed to low probability; CTPA preferred in severe obstructive disease
PE with infarction (Hampton's hump)CXR consolidation matches perfusion defect → "triple match" → falsely low probability category; look for additional unmatched defects
Large pleural effusionCompresses lung → multiple non-segmental defects → very low probability; but large effusion can also obscure underlying segmental defects

HIDA Scan (Hepatobiliary Scintigraphy)
Radiopharmaceutical: Tc-99m mebrofenin (Choletec) or Tc-99m disofenin, 5–8 mCi. IDA compounds excreted via biliary system. Patient NPO 2–4h (but not >24h — prolonged fasting or TPN → falsely absent GB visualization; pretreat with sincalide 0.02 µg/kg IV × 30 min). Morphine augmentation: 0.04 mg/kg IV at 60 min if GB not seen — contracts sphincter of Oddi, redirecting bile flow into GB.
FindingInterpretationSens / Spec
GB non-visualization at 4h (after morphine augmentation at 60 min) Acute cholecystitis — cystic duct obstruction Sens ~96%, Spec ~90%
Rim sign — pericholecystic band of increased hepatic parenchymal activity adjacent to GB fossa Severe / gangrenous cholecystitis — surgical emergency; indicates transmural inflammation or perforation Spec ~99%, Sens ~35%
GB visualization delayed (1–4h) but eventually fills Chronic cholecystitis — partial/intermittent cystic duct obstruction —
GB EF <35–40% after CCK (sincalide 0.02 µg/kg infused over 30–60 min) Biliary dyskinesia — functional cystic duct dysfunction; surgical referral (cholecystectomy) Sens ~78%, Spec ~87%
Focal accumulation of activity outside hepatobiliary system, increasing over time Bile leak — post-cholecystectomy, liver transplant anastomotic leak, trauma; activity pools dependently in abdomen/pelvis Sens ~94%
No CBD / bowel activity; intrahepatic duct dilation; activity retained in liver Complete CBD obstruction — choledocholithiasis, stricture, malignancy —
HIDA Pitfalls
Prolonged fasting / TPN (>24h NPO)Concentrated bile fills GB → GB does not fill with new radiotracer → false positive for cholecystitis; pretreat with sincalide
Hepatic dysfunction (bili >5–7 mg/dL)Delayed or absent biliary excretion; decreases sensitivity; consider US as primary modality
Duodenal bulb activity mistaken for GBDuodenum adjacent to GB fossa; follow activity dynamically — bowel activity moves, GB stays; use LAO view
Accessory bile duct / choledochal cystCan mimic bile leak or abnormal focal uptake; MRCP for anatomic clarification
Acalculous cholecystitisSame scintigraphic appearance as calculous cholecystitis; occurs in critically ill/ICU patients; sensitivity slightly lower (~80%)

GI Bleed Scan (Tc-99m RBC)
Radiopharmaceutical: Tc-99m labeled autologous RBCs, 20–25 mCi. In vitro labeling (UltraTag) preferred — highest labeling efficiency (~98%). Detects active bleeding at rates as low as 0.1–0.5 mL/min (vs. angiography threshold ~0.5 mL/min, CT angiography ~1 mL/min). Primary use: lower GI bleeding localization before IR angiography or surgical planning. Tagged RBCs circulate for 24h — delayed imaging possible for intermittent bleeds.
FindingInterpretation
Focal activity appearing at or before 5 min, distinct from blood-pool structures, increasing over time and moving in direction of peristalsis Active GI bleeding — localize to time of first appearance, not subsequent frames
Activity confined to liver, spleen, heart, great vessels, kidneys at all time points No active bleeding at time of imaging; if clinical suspicion remains, delayed images at 2h, 4h, 24h may detect intermittent bleed
Activity in renal collecting system and bladder on early images Free Tc-99m pertechnetate (poor in vivo labeling) — non-diagnostic; repeat with in vitro UltraTag kit
Persistent focal activity that does NOT move or increase over time Normal variant: penile blood pool, accessory spleen, hemangioma, uterus — not GI bleed; check multiple views
⚠ Critical rule — localize to earliest appearance: Tagged blood travels rapidly antegrade and retrograde through bowel loops. Activity first seen in the cecum at 5 min may appear to be in the sigmoid or transverse colon on later frames. Always record and use the time of first appearance for anatomic localization — not later redistribution images.
GI Bleed Scan Pitfalls
Intermittent / slow bleedingScan may be negative if bleeding has stopped; tagged RBCs have 24h half-life — delayed imaging at 2h, 4h, 24h can detect intermittent bleeds; serial imaging preferred
Poor labeling (in vivo method)Free Tc-99m → gastric, thyroid, renal activity → mimics bleed; always use in vitro UltraTag for best results
Penile / midline pelvic blood poolIn men: midline pelvic RBC pool can mimic rectal bleed; obtain lateral views; void and re-image
Upper GI bleedRapid transit can mimic lower GI; upper GI bleed activity will be seen in stomach first, then moves distally within minutes
Liver / spleen overlapping bowelHepatic flexure and splenic flexure bleeds may be obscured; SPECT/CT adds anatomic localization

Brain Death Study
Radiopharmaceutical: Tc-99m HMPAO (Ceretec) or Tc-99m ECD (Neurolite), 20–25 mCi IV. Both cross intact blood-brain barrier and are retained in viable neurons proportional to cerebral perfusion. Confirmatory/ancillary test only — does NOT replace clinical brain death exam + apnea test. Required when clinical exam is confounded (barbiturate coma, hypothermia, metabolic encephalopathy, facial/spinal trauma).
FindingInterpretation
"Hollow skull" sign — absent cerebral parenchymal activity on planar; no cortical, cerebellar, or brainstem uptake; facial/scalp activity preserved from external carotid Brain death confirmed — no cerebral perfusion
Hot nose sign — prominent nasal soft tissue activity Supportive of brain death; ECA territory continues perfusing nasal mucosa when ICA flow ceases; high specificity when combined with absent cerebral uptake
No sagittal sinus activity on flow images Absent venous drainage consistent with brain death; helps confirm absent parenchymal perfusion
Normal symmetric cortical + cerebellar + deep grey uptake ("bat wings" on anterior view) Viable cerebral perfusion — brain death NOT confirmed
Protocol: Flow study (2 sec/frame × 60 frames) immediately after injection, then static images at 30–60 min. SPECT optional but improves detection of residual perfusion. Confounders that can suppress perfusion (must exclude clinically): Barbiturate/sedative coma · Severe hypothermia (<32°C) · Toxic-metabolic encephalopathy · Profound hypotension. Spinal cord and peripheral reflexes may persist with brain death and do not invalidate the study.

Renal Scan (MAG3 / Lasix Renogram)
Radiopharmaceuticals: Tc-99m MAG3 (mercaptoacetylglycine, tubular secretion, 5–10 mCi) — preferred; works well at reduced GFR. Tc-99m DTPA (glomerular filtration) — used for GFR calculation; requires adequate function. Standard protocol: 3-phase (flow 1–2 min, function 20 min, excretion) + Lasix 0.3–1 mg/kg IV. F+20 protocol: Lasix at 20 min post-injection; F−15: Lasix 15 min before injection (preferred when dilated system suspected).
T½ After LasixInterpretationAction
<10 min No obstruction — normal washout No further workup needed
10–20 min Equivocal / indeterminate Ensure adequate hydration; consider F−15 protocol or diuretic renogram under controlled hydration; clinical correlation
>20 min Obstructed Urology referral; correlate with symptoms and ultrasound

Renovascular hypertension (captopril renogram)Baseline MAG3 + repeat 1h after captopril 25–50 mg PO; Positive: asymmetric ↓ in function (>10%) or prolonged time-to-peak on captopril scan → significant renal artery stenosis
Renal transplant — ATNGood perfusion (flow phase normal) + poor tubular function (absent/markedly delayed excretion); improves over days
Renal transplant — RejectionDecreased perfusion AND decreased function; may have photopenic halo; worse prognosis than ATN
Urine leak (post-transplant)Focal accumulation of activity outside collecting system; pools dependently in pelvis; photopenic area within leak on delayed images

MAG3 Pitfalls
DehydrationReduces Lasix response → falsely prolonged T½; hydrate 10 mL/kg IV NS before and during scan
Markedly dilated pelvis (reservoir effect)Large extrarenal pelvis retains tracer even without true obstruction; T½ may be prolonged; correlate with degree of hydronephrosis on US
Severely impaired GFR (<15 mL/min)Inadequate Lasix response; equivocal results common; MR urography may provide better anatomic and functional data

Bone Scan (Tc-99m MDP)
Radiopharmaceutical: Tc-99m MDP (methylene diphosphonate), 20–25 mCi. Accumulates at sites of osteoblastic activity proportional to bone turnover and blood flow. 3-phase protocol: Flow (dynamic 2–3 sec/frame × 60 frames) → Blood pool (5–10 min post-injection) → Delayed whole body (3–4h). SPECT or SPECT/CT added for anatomic localization. Normal: symmetric uptake, kidneys and bladder visible, mild growth plate activity in children.
PatternDifferential / InterpretationKey Pearls
Focal hot spot (delayed)Metastasis, fracture, osteomyelitis, Paget's disease, arthritis — non-specificMost common pattern; CT/MRI correlation required for specificity
3-phase positive (↑ flow + ↑ blood pool + ↑ delayed at same focal site)Osteomyelitis — all 3 phases elevated at site of infectionSens ~90%, Spec ~73%; vs cellulitis: flow + blood pool diffusely elevated but delayed normal or mildly ↑
Flow + blood pool ↑, delayed normal or mildly ↑Cellulitis (no osseous involvement)Key distinction from osteomyelitis — if delayed becomes focally hot, consider underlying osteomyelitis
Cold / photopenic defectEarly AVN (before reactive bone formation), aggressive lytic tumor, multiple myeloma, radiation fieldEarly AVN: cold on bone scan, MRI gold standard; myeloma: cold or normal bone scan (unlike most mets)
Superscan — diffusely increased skeletal uptake; absent or faint renal/soft tissue activityDiffuse bone metastases (prostate, breast), metabolic bone disease (renal osteodystrophy, hyperparathyroidism)Can appear deceptively "normal" — absent kidney activity is the key; request clinical correlation with PSA/tumor markers
Flare phenomenonTransient worsening of bone scan activity 2–6 months after starting effective chemotherapy — represents healing (osteoblastic response), NOT progressionCorrelate with tumor markers and CT; serial scans typically show improvement after 6 months

Meckel's Scan (Tc-99m Pertechnetate)
Indication: Painless rectal bleeding in children (most common cause: Meckel's diverticulum with ectopic gastric mucosa). Mechanism: Tc-99m pertechnetate (5 mCi adult; weight-based pediatric), 5–10 mCi — concentrates in gastric mucosa parietal cells (same as thyroid, salivary glands). Only detects Meckel's containing ectopic gastric mucosa — present in ~25–50% of all Meckel's but ~80–90% of symptomatic/bleeding Meckel's.
FindingInterpretation
Focal ectopic activity appearing simultaneously with gastric mucosa, in RLQ or ectopic location, increasing in intensity over time Meckel's diverticulum with ectopic gastric mucosa — positive scan. Sensitivity ~85–90% in children.
Activity appearing after gastric uptake, or activity that does not increase in intensity Normal variant (blood pool, ureteral activity) or negative scan — Meckel's not excluded if no gastric mucosa present
Pretreatment to improve sensitivity:
  • Cimetidine 300 mg PO q6h × 2 days before scan — blocks Tc-99m washout from gastric mucosa (most commonly used; increases sensitivity to ~95%)
  • Pentagastrin 6 µg/kg SQ × 15 min before — stimulates gastric mucosa uptake; avoid in active GI bleed
  • Glucagon 50 µg/kg IV — reduces bowel motility, decreases redistribution of activity
Meckel's Scan Pitfalls
Ureteral activityRight ureter can mimic RLQ ectopic uptake; always void before scanning; lateral views; activity should clear with voiding
IntussusceptionCan trap pertechnetate → false positive; correlate with US findings
No ectopic gastric mucosa~50% of asymptomatic Meckel's lack gastric mucosa — negative scan does not exclude Meckel's; surgical exploration may still be warranted with high clinical suspicion
Inflammatory bowel disease / duplication cystBoth can contain ectopic gastric mucosa → false positive; clinical and US correlation required
