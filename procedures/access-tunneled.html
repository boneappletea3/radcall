Tunneled Dialysis Catheter Placement

Fluoroscopy- and ultrasound-guided placement of tunneled cuffed venous catheters (TCVC) for hemodialysis and long-term vascular access.

Indications / Contraindications

▾

Dialysis Indications

  • New dialysis patients requiring access before AV fistula (maturation ≥1 month) or AV graft (≥3 weeks) is ready
  • Established dialysis patients with failed or maturing permanent access (bridge)
  • Patients with exhausted all fistula and graft options
  • Per NKF-K/DOQI: tunneled cuffed catheter (TCVC) indicated when access needed for >3 weeks
  • Fewer than 10% of chronic HD patients should rely on catheter as permanent access

Non-Dialysis Indications

  • Long-term chemotherapy (Hickman, Broviac, Groshong)
  • Long-term parenteral nutrition
  • Apheresis

Contraindications — Absolute

  • Coagulopathy: INR >1.5, platelets <50,000
  • Active septicemia — patient must be afebrile on organism-specific antibiotics with negative blood cultures ×48h before tunneled placement; non-tunneled catheter acceptable acutely

Contraindications — Relative

  • Central venous stenosis/occlusion (may need venoplasty first)
  • Cardiac device on ipsilateral side
  • Chest wall radiation
  • Prior failed catheter at that site
  • Note: Studies show INR up to 2.0 did not increase bleeding rates with large-bore dialysis catheters

Pre-Procedure Checklist

▾
US survey of proposed access vein. Confirm patency, caliber, and absence of thrombus. Sweep from IJ to brachiocephalic to detect central stenosis. Attenuated IJ with collaterals suggests central occlusion.
Labs (SIR Category 2 — moderate risk). INR goal <1.5–2.0; platelets goal >50K.
Anticoagulation. LMWH hold 1 dose; aspirin/Plavix no hold required for moderate risk.
Active infection exclusion. Negative blood cultures ×48h; afebrile; on organism-specific antibiotics.
IV access + sedation. Required for procedure; moderate sedation (midazolam + fentanyl) or MAC for anxious patients.
Antibiotic prophylaxis. Cefazolin 1g IV pre-op; vancomycin 1g if penicillin/cephalosporin allergy.
Confirm catheter size and type with nephrology. Typical dialysis catheter 14.5Fr split-tip or 13.5Fr step-tip; non-dialysis: Hickman/Broviac per infusion requirements.
Confirm exit site. Right chest preferred (near nipple line, approximately 8–12 cm from puncture site).
Consent. Arterial puncture, pneumothorax, hematoma, catheter malfunction/kinking, infection, thrombosis, inability to meet target flow rates.
3

Relevant Anatomy

▾

Access Site Anatomy

  • Right IJV: First-line access — straight path to SVC, best flow rates, lowest complication risk; preserves right subclavian for future AV access
  • Left IJV: Acceptable; involves two ~90° turns (IJ-brachiocephalic + brachiocephalic-SVC); stiff wire required; may have slightly lower flow rates and higher stenosis rates
  • External jugular vein: Viable when both IJVs occluded; preferable to subclavian
  • Subclavian: AVOID — associated with high central venous stenosis, thrombosis, irreversible compromise of upper extremity AV access; critical in dialysis patients with 15–20 year life expectancy requiring multiple sequential accesses
  • Femoral/translumbar IVC: Last resort; higher infection rates

Tunnel Anatomy

  • Low puncture close to clavicle creates smooth infraclavicular curve — prevents kinking at subcutaneous tunnel-to-intravascular transition
  • Tunnel runs subcutaneously from puncture site to exit site on anterior chest wall (right chest, ~8–12 cm from puncture)
  • Dacron cuff positioned 2–3 cm from exit site — allows tissue ingrowth for anchoring and infection barrier
  • Cuff ≥3 cm from exit site for dialysis catheters: too close → infection risk; too far → kink risk

Tip Positions

  • Dialysis catheters: Right atrium or SVC-RA junction for optimal flow (target 400–600 mL/min)
  • Non-dialysis (Hickman/Broviac): SVC-RA junction preferred

How I Do It

Default RadCall approach + community cards

▾
RadCall Standard Default

Supplies

Sterile US probe cover 21G micropuncture kit 0.018" microwire Peel-away sheath set (16Fr for 14.5Fr catheter) Stiff 0.035" Amplatz wire Tunneled dialysis catheter (Palindrome / HemoStar / Ash split-tip) Tunneling rod/device 1% lidocaine with epinephrine #11 and #15 scalpels Mosquito and Kelly hemostats 2-0 nonabsorbable suture Heparinized saline (5000 units/mL)

Steps

1

US survey of right IJV

Confirm patency; identify carotid; note caliber and depth; sweep to brachiocephalic. Look for attenuated lumen or collaterals suggesting central stenosis.
2

Patient positioning

Supine, head turned left; Trendelenburg ×10–15°. Distends the IJ and reduces air embolism risk.
3

Sterile prep + drape

Full anterior chest (both sides), neck and upper abdomen; large sterile drape. Mark exit site on anterior chest wall, below and medial to nipple, ~8–12 cm from planned IJ puncture site.
4

Infiltrate exit site + incision

Infiltrate exit site with 1% lidocaine with epinephrine (skin + subcutaneous). Make small transverse incision at exit site.
5

Create subcutaneous tunnel

Advance tunneling rod from exit site toward planned IJ puncture site through subcutaneous tissue, staying superficial to pectoralis fascia. Thread catheter onto tunneling rod; pull catheter through tunnel so it exits at the puncture site with appropriate length for IJ entry and tip at right atrium.
6

US-guided IJ access

Micropuncture of right IJV low in neck near clavicle; single-wall puncture. Advance 0.018" microwire under fluoroscopy to IVC (beyond existing catheters if present); confirm wire position in right atrium/IVC on fluoroscopy.
7

Upsize to stiff wire

Skin incision at puncture site; blunt dissection. Advance coaxial micropuncture system; exchange inner dilator for 0.035" stiff wire (Amplatz or Rosen). Confirm wire tip in IVC — NOT curled in RA — before advancing sheath.
8

Advance peel-away sheath

Advance peel-away sheath over stiff wire with smooth, controlled motion. Keep wire straight in IVC to prevent buckling and central vein laceration. Withdraw inner dilator and wire; occlude hub immediately with thumb.
9

Advance catheter through sheath

Advance catheter through peel-away sheath while splitting sheath. Maintain catheter position under fluoroscopy. Target tip: right atrium or SVC-RA junction for dialysis catheters.
10

Confirm position + cuff placement

Verify both lumens aspirate freely. Confirm Dacron cuff positioned 2–3 cm from exit site; if cuff is too far from exit site after insertion, pull catheter back to reposition cuff correctly.
11

Secure + lock

Suture catheter at exit site; sterile dressing. Lock lumens with heparinized saline per volumes printed on catheter hub (arterial: 1.3 mL; venous: 1.7 mL — confirm on hub label). Do not exceed lock volume.
12

Post-procedure CXR

Confirm tip position (right atrium) and exclude pneumothorax before patient leaves suite.

Troubleshooting

▾
Problem

Wire won't advance beyond IJ-brachiocephalic junction (left-sided access)

Likely cause: Sharp angle at left brachiocephalic-SVC junction; wire buckling against wall.

Next step: Use hydrophilic stiff wire; rotate patient's head toward ipsilateral shoulder; careful fluoroscopic guidance through turn.

Problem

Catheter kinked at infraclavicular region

Likely cause: IJ puncture site too high, creating a sharp angle where catheter transitions from subcutaneous tunnel to vein.

Next step: Ensure puncture site is LOW near clavicle. Revise tunnel trajectory to reduce angle at transition point.

Problem

Unable to pass peel-away sheath smoothly

Likely cause: Wire tip curled in right atrium (buckling risk); underdilated tract; wire kinked.

Next step: Confirm Amplatz wire tip is in IVC (not curled in RA). Dilate tract incrementally. Apply smooth controlled forward pressure — do not force. If resistance persists, confirm wire position fluoroscopically before reattempting.

Problem

Inadequate flow rates (dialysis catheter)

Likely cause: Tip malposition in SVC; fibrin sheath; kinking; thrombosis.

Next step: Fluoroscopy to confirm tip position (should be in RA, not SVC). Check for fibrin sheath. Try reversing lumens (temporary fix). TPA lock for fibrin sheath. If persistent — stripping or catheter exchange.

Problem

Cuff excessively far from exit site

Likely cause: Catheter advanced too far before confirming cuff position; measured incorrectly pre-tunnel.

Next step: Pull catheter back at exit site to reposition cuff 2–3 cm from exit site; re-suture.

Problem

Air embolism

Likely cause: Open port during sheath manipulation; inadequate Trendelenburg positioning.

Next step: Immediately left lateral decubitus + Trendelenburg. Occlude all open ports. 100% O². Aspirate air through catheter if feasible.

Complications

▾

Immediate

  • Pneumothorax (1–3% IJ approach) — small stable: O² ± serial CXR; large/growing: small-bore chest tube
  • Arterial puncture (1–3%) — remove needle, firm pressure; if large sheath in carotid: vascular surgery emergency
  • Hematoma (1–3%) — usually self-limited with compression
  • Air embolism (<1%) — left lateral decubitus + Trendelenburg; 100% O²

Delayed

  • Catheter malfunction / inadequate flow — most common long-term complication; fibrin sheath, tip malposition, kinking, thrombosis; TPA instillation or fluoroscopic catheter exchange
  • CLABSI — 1–3 per 1000 catheter-days; Staph aureus or fungal: remove catheter; antibiotic lock therapy for selected organisms (CoNS, gram-negatives)
  • Central venous thrombosis — catheter removal + anticoagulation
  • Subcutaneous hematoma along tunnel — usually self-limited; delayed cuff incorporation if large
7

Post-Procedure Care

▾

Immediate

  • Fluoroscopic confirmation of tip at right atrium before leaving suite
  • CXR post-placement: tip position and pneumothorax exclusion
  • Lock lumens with heparinized saline per volume printed on catheter hub — do not exceed
  • Dialysis team notified: catheter ready for use at next dialysis session; document flow rates at first use (target 400–600 mL/min)

Discharge Instructions

  • Exit site dressing: sterile gauze first 24h; then chlorhexidine-impregnated dressing per institutional protocol
  • Sutures removed at 2–3 weeks once cuff has incorporated
  • Patient education: site care; no submerging in water; keep dressing dry; signs of infection (redness, warmth, discharge, fever)
8

Critical Pearls

▾
✦
Right IJV is always preferred: Straight line to SVC; preserves left subclavian for future AV access. Never use subclavian in dialysis patients — subclavian stenosis is irreversible and can exhaust future access options.
✦
Low neck puncture prevents kinking: Puncture site near clavicle creates smooth infraclavicular curve — prevents kinking at the critical point where catheter transitions from subcutaneous tunnel to intravascular segment.
✦
Peel-away sheath requires stiff wire in IVC: The wire tip MUST be in the IVC — not curled in the RA — before advancing the sheath. If the wire buckles in the right heart, the sheath can lacerate the SVC.
✦
Cuff position is critical: 2–3 cm from exit site for proper tissue ingrowth. Too close → infection route directly to bloodstream. Too far → kink risk at infraclavicular curve.
✦
Active septicemia = absolute contraindication to tunneled placement: Place non-tunneled catheter first; exchange to tunneled once bacteremia clears (confirmed negative cultures ×48h on appropriate antibiotics).
✦
Dacron cuff incorporation takes 2–4 weeks: Securing sutures should remain in place until then. Do not remove early — premature suture removal before cuff incorporation can allow catheter migration.
✦
Target flow rates 400–600 mL/min: Confirm at first dialysis use. Inadequate flow → fibrin sheath is the most common cause. TPA lock or catheter exchange are first-line management.
9

Catheter Management

▾

Flushing + Locking

  • Flush each lumen with heparinized saline after each use
  • Use lock volumes printed on hub — never exceed (can push heparin into circulation)
  • Typical volumes: arterial lumen 1.3 mL; venous lumen 1.7 mL (confirm on hub)

Dressing Changes

  • Chlorhexidine-impregnated sponge + transparent dressing q7d
  • Change more frequently if soiled or wet
  • Strict sterile technique at every dressing change

Fibrin Sheath (Most Common Cause of Inadequate Flow)

  • TPA (1 mg/mL) instillation; dwell ×30 min; aspirate; repeat ×2 if needed
  • If TPA fails → fluoroscopic catheter exchange with fibrin sheath disruption, or stripping via femoral approach

Thrombotic Occlusion (Can't Aspirate)

  • TPA lock per protocol
  • If fails → image-guided exchange; check for fibrin sheath vs intraluminal thrombus vs kink

Catheter-Related Bloodstream Infection (CLABSI)

  • Obtain blood cultures from catheter AND peripheral simultaneously
  • Remove catheter for: Staph aureus, fungal, or tunnel/exit-site infection
  • Antibiotic lock therapy for salvage in selected organisms: coagulase-negative Staph, gram-negatives — only if no tunnel infection and catheter is functioning
  • Catheter exchange: Over wire under fluoroscopy at same site if no tunnel infection; new site and new tunnel if infected
