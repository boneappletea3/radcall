Image-Guided Percutaneous Abscess / Fluid Collection Drainage

Indications / Contraindications

▾

Indications for PCD

  • Symptomatic abdominopelvic abscesses, particularly >5 cm (PCD provides better results than needle aspiration alone for large collections)
  • Abscesses with enteric fistulas — first-line treatment; expect extended dwell times and up to 40% failure rate
  • Postoperative abscesses — 81% clinical success achievable despite loculations
  • Crohn's abscesses — lower threshold for PCD; bridge to reduce postoperative sepsis; enables primary anastomosis over stoma
  • Diverticular abscesses — preoperative PCD can reduce postoperative sepsis
  • Infected cystic tumors unresponsive to antibiotics — sepsis palliation, pain management
  • Hepatic abscesses (pyogenic; post-ablation or radioembolization)
  • Splenic abscesses (simple, uniloculated)

When NOT First-Line / Avoid PCD

  • Grossly perforated viscus or free peritonitis — peritonitis mortality up to 60%; PCD adds risk without benefit
  • Acute hematoma without liquefaction — not yet drainable
  • Abscess without mature wall
  • Interloop abscesses — lack safe access; treat with antibiotics ± needle aspiration or open surgery
  • Suspected malignant cystic mass — seeding risk; tissue diagnosis first

When Antibiotics ± Needle Aspiration Are Sufficient

  • Abscesses <3–4 cm — >50–60% resolve with antibiotics alone
  • Amebic abscesses — amebicidal agents ± aspiration for refractory/large collections
  • Tubo-ovarian abscesses <5 cm — 2/3 resolve with antibiotics; reserve PCD for complex or ruptured cases
  • Echinococcal cysts — specialized PAIR technique with scolicial agent required

Pre-Procedure Checklist

▾
Review imaging. CT preferred for planning — map collection location, size, loculations, relationship to adjacent organs (bowel, vessels, bladder), and safest access route. Identify any fistulae.
Labs. Platelets ≥50,000/µL; INR <1.5; Hgb ≥9.0 g/dL (especially high-risk cases).
Anticoagulation. Aspirin need not be held. Thienopyridines (clopidogrel) — hold 5 days. LMWH — hold 2–4 half-lives. Warfarin — hold 5 days. Urgent cases may proceed with blood products.
Antibiotics. Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics at least 1 hour before procedure. Typical regimens: 2nd/3rd-gen cephalosporin or ampicillin-sulbactam + aminoglycoside. Adjust per known sensitivities.
Ancillary prep for deep pelvic collections. Foley catheter for bladder decompression. Overnight oral or intraprocedural rectal contrast to opacify bowel and distinguish it from collection.
Sedation planning. IV access for medications; moderate sedation (midazolam + fentanyl preferred); MAC anesthesia for transrectal/transvaginal approaches; general anesthesia for pediatric patients.
Consent. Bleeding, infection/bacteremia/sepsis, injury to adjacent organs (bowel, vessels, bladder), drain dislodgement, fistula formation, incomplete drainage, need for repeat procedure or surgery.

Relevant Anatomy

▾

Access Approach Principles

  • Safest, most direct, shortest percutaneous route to collection
  • Avoid intervening organs and vital structures (arteries, veins, nerves, bowel)
  • Avoid contamination of sterile spaces — no transcolonic approach; exceptions: transgastric for pancreatic collections, transrectal/transvaginal for deep pelvic collections
  • Place catheter in most dependent portion of cavity — gravity drainage is the primary mechanism
  • Use angled approach to facilitate wire coiling and catheter advancement

Subphrenic & Hepatic

  • Subphrenic: Angle needle cranially from anterior subcostal (<7th rib) or lateral (<10th rib). Fluoroscopic evaluation during all respiratory phases to avoid pleural transgression — risk of empyema if pleural space traversed.
  • Hepatic: Subcostal approach preferred. Include normal liver parenchyma in tract. Avoid gallbladder and major bile ducts. Intercostal approach for dome collections — tilt gantry to avoid pleura.

Peripancreatic & Splenic

  • Peripancreatic: CT required. Inferolateral approach posterior to stomach. Avoid transgressing stomach/bowel. Pancreatic duct fistula expected in many cases.
  • Splenic: Lateral or posterolateral approach. Include splenic parenchyma in tract. Careful positioning — high risk of pneumothorax.

Deep Pelvic Collections

  • Foley catheter + rectal contrast before procedure — decompresses bladder, distinguishes bowel from collection
  • Transgluteal: Through greater sciatic foramen. Avoid sciatic nerve (posterolateral to neurovascular bundle).
  • Transvaginal: Non-menstruating women with posterior pelvic collection.
  • Transperineal: Alternative for presacral/perirectal collections.

Danger Structures by Location

  • Subphrenic: pleural space, lung base
  • Hepatic: hepatic vessels, bile ducts, gallbladder
  • Pelvic: sciatic nerve (transgluteal), iliac vessels, bladder, rectum
  • Peripancreatic: pancreatic duct, splenic vein, SMV

How I Do It

Default RadCall approach + community cards

▾
RadCall Standard Default

Supplies

CT or ultrasound guidance 21–22g micropuncture needle 0.035"–0.038" guidewire (Amplatz/Lunderquist) Coaxial dilator system (Cope/Neff/AccuStick) 8–14 Fr pigtail locking drainage catheter Trocar drainage kit (for large superficial collections) Sterile prep/drape 1% lidocaine Drainage bag Culture tubes (aerobic, anaerobic, AFB, fungal) 60 mL syringe Suture for catheter security

Seldinger vs. Trocar

Seldinger (preferred for most)

  • 21–22g initial needle → coaxial wire → serial dilation → drainage catheter
  • Preferred for small, deep, high-risk collections
  • More controlled; less pain; allows skinny-needle access confirmation before committing large catheter

Trocar

  • Combined catheter/stiffening cannula/sharp stylet advanced directly after small-gauge confirmation
  • Preferred for large, superficial, simple collections
  • Faster; avoids serial dilation leakage; but more painful and direct large-bore injury if misplaced
  • Do not use for deep pelvic or complex cases

Steps (Seldinger Technique)

1

Planning scan

CT preferred for deep/complex collections; US real-time guidance for superficial collections. Confirm collection size, location, access window, adjacent structures. Mark access site.
2

Prep, drape, and anesthesia

Sterile prep and drape. Infiltrate 1% lidocaine from skin to collection wall along intended tract. Do not puncture collection wall until access needle is ready.
3

Access needle and fluid confirmation

Advance 21g access needle to collection under CT/US guidance. Aspirate 5 mL — confirm appearance (pus, bile, blood, serous) and send for culture. If too viscous to aspirate: wire test — advance wire into cavity; wire assuming cavity shape confirms correct position.
4

Wire placement

Advance 0.038" Amplatz or Lunderquist wire into cavity with generous loop. Remove needle. Skin incision at entry site.
5

Serial dilation and catheter placement

Serially dilate over wire (2–3 Fr steps) to catheter size. Advance drainage catheter into most dependent portion of collection. Release pigtail lock to secure catheter.
6

Confirm position and initial aspiration

Optional: inject 1–2 mL dilute contrast to confirm catheter is within collection, exclude fistulae, and assess collection size. Connect to drainage bag. Aspirate as much fluid as possible initially. Send fluid for culture, Gram stain, cell count, pH, amylase as appropriate.
7

Secure catheter

Suture catheter to skin with loop suture and additional retention suture. Apply sterile dressing. Document drain output and character at time of placement.

Troubleshooting

▾
Problem

Can't aspirate despite correct position (viscous pus)

Likely cause: Collection too thick or inspissated to aspirate through small access needle.

Next step: Perform wire test — advance 0.038" wire into cavity; wire taking the shape of the cavity confirms correct position. Use biliary dilator with multiple side holes to initiate drainage. Consider upsizing catheter.

Problem

Collection not fully draining (loculated)

Likely cause: Loculations compartmentalize the collection preventing complete drainage through a single catheter position.

Next step: Gentle irrigation with normal saline (10–20 mL flush). Inject intracavitary fibrinolytics (tPA 4 mg in 30 mL saline dwell × 1h daily × 3–5 days) for loculated collections not responding to catheter alone. Consider catheter repositioning or additional catheter for multi-locular disease.

Problem

Drain output suddenly stops

Likely cause: Catheter occlusion by debris, kinking, or positional change.

Next step: Flush catheter with 10 mL saline. If still no output — CT to confirm catheter position and collection status. May need catheter repositioning, upsizing, or additional catheter placement.

Problem

Drain pulling out / dislodgement

Likely cause: Inadequate fixation, patient movement, or tube entanglement.

Next step: Do not force reinsertion blindly. Obtain CT to confirm whether collection is still present. If needed — IR re-access under imaging guidance.

Problem

Enteric fistula suspected (enteric contents in drain)

Likely cause: Communication between collection and bowel — can be present at time of drainage or develop during treatment.

Next step: Confirm with contrast injection through drain. Low-output fistula: continue drainage and keep NPO. High-output or persistent fistula: surgical consultation. Set expectations early — drainage dwell time measured in weeks.

Problem

Patient deteriorates after drainage (sepsis, hemodynamic instability)

Likely cause: Bacteremia from release of bacteria into circulation — expected in 5–10% within hours of drainage.

Next step: Assess drain patency. Blood cultures. Escalate/broaden antibiotics. IV fluid resuscitation. Ensure drain is patent and draining. Supportive care — usually transient.

Complications

▾

Immediate

  • Hemorrhage (1–4%) — most common in highly vascular locations (hepatic, splenic); observation for minor; angiography and embolization for active arterial bleed
  • Bacteremia/Sepsis (5–10%) — release of bacteria into circulation within hours of drainage; prevention: broad-spectrum antibiotics pre-procedure; management: supportive care + antibiotics; usually transient
  • Bowel injury (<1% with CT/US guidance) — from traversing adjacent bowel; management depends on extent
  • Pleural transgression — subphrenic collections; empyema or pneumothorax; prevention: careful respiratory phase planning; management: pleural tube if large

Delayed

  • Fistula formation (10–20% with enteric-adjacent collections) — most resolve with prolonged drainage + bowel rest; surgical repair for persistent/high-output fistulae
  • Drain dislodgement — common; prevention: secure suture lock; management: reimaging and IR re-access
  • Incomplete drainage / treatment failure (15–25%) — risk factors: multiloculation, enteric fistula, immunosuppression; management: fibrinolytics, additional catheters, or open surgery
  • Skin/tract infection — rare with sterile technique; local wound care; systemic antibiotics if cellulitis

Post-Procedure Care

▾

Monitoring and Response Assessment

  • Document drain output q8h — volume and character (purulent, serous, bloody, bilious, enteric)
  • Flush drain BID with 10 mL NS to maintain patency (nursing orders)
  • Serial clinical assessment: fever curve, WBC trend, pain
  • Send fluid cultures at placement and at 48h if inadequate clinical response
  • CRP/WBC at 48–72h as objective response marker

Catheter Removal Criteria

  • Remove when: Output <10 mL/24h × 2 consecutive days plus defervescence plus WBC normalizing plus CT confirms resolved collection or residual <2 cm plus no fistula present
  • Upsize when: Output <30 mL/24h despite clinical evidence of remaining collection — upgrade to next 2 Fr size
  • Do NOT remove catheter solely because output is low without imaging confirmation — residual cavity may persist despite little drainage
  • Discharge with drain in place: Patient/caregiver education; home nursing setup; follow-up imaging at 2 weeks

Critical Pearls

▾
❦
CT for planning, US for real-time guidance: CT is preferred for planning most abdominal/pelvic abscess drainage — superior visualization of collection anatomy, access windows, and adjacent structures. Use US for real-time guidance of superficial or large collections.
❦
Antibiotics at least 1 hour pre-procedure: Bacteremia is expected in 5–10% within hours of drainage from sudden bacterial release. Broad-spectrum IV coverage before the procedure reduces but does not eliminate this risk.
❦
Seldinger for deep/high-risk; trocar for large/superficial: Do not use trocar technique for deep pelvic or complex cases — misplacement of a large-bore catheter in a confined space carries serious risk. Seldinger allows skinny-needle confirmation before committing.
❦
Wire test for viscous collections: If collection is too thick to aspirate through the access needle, advance the 0.038" wire. If the wire assumes the shape of the cavity, you are correctly positioned. Do not abandon the access — proceed with dilation.
❦
Most dependent placement: Place the catheter tip in the most dependent portion of the cavity. Gravity is the primary drainage mechanism — an anteriorly positioned catheter in a posterior collection will fail.
❦
Inject 1–2 mL contrast on initial placement: Confirms intra-cavity position, reveals unsuspected fistulae, and sizes the collection. This small confirmatory step significantly guides subsequent management.
❦
tPA fibrinolytics for loculated collections: Intracavitary tPA (4 mg in 30 mL saline, dwell × 1h daily × 3–5 days) for collections not responding after 48h significantly improves drainage and avoids surgery in many cases.
❦
Low daily output does not mean resolved: Always obtain imaging before removing a drain. Residual cavity can persist despite little external drainage. Premature removal leads to recurrence and repeat procedure.
❦
Enteric fistula extends dwell time significantly: Set expectations with the clinical team early. Drain dwell times for enteric fistulae are often weeks, not days. Up to 40% will ultimately require surgical management.
❦
Foley + rectal contrast before pelvic drainage: Bladder decompression and bowel opacification are mandatory before accessing deep pelvic collections. The investment in this preparation prevents organ injury.

Drain Management

▾

Daily Assessment Protocol

  • Document exact drain output (mL/24h) and character — purulent, serous, bloody, bilious, or enteric
  • Flush drain with 10 mL NS BID unless contraindicated; document flush return vs. net output
  • Assess drainage bag for proper seal; check skin entry site for leak, skin erosion, or local infection
Output (mL/24h)InterpretationAction
>50 mL/dayCollection actively drainingContinue; upsize catheter if output is thick or declining despite residual collection
10–50 mL/dayDecreasing responseObtain imaging at ~48–72h to confirm progress; consider upsizing if collection persists
<10 mL/day × 2 daysPotential resolutionImage first. Remove catheter only if CT confirms resolved collection or residual <2 cm with clinical improvement
Sudden increaseNew fistula or cavity expansionAssess character: bloody → rule out vessel erosion; feculent → bowel fistula; bilious → biliary fistula. Contrast injection through drain to confirm.

Catheter Exchange / Upsizing

  • Perform over wire via fluoroscopy
  • Do not pull old catheter until new catheter is in place over the wire
  • Upsize by 2 Fr steps
  • Inject contrast after exchange to confirm position and re-assess collection

Intracavitary Fibrinolytics (tPA)

  • Indication: Loculated collection with inadequate drainage after 48h
  • Regimen: tPA 4 mg in 30 mL saline; instill through catheter; clamp × 1h; then release to gravity drainage
  • Duration: Daily × 3–5 days
  • Significantly improves drainage in multi-loculated disease and reduces need for surgery
