Subcutaneous Port Placement

Indications / Contraindications

▾

Indications

  • Long-term intermittent chemotherapy — most common indication
  • Long-term IV antibiotics or other medications requiring central access
  • Frequent long-term blood transfusions or blood draws
  • Best suited for patients who need central access intermittently over months–years with minimal maintenance

Port Type — Discuss with Referring Team

  • Single lumen: Standard; most chemotherapy patients
  • Double lumen: Simultaneous incompatible infusions (lymphoma, TPN + chemo)
  • Power-injectable port: Preferred whenever possible — future contrast-enhanced CT nearly universal in oncology; standard ports cannot safely be used for power injection
  • Size selection: Larger port for larger patients (easier palpation/access); smallest available for thin patients (reduces erosion risk)
  • Chest port preferred over arm port — arm ports carry higher infection and subclavian thrombosis risk

Absolute Contraindications

  • INR >1.5 or platelets <75,000
  • Systemic infection (clinical or laboratory evidence)
  • Bevacizumab (Avastin) within 14 days of port placement — increased wound dehiscence risk

Relative Contraindications

  • Prior radiation field involving access or pocket site — consider contralateral side
  • Known central venous occlusion
  • Implanted cardiac device on ipsilateral side

Pre-Procedure Checklist

▾
Device selection. Communicate with referring physician to confirm appropriate device: single vs. double lumen, power-injectable, and size. Document in procedure note.
Review cross-sectional imaging. Large apical lung mass → contralateral side. Assess for prior radiation field at access and pocket sites. Identify any known central venous occlusion.
Labs (SIR Category 3 — high risk). INR <1.5 (stricter than for simple central line); platelets >75,000. Correct coagulopathy before proceeding.
Anticoagulation hold (SIR Cat 3). Warfarin: hold 5 days. LMWH: hold 24h. DOACs: hold 24–48h. Confirm bridge therapy plan with referring team if high thrombotic risk.
Bevacizumab window. Enforce 14-day mandatory window. Wound dehiscence risk is clinically significant. Verify with oncology and document in chart.
Antibiotic prophylaxis. Cefazolin 1g IV within 60 min pre-procedure. Penicillin/cephalosporin allergy: vancomycin 1g IV pre-procedure; ceftizoxime 1g immediately before as an alternative.
Sedation plan. Optional conscious sedation (midazolam + fentanyl). Most patients tolerate procedure with local anesthetic alone. Confirm sedation team availability if needed.
Pre-procedure US survey. Thorough sonographic survey of neck and upper chest vessels before prepping. Confirm target vein patency. Identify anatomy and carotid position. Mark IJV location on skin.
Sterile technique. Full surgical scrub; sterile gown, gloves, cap, mask. All personnel wear masks. Double chlorhexidine scrub of neck and upper chest. Large sterile drape.
Device preparation. Port soak in neomycin antibiotic solution. Flush all needles, wires, and sheaths with saline before starting.
Consent. Pocket hematoma, wound dehiscence, infection, arterial puncture, pneumothorax, air embolism, catheter malposition, port leak, catheter pinch-off, thrombosis, port erosion through skin.

Relevant Anatomy

▾

Venous Access

  • Right or left IJV strongly preferred — subclavian carries 5–10% symptomatic thrombosis vs. <1% for IJV; also avoids pinch-off syndrome
  • Target puncture site: ~1 cm above clavicle, low in the neck; higher puncture → catheter kinking; lower puncture harder and unnecessary
  • Keep puncture site close to clavicle to ensure catheter lies in a gentle curve to the pocket

Pocket Anatomy

  • Location: Infraclavicular space, midway between sternal notch and humeral head; approximately 8–12 cm from puncture site
  • Depth: Subcutaneous space, ~1 cm deep — not sub-pectoral for standard ports (too deep impairs palpation)
  • Size: Just large enough for port — tight fit reduces port migration
  • Stay lateral to midclavicular line to avoid pneumothorax during pocket creation

Tunnel Anatomy

  • Catheter travels from pocket to venous puncture site in subcutaneous tunnel
  • Tunnel from pocket toward puncture site; catheter pulled through in same direction
  • Catheter cut to appropriate length to ensure tip at SVC-RA junction
  • External measurement: pocket → jugular notch → SVC-RA junction; subtract sheath length

How I Do It

Default RadCall approach + community cards

▾
RadCall Standard Default

Supplies

Sterile US probe cover 21G micropuncture kit Peel-away sheath (catheter-matched) 0.035" Amplatz superstiff wire Port + catheter (selected device) Tunneling device 1% lidocaine WITH epinephrine #11 and #15 scalpels Bovie electrocautery Kelly hemostat Weitlaner retractor 0-gauge nonabsorbable suture (port anchor) 2-0 Vicryl (deep closure) 4-0 Monocryl or staples (skin) 60 cc neomycin solution (3 × 20 cc) Non-coring Huber needle (22G) Heparinized saline 300–500 units/mL Sterile occlusive dressing

Steps

1

Pre-procedure US survey

Thorough sonographic survey of neck and upper chest vessels before prepping. Mark IJV position. Confirm patency and identify carotid. Survey both sides if needed (prior radiation, IVC filter).
2

Positioning and prep

Patient supine; head turned away from access side; slight Trendelenburg. Double chlorhexidine scrub of neck and upper chest bilaterally. Large sterile drape.
3

Mark sites and measure

Mark pocket site (infraclavicular, midway between sternal notch and humeral head) and puncture site (~1 cm above clavicle). Estimate catheter length using external measurement: from pocket to jugular notch to SVC-RA junction.
4

Pocket creation

Infiltrate pocket site generously with 1% lidocaine + epinephrine: skin, subcutaneous tissue, and pocket margins. Transverse incision slightly wider than port. Use Bovie throughout for hemostasis. Blunt digital dissection to pocket size. Trial port in pocket — skin edges should approximate without tension. Irrigate with 60 cc neomycin solution (three 20 cc syringes).
5

IJV access

Sterile US probe; longitudinal view of IJV. Advance 21G micropuncture needle into center of vein under real-time visualization. Advance 0.018" microwire. Remove needle. Advance 5Fr micropuncture sheath over wire. Advance wire under fluoroscopy to right atrium — ideally to IVC.
6

Catheter length measurement

Place clamp at skin/sheath hub junction; subtract sheath length from external measurement. Alternatively, mark skin at pocket incision with sterile pen. Blunt dissection of soft tissue at puncture site (reduces kinking).
7

Exchange for peel-away sheath

Advance 0.035" Amplatz superstiff wire through micropuncture sheath to IVC. Exchange micropuncture sheath for peel-away sheath over Amplatz wire. Tip of wire must be in IVC — not curled in RA (prevents buckling and SVC laceration risk).
8

Tunneling

Attach catheter to tunneling device. Tunnel subcutaneously from pocket toward puncture site. Pull catheter through tunnel. Remove tunneler. Cut catheter to measured length. Flush and inspect all connections.
9

Catheter insertion

Advance catheter through peel-away sheath into SVC. Hold catheter firmly with thumb/forefinger while splitting peel-away sheath — control is essential to prevent air embolism or kinking. Advance to SVC-RA junction under fluoroscopy.
10

Connect and confirm

Connect catheter to port. Flush with saline. Confirm backflow. Access with non-coring Huber needle. Confirm free flow and aspiration.
11

Anchor port

Anchor port to pocket floor with 0-gauge nonabsorbable suture × 2 through both port anchoring holes. Do not skip — prevents rotation and migration.
12

Pocket closure and lock

Irrigate pocket again. Close in layers: deep fascia with 0-Vicryl; subcutaneous with 2-0 Vicryl; skin with 4-0 Monocryl or staples. Lock port with 10 mL saline + 5 mL heparinized saline (300–500 units/mL) via Huber needle.
13

CXR

Post-placement chest radiograph to confirm catheter tip position at SVC-RA junction and to exclude pneumothorax.

Troubleshooting

▾
Problem

Wire won't advance beyond clavicle

Likely cause: Wire not in vein, or tight anatomy at thoracic inlet.

Next step: Confirm wire is in vein with US. Advance under fluoroscopy. Use a hydrophilic wire for tight anatomy. Ensure head is turned sufficiently away from access side.

Problem

Peel-away sheath won't advance

Likely cause: Amplatz wire tip not in IVC — curled in RA or stuck in SVC curve.

Next step: Confirm wire tip is in IVC under fluoroscopy before advancing sheath. Dilate incrementally. Smooth forward pressure without rotation. Never force — risk of SVC laceration.

Problem

Catheter length mismatch

Likely cause: External measurement error or patient positioning change during procedure.

Next step: Recheck measurement. Reposition patient (arm abduction, patient sitting up) may shift tip. Adjust catheter cut length before finalizing — verify tip under fluoroscopy at SVC-RA junction before closing pocket.

Problem

Pocket hematoma developing

Likely cause: Inadequate hemostasis during pocket creation; anticoagulation status.

Next step: Bovie hemostasis throughout pocket creation. Do not close over a hematoma — explore and irrigate. Extend procedure time as needed. Compression after closure. Large post-procedure hematoma: consider drainage if expanding or infected.

Problem

Post-procedure pocket infection

Likely cause: Skin flora (Staph epidermidis, Staph aureus) — early (<30 days) or late (>30 days) presentation.

Next step: Superficial cellulitis → IV antibiotics. Deep pocket involvement or catheter-related → remove port entirely. Antibiotic lock rarely salvages infected ports vs. tunneled catheters.

Problem

Catheter pinch-off seen on CXR

Likely cause: Subclavian approach — medial clavicle compresses catheter, risk of catheter fragmentation over time.

Next step: If IJV used correctly, this should not occur. On CXR: look for medial indentation of catheter between clavicle and first rib. If identified: remove/replace port before catheter fractures and embolizes.

Complications

▾

Early (<30 days)

  • Pocket hematoma — most common early complication; prevent with Bovie hemostasis; manage with compression; drain if large or infected
  • Wound dehiscence/necrosis — risk with bevacizumab; pocket too superficial or tight; wound care; may require port removal and revision
  • Infection (early) — skin flora; IV antibiotics; port removal for most port infections
  • Pneumothorax — 1–3%; subclavian approach higher risk; see CVL page for management
  • Air embolism — peel-away sheath open to air; always occlude hub; manage with left lateral decubitus + Trendelenburg
  • Arterial puncture — confirm with US and manometry before sheath placement

Late (>30 days)

  • Port flip/rotation — small ports in large patients; prevent by anchoring firmly through both anchoring holes
  • Catheter pinch-off — subclavian approach only; medial clavicle compression → fragmentation; prevent with IJV or lateral subclavian approach; monitor on CXR
  • Catheter fracture/embolism — rare; endovascular retrieval with snare
  • Port erosion through skin — port too superficial, too large for thin patient, or poor wound healing; repositioning or removal
  • Venous thrombosis — subclavian 5–10%; IJV <1%; anticoagulation; port removal if symptomatic
  • Late infection — more varied organisms; port removal usually required

Post-Procedure Care

▾

Immediate

  • CXR post-placement: Tip position at SVC-RA junction; exclude pneumothorax
  • Access port with 22G non-coring Huber needle; confirm aspiration and free flow
  • Lock with 10 mL normal saline + 5 mL heparinized saline (300–500 units/mL)
  • Sterile dressing over incision and Huber needle; change at 7 days

Wound and Follow-up

  • Sutures/staples removed at 10–14 days
  • First oncology use: 7–14 days post-placement — allow wound healing before first access by oncology team
  • Patient education: Non-coring Huber needle only for port access; how to care for incision; signs of infection (redness, swelling, fever, wound opening); when to call
  • Resume anticoagulation per referring team — typically 24h post-procedure
8

Critical Pearls

▾
✦
Confirm bevacizumab timing BEFORE placement — 14-day mandatory window. Wound dehiscence risk is real and clinically significant. Document this in the procedure note.
✦
Power-injectable port is almost always preferred — future contrast-enhanced CT is nearly universal in oncology patients. Standard ports cannot safely be used for power injection. Select power-injectable by default unless there is a specific contraindication.
✦
Bovie throughout pocket creation — maintain a dry pocket; do not close over a hematoma. Pocket hematoma is the most common early complication and is largely preventable.
✦
Neomycin pocket irrigation significantly reduces early infection rates — irrigate with 60 cc neomycin solution (three 20 cc syringes) at two stages: after initial pocket creation and again before final closure.
✦
IJV access preferred over subclavian — subclavian thrombosis 5–10% vs. <1% for IJV; also avoids "pinch-off" syndrome. There is essentially no reason to use subclavian when US-guided IJV access is available.
✦
Longitudinal US probe for IJV puncture — direct visualization of needle tip in center of vein throughout insertion. Avoid oblique or transverse-only approaches where tip visualization is uncertain.
✦
Amplatz wire tip must be in IVC before advancing peel-away sheath — wire curled in RA allows sheath to buckle and risks SVC laceration. Confirm under fluoroscopy every time.
✦
Anchor port through BOTH anchoring holes with 0-gauge nonabsorbable suture — prevents rotation and migration, especially in larger patients with looser subcutaneous tissue.
✦
Small ports for thin patients — use smallest available device; pocket depth ~1 cm; too superficial risks erosion through skin over time, especially with repeated access.
✦
Communicate with oncology before placement — confirm correct device type (single/double lumen, power-injectable) is selected and documented. A port placed without the right specifications may need to be replaced.

Port Access and Care

▾

Port Access Technique

  • Palpate port through skin to confirm position before accessing
  • Apply topical anesthetic (EMLA cream 60 min prior, or LMX) if not emergent
  • Sterile prep and gloves
  • Use ONLY non-coring Huber needle — regular needles will core the septum and permanently damage the port
  • Common gauges: 22G for blood draws; 19–20G for power injection of contrast
  • Insert perpendicular to port septum; advance through skin and septum until you feel the hard back-wall of the port reservoir
  • Confirm position: Aspirate blood; flush with 10 mL saline. If resistance — re-access; do not force
  • After each use: flush with 10 mL normal saline; lock with 5 mL heparinized saline (300–500 units/mL) per institutional protocol
  • De-access after each infusion; leave Huber in place only if daily or continuous use

Routine Maintenance

  • Routine flush (between uses): Heparin lock every 4–6 weeks — institutional variation; some centers use saline lock only
  • Thrombotic occlusion: TPA (alteplase) 2 mg/2 mL instillation; dwell ×30 min; repeat if needed. If fails → image-guided catheter exchange
  • Port access failure (can't aspirate): Check patient position (try raising arm, rotating head); fluoroscopy to confirm tip position; look for catheter pinch-off on CXR; possible fibrin sheath → TPA instillation
  • Port does not require flushing if accessed and used at every clinical visit — flush and lock only when de-accessing
